Derivative Instrument & Derivative Market Features
LOS 66.a – 66.b · OTC vs Exchange · Key Definitions
📖 Core Definition
  • Derivative: security whose value derives from an underlying asset or variable at a future date
  • Underlying: the asset/variable determining the derivative's value (stock, bond, index, rate, commodity, currency, crypto, weather, longevity)
  • Value at any point = f(underlying value)
Basic Features of a Derivative Contract
FeatureDescription
UnderlyingAsset/variable driving the contract
Forward price / contract pricePrice specified in contract
Contract sizeQuantity of the underlying
Settlement date (maturity date)Date of future transaction
Zero value at initiationNeither party pays at start
🔄 Settlement Types
  • Deliverable contract: physical exchange of underlying + cash at settlement
  • Cash-settled contract: only net gains/losses exchanged; economically equivalent to deliverable (ignoring transaction costs)
Forward Contract — Payoff Summary
PositionGain whenLoss when
Long (buyer)Spot > Forward priceSpot < Forward price
Short (seller)Spot < Forward priceSpot > Forward price
Gains of one party = Losses of other (zero-sum)
  • Long exposure: profits when underlying ↑ (forward buyer)
  • Short exposure: profits when underlying ↓ (forward seller)
  • Hedger: uses derivative to offset existing risk
  • Speculator: takes on risk via derivatives with no existing offsetting position
  • Full hedge: derivative payoff exactly offsets existing risk
  • Partial hedge: derivative reduces but doesn't eliminate risk
🏛️ Exchange-Traded Derivatives (ETD)
  • Standardized contracts — terms set by exchange
  • Traded at a centralized location (exchange)
  • Backed by Central Clearinghouse (CCH) / Central Counterparty (CCP)
  • CCH uses novation: takes opposite side of every trade
  • Requires margin deposits from both parties
  • Low counterparty credit risk
  • More liquid, more transparent, lower trading costs
  • Easier to clear and settle
  • Exchange members = dealers / market makers
  • Largest: National Securities Exchange (India), B3 (Brazil), CME Group (USA)
  • Products: futures, some options
🤝 OTC (Over-the-Counter) Derivatives
  • Custom instruments — tailored to end-user needs
  • Dealer market with no central location
  • Largely unregulated, less transparent
  • Subject to counterparty credit risk
  • More difficult to clear and settle
  • Higher transaction costs, less liquid
  • No collateral requirement (unless mandated)
  • Products: forwards, most swaps, some options
Post-2008 Central Clearing Mandate
  • Many swap trades now use Swap Execution Facility (SEF)
  • CCP replaces trade with two trades (itself as counterparty to both)
  • Reduces counterparty risk but concentrates risk at CCP
  • Still customizable and with dealers
🌐 Types of Underlyings
  • Stocks/Bonds: price uncertainty
  • Index: S&P 500, bond indices
  • Currency: FX rate risk
  • Interest Rate: higher rate = gain for rate forward buyer
  • Commodities: hard (gold, oil) & soft (cotton, coffee)
  • Credit: CDS on default events
  • Weather: farming, energy, tourism
  • Cryptocurrency: BTC etc.
  • Longevity: life insurers/annuity providers
Advantages over cash market: Lower cost · Greater leverage · Less market impact · Ease of short selling
EXAM ANSWERS: Q1→C (payoff based on asset/rate). Q2→C (ETDs have LOW default risk due to clearinghouse)

Forward Commitment & Contingent Claim Features
LOS 67.a – 67.c · Futures · Swaps · Options · CDS · Payoffs
📋 Forward Contracts
  • Buyer commits to buy, seller commits to sell
  • Specific asset, price, date
  • OTC, custom, counterparty risk
  • No daily mark-to-market (typically)
  • Long: gain if spot > forward price at settlement
  • Short: gain if spot < forward price
📈 Futures Contracts
  • Standardized, exchange-traded forward
  • More liquid, regulated, transparent
  • Backed by CCH
  • Daily MTM (mark-to-market)
  • Margin required (no loan, no interest)
  • Minimal counterparty risk
🔁 Swaps
  • Exchange series of payments on multiple settlement dates
  • Net payment at each settlement date
  • OTC, dealer market, counterparty risk
  • Equivalent to series of forward contracts
  • Zero value at initiation
⚙️ Futures Margin Details
TermMeaning
Initial marginDeposit before any trade; ~1 day max price move on full contract value
Maintenance marginMinimum balance required
Margin callIf balance falls below maintenance → must restore to initial margin (not just maintenance — unlike equities)
Mark-to-marketDaily settlement of gains/losses
Settlement priceAverage price of trades near end of session; resets contract value to zero daily
Price limits & circuit breakers: Exchange-imposed daily max price change; if hit, trading halted briefly
💱 Interest Rate Swaps
  • Fixed-for-floating swap on notional principal
  • Swap rate: fixed rate set at initiation → zero value
  • MRR (Market Reference Rate): floating rate (e.g., SOFR)
  • Fixed payer pays: Notional × Fixed rate / 4 (quarterly)
  • Floating payer pays: Notional × MRR / 4
  • Only net payment exchanged
  • Floating-rate liability → enters swap as fixed-rate payer to hedge
Fixed quarterly payment Payment = Notional × (Fixed rate / 4)
🛡️ Credit Default Swap (CDS)
  • Protection buyer: makes fixed periodic payments
  • Protection seller: pays only if credit event occurs (default, bankruptcy, involuntary restructuring)
  • Underlying = reference security
  • Fixed payments = yield premium on reference bonds
  • Buyer pays credit spread for default insurance
  • CDS is a contingent claim (payment depends on event)
  • Seller has similar risk to holding the reference bond

Derivative Benefits, Risks & Issuer/Investor Uses
LOS 68.a – 68.b · Advantages · Risks · Hedge Accounting
✅ Advantages of Derivatives
1. Risk Management
  • Change, transfer, or manage risk without cash market transactions
  • Create risk exposures not available in cash markets
  • Put option = floor on sale price (downside protection)
  • Call option = upside without downside
2. Information Discovery
  • Implied volatility: derived from option prices (unobservable)
  • Futures/forwards reveal expected future asset prices
  • Interest rate futures across maturities → expected future rates & central bank moves
3. Operational Advantages
  • Short selling: easier via forwards/futures (no borrowing asset)
  • Lower transaction costs: esp. vs physical commodities (no storage/transport)
  • Greater leverage: less cash for equivalent exposure
  • Greater liquidity: large transactions easier to execute
4. Market Efficiency
  • Low costs + leverage + ease of shorting → easier arbitrage → more efficient prices
⚠️ Risks of Derivatives
Risk TypeDescription
Implicit LeverageSmall cash requirement amplifies losses. Futures margins 3–12% → leverage 8:1 to 33:1. 4% margin: 1% futures price drop = 25% margin drop
Basis RiskUnderlying of derivative ≠ position being hedged. Returns on portfolio vs index may differ. Hedge horizon vs settlement date may differ
Liquidity RiskCash flows from derivative hedge ≠ position cash flows. E.g., farmer gets margin calls before harvest income arrives
Counterparty Credit RiskOTC contracts without CCH. Option seller: ZERO counterparty risk (received premium). Option buyer: faces credit risk if option in-the-money
Systemic RiskExcessive speculation → widespread market damage. Regulators use central clearing mandates to reduce
🏢 Derivatives Use by ISSUERS (Corporates)
  • Manage asset/liability values and earnings volatility
  • Hedge FX risk on foreign income (FX forwards)
  • Enter swap as floating-rate payer to convert fixed-rate debt to floating → lower duration → less balance sheet sensitivity to rates
  • Sell forwards on inventory commodity → offset inventory fair value changes
Hedge Accounting Types
TypePurposeExample
Cash flow hedgeStabilize future cash flowsFX forward on foreign receivable; floating→fixed swap
Fair value hedgeOffset changes in asset/liability valuesSwap to stabilize debt value; forward on inventory
Net investment hedgeReduce volatility of foreign subsidiary equity on balance sheetFX forward/future on subsidiary equity
👤 Derivatives Use by INVESTORS
  • Hedge, modify, or increase exposure to underlying risk
  • Buy silver forwards → silver exposure with low/no cash upfront
  • Enter swap as floating-payer/fixed-receiver → increase bond portfolio duration
  • Buy equity index futures → increase market exposure (cheap, fast)
  • Sell equity index futures → decrease market exposure
  • Buy puts on equity index → decrease downside, preserve upside
Issuer vs Investor:
Hedging inventory price risk = ISSUER
Modifying securities portfolio risk = INVESTOR
Stabilizing foreign subsidiary balance sheet = ISSUER

Arbitrage, Replication & Cost of Carry in Pricing Derivatives
LOS 69.a – 69.b · No-Arbitrage · Replication · Carry Costs · FX Forward
⚡ No-Arbitrage & Arbitrage
  • Arbitrage: buy asset at one price, simultaneously sell identical-payoff portfolio at higher price → risk-free profit, zero cost
  • No-arbitrage condition: two portfolios with identical future payoffs must have the same current price (Law of One Price)
  • Arbitrage opportunities exist briefly → quickly exploited until eliminated
  • Small price differences persist if arbitrage gain < transaction costs
Arbitrage Actions on Forward Price
ConditionAction
Forward price too HIGHSELL forward + BUY underlying (borrow at Rf)
Forward price too LOWBUY forward + SHORT SELL underlying (invest at Rf)
Arbitrage moves forward price toward no-arbitrage level
🔧 Replication
  • Replication: creating a cash market portfolio with the same payoffs as a derivative for all possible future values of the underlying
DerivativeReplicating Portfolio
Long forward at F₀Borrow S₀ at Rf → buy underlying (repay S₀(1+Rf)^T at T)
Short forward at F₀Short sell underlying, invest proceeds at Rf
Payoff Verification
  • Long forward payoff: S_T − F₀(T)
  • Replication payoff: S_T − S₀(1+Rf)^T
  • Both equal when F₀(T) = S₀(1+Rf)^T
📐 No-Arbitrage Forward Price Formulas
Basic (no costs/benefits) F₀(T) = S₀ × (1 + Rf)^T
Continuous compounding F₀(T) = S₀ × e^(r×T)
With storage costs (PV) F₀(T) = [S₀ + PV₀(cost)] × (1+Rf)^T
With cash benefits (PV) F₀(T) = [S₀ − PV₀(benefit)] × (1+Rf)^T
General (costs + benefits) F₀(T) = [S₀ + PV₀(cost) − PV₀(benefit)] × (1+Rf)^T
Continuous (r=risk-free, c=storage rate, b=benefit rate) F₀(T) = S₀ × e^((r+c−b)×T)
Cost of Carry = benefits of holding − costs of holding. Greater costs → ↑ forward price. Greater benefits → ↓ forward price.
💰 Cost of Carry Components
ItemEffect on F₀Type
Storage costs↑ increasesCost (physical commodities)
Insurance costs↑ increasesCost
Risk-free rate (Rf)↑ increasesOpportunity cost
Dividends↓ decreasesMonetary benefit
Coupon interest↓ decreasesMonetary benefit
Convenience yield↓ decreasesNon-monetary benefit; hard-to-short assets
Convenience yield: non-monetary benefit of holding an asset. Arises when asset is difficult to sell short — shortage may drive spot price up temporarily
🌍 FX Forward Pricing
No-Arbitrage FX Forward (effective annual rates) F₀(p/b) = Spot(p/b) × (1 + rate_price) / (1 + rate_base)
Continuous compounding version F₀ = Spot × e^(r_price − r_base) × T
  • Higher interest rate currency depreciates in the forward
  • Currency with lower rate = forward premium
  • Covered interest parity holds → no arbitrage
Example: USD/EUR, Rf_USD=2%, Rf_EUR=3%, Spot=1.10 Forward = 1.10 × (1.02/1.03) = 1.0893
EXAM ANSWERS: Q1→A (certain payoffs → discount at Rf) | Q2→C (identical payoffs → same price) | Q3→A (convenience yield = non-monetary, hard to short) | Q4→C (borrow at Rf to buy underlying) | Q5→B (Rf + storage = convenience yield → F = Spot)

Pricing & Valuation of Forward Contracts
LOS 70.a – 70.b · Forward Value · Implied Forward Rates · FRA
📊 Forward Contract Value at Different Times
TimeValue to Buyer (Long)
At initiation (t=0)V₀(T) = 0
During life (t < T)Vt(T) = St − F₀(T)×(1+Rf)^−(T−t)
At expiration (t=T)VT(T) = ST − F₀(T)
General mid-life value (with holding costs/benefits) Vt(T) = [St + PVt(costs) − PVt(benefits)] − F₀(T)×(1+Rf)^−(T−t)
Key: Price of forward is CONSTANT (= F₀(T)); VALUE fluctuates. Value to seller = negative of value to buyer.
  • Long gains when underlying price ↑ (value becomes positive)
  • Short gains when underlying price ↓
  • Exit: sell the asset short at St, invest F₀(T)×(1+Rf)^−(T−t) in discount bond
📐 Implied Forward Rates
  • Forward rate: yield for a future period (not starting today)
  • Spot rates (Zn): zero-coupon bond YTM maturing in n years
  • Implied forward rate: rate at which two strategies produce same ending value
Notation
SymbolMeaning
F1,1 (1y1y)1-year rate, 1 year from now
F2,1 (2y1y)1-year rate, 2 years from now
F3,2 (3y2y)2-year rate, 3 years from now
3m6m6-month rate, 3 months from now
General implied forward rate formula (1+Z_A+B)^(A+B) = (1+Z_A)^A × (1+F_A,B)^B Solve for F_A,B: F_A,B = [(1+Z_{A+B})^(A+B) / (1+Z_A)^A]^(1/B) − 1
Example: F2,1 with Z2=2%, Z3=3% (1.03)³ / (1.02)² − 1 = 109.27/104.04 − 1 = 5.03%
📋 Forward Rate Agreements (FRA)
  • FRA: fixed-rate payer (long) pays forward rate; floating-rate payer pays future MRR on notional principal
  • Only net amount exchanged at settlement
  • Settlement at beginning of forward period (PV of end-of-period difference)
  • FRA = single-period swap
  • Used primarily by financial institutions to manage interest-sensitive assets/liabilities
  • FRAs are building blocks of multi-period interest rate swaps
FRA Settlement Payment to Fixed-Rate Payer Payment = Notional × [(MRR_realized − MRR_forward)/m] × [1/(1 + MRR_realized/m)] where m = periods per year (e.g., 2 for 6-month)
No-arbitrage FRA rate (e.g., F_3m6m) [1 + Z_9m × (9/12)] = [1 + Z_3m × (3/12)] × [1 + F_3m6m × (6/12)]
Fixed-rate payer GAINS when realized MRR > forward rate (floating went up)
EXAM ANSWERS: Q1→A (underlying ↑ → gain for buyer) | Q2→B (2y3y = begins 2yr from now, lasts 3yr → ends 5yr from now) | Q3→C (F2,2 needs 4yr data, only have 3yr)

LOS 71.a – 71.b · Futures vs Forward Price · Convexity Bias · Interest Rate Futures
🔄 Forward vs Futures: Price & Value
ForwardFutures
PriceConstant over life (no MTM payments)Changes daily with settlement price
ValueFluctuates with underlying changesReset to zero daily after MTM
SettlementAt expiration (one payment)Daily (mark-to-market)
Counterparty riskSignificant (OTC)Minimal (CCH)

MTM: Each day, futures price resets to new settlement price. Value returns to zero after each settlement.
📈 When Do Forward ≠ Futures Prices?
  • If interest rates are constant or uncorrelated with futures prices: Forward price = Futures price
Correlation (rates vs futures)Futures vs ForwardWhy
Positive (+)Futures MORE attractiveDaily gains come when rates high → earn more interest on excess margin
Negative (−)Futures LESS attractiveDaily gains come when rates low → earn less on excess margin
In practice, differences are small due to short maturities and near Rf funding costs
📉 Interest Rate Futures Pricing
Futures Price Quotation Futures price = 100 − (100 × MRR_A, B−A) Example: Futures = 97 → MRR_6m,6m = 3%
Basis Point Value (BPV) BPV = Notional × Period × 0.0001 Example: €1M × (1/2) × 0.0001 = €50 per basis point
  • Interest rate futures = exchange-traded equivalent of FRAs
  • Subject to daily mark-to-market like all futures
  • Quoted on price basis (not rate), so higher price = lower rate
  • BPV: dollar change in contract value per 1bp (0.01%) rate change
🌊 Convexity Bias
  • Convexity bias: forwards and futures may have significantly different prices for longer-term interest rate contracts
  • Forward payoffs exhibit convexity (like bonds)
  • Rate ↑ by 1bp: forward value falls by less than it rises for a 1bp rate ↓
  • Futures: symmetric $50/bp in both directions (linear)
  • Forward: asymmetric payoffs (larger gain when rates fall vs loss when rates rise)
  • Convexity bias increases with longer maturity
  • Futures prices are lower than equivalent forward prices (convexity disadvantage)
EXAM ANSWERS: Q1→B (future value of current spot price) | Q2→B (positive correlation → futures more attractive)

Pricing & Valuation of Interest Rate & Other Swaps
LOS 72.a – 72.b · Swap as FRA Series · Par Swap Rate · Swap Value
🔁 Swap as Series of FRAs
  • Interest rate swap = economically equivalent to series of FRAs
  • Each payment in swap ↔ one FRA with contract rate = swap fixed rate
  • First payment is known at initiation (based on current MRR)
  • Remaining payments = unknown floating rates → equivalent to FRAs
Key Difference from FRAs
  • In swap: ALL forward contract rates equal the same fixed rate (swap rate)
  • Individual FRAs in the swap are NOT zero-value at initiation
  • Some FRAs have positive value, some negative value
  • The sum of all FRA values = 0 at initiation
Replication of Fixed-Rate Payer Swap
  • Borrow at fixed rate F + lend proceeds at floating MRR → same cash flows as swap
  • Fixed-rate payer swap ≈ issue fixed-rate bond + buy floating-rate note
  • Fixed-rate receiver ≈ issue floating-rate note + buy fixed-rate bond
💲 Par Swap Rate & Swap Value
  • Price of swap = fixed rate specified in contract (par swap rate) — CONSTANT
  • Value of swap = depends on how expected future floating rates change — VARIABLE
  • At initiation: PV(fixed payments) = PV(expected floating payments) → value = 0
Par Swap Rate Equation (quarterly, 4 periods) MRR₁/(1+S₁) + MRR₂/(1+S₂)² + MRR₃/(1+S₃)³ + MRR₄/(1+S₄)⁴ = F/(1+S₁) + F/(1+S₂)² + F/(1+S₃)³ + F/(1+S₄)⁴ Solve for F (par swap rate)
Mid-Life Swap Value
  • Expected future rates ↑ → value to fixed-rate payer ↑
  • Expected future rates ↓ → value to fixed-rate payer ↓
  • Value = PV(expected future floating payments) − PV(future fixed payments)
📋 Swap Position Summary
PositionCash Market EquivalentSimilar To
Fixed-rate payer (floating receiver)Issue fixed-rate bond + buy floating-rate noteLong interest rate risk (benefits if rates ↑)
Floating-rate payer (fixed receiver)Issue floating-rate note + buy fixed-rate bondShort interest rate risk (benefits if rates ↓)
Uses
  • Company with floating-rate debt → enters swap as fixed-rate payer → convert to fixed liability (hedge)
  • Investor wanting longer duration → enter as floating-payer/fixed-receiver
  • Key: swap fixed rate (par swap rate) derived from spot rate curve
  • Notional principal never exchanged in interest rate swaps
EXAM ANSWERS: Q1→B (floating-rate receiver = issue floating + buy fixed) | Q2→A (price = fixed rate specified in contract)

📏 Option Bounds (European)
OptionLower Bound (Min)Upper Bound (Max)
European CallMax[0, S_t − X(1+Rf)^−(T−t)]S_t
European PutMax[0, X(1+Rf)^−(T−t) − S_t]X(1+Rf)^−(T−t)
Key Points
  • Call upper bound = S (no one pays more than asset market value for the right to buy it)
  • Put upper bound = PV(X) not X (European can't be exercised early)
  • Both lower bounds ≥ 0 (options can't have negative value)
  • Lower bound for call = S − PV(X) because you could replicate by buying stock and borrowing PV(X)
Portfolio proof for call lower bound Long call + Bond[PV(X)] + Short stock ≥ 0 → c₀ ≥ S₀ − X(1+Rf)^−T

Option Replication Using Put–Call Parity
LOS 74.a – 74.b · PCP · Put-Call-Forward Parity · Synthetic Securities · Corporate Finance
⚖️ Put-Call Parity (PCP)
Building Blocks
PortfolioComponentsPayoff at T
Fiduciary CallLong call (X) + Long bond [pays X at T]S when S≥X; X when S<X
Protective PutLong stock + Long put (X)S when S≥X; X when S<X
Same payoffs → same price (no-arbitrage)
Put-Call Parity Equation c + X(1+Rf)^−T = S + p (Call + PV(X) = Stock + Put)
Requirements
  • European options only
  • Same exercise price (X) for put and call
  • Same expiration date (T)
  • Same underlying asset
🔧 Synthetic Positions (from PCP)
Rearrangements of: c + X(1+Rf)^−T = S + p S = c − p + X(1+Rf)^−T [Synthetic stock] p = c − S + X(1+Rf)^−T [Synthetic put] c = S + p − X(1+Rf)^−T [Synthetic call] X(1+Rf)^−T = S + p − c [Synthetic bond]
Reading the Signs: + = Long, − = Short
SyntheticLongShort
StockCall + BondPut
PutCall + BondStock
CallStock + PutBond
BondStock + PutCall
PCP Example: Estimate call price S=$52, p=$1.50, X=$50, Rf=5%, T=0.25yr c = p + S − X(1+Rf)^−T c = 1.50 + 52 − 50/1.05^0.25 = $4.11
🔮 Put-Call-Forward Parity
  • Replace stock S₀ with synthetic asset: long forward + bond paying F₀(T)
  • Synthetic asset cost = PV of forward price = F₀(T)(1+Rf)^−T
Put-Call-Forward Parity F₀(T)(1+Rf)^−T + p₀ = c₀ + X(1+Rf)^−T Rearranged: p₀ − c₀ = [X − F₀(T)] × (1+Rf)^−T
  • Does NOT include the underlying asset directly
  • Uses bond paying F₀(T) + forward + put + call
  • If F₀(T) = X → put price = call price (put-call parity at-the-money forward)

Valuing a Derivative Using a One-Period Binomial Model
LOS 75.a – 75.b · Hedge Ratio · No-Arbitrage Pricing · Risk-Neutral Probabilities
🌲 One-Period Binomial Model Setup
Required Inputs
  • S₀: current underlying value
  • X: exercise price of option
  • Rᵘ: up-move return (= S^u / S₀)
  • Rᵈ: down-move return (= S^d / S₀)
  • Rf: risk-free rate over the period
  • NOT required: actual probability of up/down move
Binomial Tree Structure
S₀ → S^u = S₀ × Rᵘ (up-move) S₀ → S^d = S₀ × Rᵈ (down-move) Call payoffs: c^u = Max(0, S^u − X) c^d = Max(0, S^d − X) Put payoffs: p^u = Max(0, X − S^u) p^d = Max(0, X − S^d)
⚖️ Hedge Ratio Method (No-Arbitrage)
Hedge ratio h (number of shares per option written/bought) h = (c^u − c^d) / (S^u − S^d) [for call: short call + long h shares] h = (p^u − p^d) / (S^u − S^d) [for put: long put + long h shares]
Steps for Call (short call, long h shares)
1. V^u = h×S^u − c^u (portfolio value up-state) 2. V^d = h×S^d − c^d (portfolio value down-state) 3. Set V^u = V^d → solve for h 4. V₀ = V^u / (1 + Rf) [discount certain payoff] 5. V₀ = h×S₀ − c₀ → solve: c₀ = h×S₀ − V₀
Steps for Put (long put, long h shares)
1. V^u = h×S^u + p^u 2. V^d = h×S^d + p^d 3. Set V^u = V^d → solve for h 4. V₀ = V^u / (1 + Rf) 5. V₀ = h×S₀ + p₀ → solve: p₀ = V₀ − h×S₀
🎲 Risk-Neutral Probability Method
Risk-neutral probability of UP move (π_U) π_U = (1 + Rf − Rᵈ) / (Rᵘ − Rᵈ) π_D = 1 − π_U [prob of down-move]
Steps
1. Calculate π_U and π_D 2. Calculate option payoffs: c^u, c^d (or p^u, p^d) 3. E[payoff] = π_U × c^u + π_D × c^d 4. Option value = E[payoff] / (1 + Rf)
IMPORTANT: π_U and π_D are NOT actual probabilities — they are risk-neutral pseudo-probabilities derived from no-arbitrage conditions. We CAN discount at Rf because based on arbitrage.
📝 Worked Example
Setup: S₀=$30, X=$30, Rᵘ=1.15, Rf=7%
Rᵈ = 1/1.15 = 0.87 S^u = 30 × 1.15 = $34.50 S^d = 30 × 0.87 = $26.10 π_U = (1.07 − 0.87)/(1.15 − 0.87) = 0.20/0.28 = 0.715 π_D = 1 − 0.715 = 0.285
Call (X=$30)
c^u = Max(0, 34.50−30) = $4.50 c^d = Max(0, 26.10−30) = $0 E[call] = 0.715×4.50 + 0.285×0 = $3.22 c₀ = 3.22 / 1.07 = $3.01
Put (X=$30)
p^u = Max(0, 30−34.50) = $0 p^d = Max(0, 30−26.10) = $3.90 E[put] = 0.715×0 + 0.285×3.90 = $1.11 p₀ = 1.11 / 1.07 = $1.04
🔑 Key Concepts Summary
  • Actual probabilities NOT needed for no-arbitrage pricing
  • Hedge ratio = number of shares to hold per option written/bought
  • Portfolio with hedge ratio has certain payoff regardless of move
  • Certain payoff → discount at Rf (no risk premium)
  • Both methods (hedge ratio and risk-neutral prob) give same answer
  • Risk-neutral probabilities come from no-arbitrage relationship
  • In practice: many short periods → many outcomes (binomial tree)
  • One-period model illustrates core concept
  • Discount always at Rf because model is arbitrage-based (riskless portfolio)
