R12 FIRMS AND MARKET STRUCTURES
12.1 Breakeven, Shutdown & Scale (LOS 12.a)
Core Definitions
Short run: Some factors (capital) are FIXED — firm can't change scale
Long run: ALL costs variable — can exit/enter
Breakeven point: P = ATC → TR = TC → Economic profit = 0
Quasi-fixed cost: e.g., an employee — avoidable in LR, not easily in SR
Shutdown Decision (Total Approach)
  • TR = TC → Break even
  • TC > TR > TVC → Operate SR, exit LR
  • TR < TVC → Shut down SR AND LR
  • Applies to both price-takers AND price-searchers
  • If no breakeven exists: minimize loss by operating at smallest negative (TR − TC)
Decision Rules — Operate vs. Shut Down
Condition Short Run Long Run
AR ≥ ATC ✓ Operate ✓ Stay
AR ≥ AVC & AR < ATC ✓ Operate (cover some FC) ✗ Exit
AR < AVC ✗ Shut Down ✗ Exit
Economies & Diseconomies of Scale
Economies of Scale
  • Downward LRATC segment
  • Labor specialization
  • Mass production
  • More efficient equipment
  • Lower input prices (bulk)
Minimum Efficient Scale
  • Lowest point of LRATC curve
  • Under perfect competition, all firms operate here in LR equilibrium
  • LRATC = Market price in LR
  • Zero economic profit in LR
Diseconomies of Scale
  • Upward LRATC segment
  • Bureaucratic inefficiency
  • Workforce motivation issues
  • Barriers to innovation
  • Example: US auto industry
LRATC is U-shaped. Flat portion = Constant returns to scale (relatively constant costs across plant sizes)
12.2 Characteristics of Market Structures (LOS 12.b)
5 Factors: (1) Number/size of firms  (2) Product differentiation  (3) Pricing power  (4) Barriers to entry/exit  (5) Non-price competition

Perfect Competition

  • Many firms
  • Very low barriers
  • Identical products (no branding)
  • Compete on price only
  • No pricing power
  • Horizontal demand (P = MR)
  • Example: Wheat market

Monopolistic Competition

  • Many firms
  • Low barriers
  • Differentiated products
  • Price + marketing + features
  • Some pricing power
  • Downward-sloping demand (elastic)
  • Example: Toothpaste market

Oligopoly

  • Few firms
  • High barriers
  • Good subs or differentiated
  • Price + marketing + features
  • Some to significant power
  • Interdependent decisions
  • Example: Auto, oil industries

Pure Monopoly

  • Single firm
  • Very high barriers
  • No good substitutes
  • Advertising (vs substitutes)
  • Significant pricing power
  • Steepest downward demand
  • Example: Local electric utility
Profit maximization (ALL structures): Produce where MR = MC
Perfect competition: P = MR = MC  |  All others (price-searchers): P > MR = MC
Monopoly sources of power: Copyrights/patents | Control of essential resource | Government regulation/legal protection
12.3 Monopolistic Competition — Supply/Demand (LOS 12.c)
Short Run
  • Maximize profit where MR = MC
  • Price from demand curve at that quantity
  • P* > ATC* → Positive economic profits
  • Low barriers → new firms enter in pursuit of profits
Long Run
  • New entry shifts demand curve DOWN for each firm
  • P* = ATC* → Zero economic profit
  • Still produce at MR = MC
  • Alternatively: increased marketing ↑ ATC until ATC = price
  • Advertising expenses tend to be high
LR Characteristics (vs Perfect Competition): Price > MC | ATC not at minimum (excess capacity) | Price slightly higher | LR equilibrium: MR = MC, P = ATC
Excess capacity: Firms produce below minimum ATC — inefficient scale, but product differentiation has consumer benefits
12.4 Oligopoly — Pricing Models (LOS 12.d)
Key Feature: INTERDEPENDENCE — each firm considers rivals' reactions
1. Kinked Demand Curve Model
  • Rivals match price DECREASES but NOT increases
  • Creates KINK at current price PK
  • Above PK: demand MORE elastic (flatter)
  • Below PK: demand LESS elastic
  • GAP in MR curve at kink price
  • MC through gap → PK is profit-maximizing price → price stability
2. Cournot Duopoly Model
  • Two firms, identical MC curves
  • Each sets price based on rival's PREVIOUS period price
  • Assumes competitor's price won't change
  • LR: Both sell equal quantities (market split equally)
  • Price: between monopoly and competitive
  • More producers → price → competitive equilibrium
3. Nash Equilibrium
  • Nash Equilibrium: No firm can UNILATERALLY change price to increase profit
  • Cournot model results in Nash equilibrium
  • Strategic games: best choice depends on other firms' expected actions
  • Developed by Nobel laureate John Nash
4. Stackelberg / Dominant Firm Model
  • Sequential pricing: Leader sets price FIRST
  • Follower responds to leader's price
  • LR: Leader charges higher price, earns larger profit share
  • Dominant firm (DF): large market share, lower costs
  • DF sets market price; competitive firms (CFs) take price as given
Collusion
Collusion: Joint agreement to charge a given price or restrict output. Maximizes total industry profit (= monopoly outcome). Illegal in many countries.
Cartel: OPEC is classic example. Members may "cheat" by producing more than agreed.
Collusion MORE Stable When:
  • Fewer firms
  • More similar products (less differentiated)
  • More similar cost structures
  • Purchases small and frequent
  • Retaliation for cheating is certain & severe
  • Less outside competition
LR Oligopoly Price Range
  • Upper bound: Pcollusion = monopoly price (max total profit)
  • Lower bound: Pcompetition = competitive price (zero econ profit)
  • Outcome depends on firm interdependence & market specifics
12.5 Identifying Market Structures — Concentration Measures (LOS 12.e)
N-firm Concentration Ratio
N-firm CR = Σ (market shares of largest N firms) %
  • Simple to calculate and understand
  • Does NOT directly measure pricing power or demand elasticity
  • Limitation: Insensitive to mergers within the top N group
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI)
HHI = Σ (market sharei)2
  • More sensitive to mergers — better reflects concentration changes
  • Used by regulators to assess merger approval
  • Limitation (both measures): Ignore barriers to entry
  • High share ≠ high pricing power if entry barriers are LOW
R13 UNDERSTANDING BUSINESS CYCLES
13.1 Business Cycle Phases (LOS 13.a)
📉 Trough
  • GDP growth: negative → positive
  • High unemployment; overtime & temp workers rising
  • Durables & housing spending may increase
  • Moderate/decreasing inflation
📈 Expansion
  • GDP growth increasing
  • Unemployment falls; hiring accelerates
  • Investment in equipment & housing rises
  • Inflation may increase
  • Imports increase
🏔 Peak
  • GDP growth slows to zero
  • Unemployment low but hiring slows
  • Consumer/business investment grow slower
  • Inflation rate increases
📉 Contraction
  • GDP growth negative
  • Hours fall; unemployment rises
  • Consumer spending, housing, investment fall
  • Inflation decreases (with lag)
  • Imports decrease
Business Cycle Measurement Methods
  • Classical cycle: Real GDP relative to starting value
  • Growth cycle: Real GDP vs. long-term trend/potential (% difference)
  • Growth rate cycle: Annualized % change period-to-period — preferred by economists; shows peaks/troughs earliest
Key Facts
  • Expansion defined: 2+ consecutive quarters of GDP growth
  • Recession: 2+ consecutive quarters of declining GDP
  • Cycles recur but NOT at regular intervals (1 year to >1 decade)
  • NBER (US) uses broader data: unemployment, industrial production, inflation
13.2 Credit Cycles (LOS 13.b)
Credit cycles: Cyclical fluctuations in interest rates and availability of loans
  • Expansion → loose credit, lower rates  |  Contraction → tight credit, higher rates
  • Credit cycles MAY AMPLIFY business cycles
  • Can create asset bubbles (e.g., subprime mortgages pre-2007)
  • Expansions stronger AND contractions deeper when they coincide with credit cycles
  • Credit cycles tend to be LONGER in duration than business cycles
  • Most correlated sector: Construction / real estate (depends heavily on credit availability)
13.3 Resource Use, Sectors & Indicators (LOS 13.c)
Inventory-Sales Ratio (Key Indicator)
  • Late expansion: Sales slow → unsold inventory builds → ratio rises above normal → firms cut production → triggers contraction
  • Early expansion (post-trough): Sales accelerate → inventories depleted → ratio falls below normal → firms increase output
  • Inventory counted in GDP even if unplanned — can mislead on GDP strength
Labor & Capital Adjustment
  • Firms adjust hours first (overtime/undertime), then hire/lay off
  • Hiring/firing is costly → unemployment is a lagging indicator
  • Recession: Reduce capacity by deferring maintenance & not replacing equipment (NOT selling assets)
  • Expansion: Invest in new plant/equipment only if expansion expected to persist
Consumer Spending Cyclicality
Durable Goods — MOST Cyclical
  • Appliances, furniture, automobiles
  • High value — easily postponed
  • Consumers delay during contractions
Services — Moderately Cyclical
  • Travel, lodging, restaurants (discretionary)
  • Telecom, healthcare, insurance (non-discretionary)
Non-Durable Goods — LEAST Cyclical
  • Food at home, household products
  • Relatively stable across cycle
Housing Sector Determinants
  • (1) Mortgage rates: Lower rates → ↑ buying & construction
  • (2) Income vs. home costs: High income relative to costs → ↑ activity (can fall even as incomes rise if home prices rise faster)
  • (3) Speculative activity: Rising prices → more buying → excess building → eventual collapse
  • (4) Demographics: Ages 25–40 = peak household formation; urbanization in emerging economies → ↑ construction
External Trade
  • Imports ↑ when: domestic GDP grows | domestic currency appreciates
  • Exports ↑ when: foreign GDP grows | domestic currency depreciates
  • GDP effects are more DIRECT and IMMEDIATE than currency effects (currency effects respond to persistent trends, not short-term volatility)
Economic Indicators
TypeDefinitionExamples
LeadingChange direction BEFORE business cycle peaks/troughsStock prices, building permits, new orders for durable goods, money supply (M2), consumer expectations, yield curve spread
CoincidentChange direction at SAME TIME as peaks/troughsIndustrial production, employment level, personal income, manufacturing & trade sales
LaggingChange direction AFTER peaks/troughsUnemployment rate, CPI, outstanding commercial loans, inventory-sales ratio, average prime lending rate
R14 FISCAL POLICY
14.1 Fiscal vs. Monetary Policy (LOS 14.a)
Fiscal PolicyMonetary Policy
DefinitionGovt use of spending & taxation to influence economyCentral bank control of money supply & credit
Primary GoalEconomic growth, price stability, redistributionPrice stability (inflation control)
ToolsTransfer payments, govt spending, taxesPolicy rate, reserve requirements, open market ops
Budget SurplusTax revenue > Expenditure → Contractionary—
Budget DeficitExpenditure > Tax revenue → Expansionary—
Keynesian View
  • Fiscal policy strongly affects aggregate demand
  • Especially effective below full employment
  • Fiscal stimulus can boost real output
Monetarist View
  • Fiscal stimulus only temporary
  • Monetary policy = tool for inflation over time
  • Monetary policy NOT to counter business cycles
Discretionary fiscal policy: Deliberate govt spending/tax changes  |  Automatic stabilizers: Built-in mechanisms triggered by economic state (e.g., ↓ tax revenue + ↑ unemployment benefits during recession)
14.2 Fiscal Policy Objectives (LOS 14.b)
Objective 1
Influence level of economic activity and aggregate demand
Objective 2
Redistribute wealth and income among population segments
Objective 3
Allocate resources among economic agents and sectors
National Debt — Arguments
CONCERNED About Large Deficit
  • Higher future taxes → disincentives → lower LR growth
  • Loss of market confidence → default risk or money printing → inflation
  • Crowding-out effect: Govt borrowing ↑ interest rates → ↓ private investment
NOT CONCERNED About Large Deficit
  • Debt held domestically → problem overstated
  • Debt funds productive capital → future gains repay it
  • Deficits may prompt needed tax reform
  • Ricardian equivalence: Private savings offset govt deficit → no net effect
  • Below full employment → no crowding out
Debt ratio = Aggregate Debt / GDP. Rises when real interest rate > real GDP growth rate (keeping tax rates constant)
14.3 Fiscal Policy Tools (LOS 14.c)
Spending Tools
  • Transfer payments (entitlements): Social Security, unemployment insurance — redistribute wealth; NOT counted in GDP
  • Current spending: Routine govt purchases of goods & services
  • Capital spending: Infrastructure (roads, schools, hospitals) — boosts future productivity; LONG implementation lag
Revenue Tools
  • Direct taxes: Income, corporate, wealth, estate, capital gains, Social Security — progressive taxes redistribute income
  • Indirect taxes: Sales tax, VAT, excise taxes — quickly implemented, can reduce consumption of specific goods (tobacco, alcohol)
Desirable Tax Attributes: Simplicity | Efficiency (minimal market distortion) | Fairness (horizontal equality: similar situations, similar taxes; vertical equality: richer pay more) | Sufficiency (adequate revenue)
Advantage of indirect taxes: Quick to implement social policies & raise revenue at low cost  |  Disadvantage of direct taxes & capital spending: Long time lags before impact
Fiscal Multiplier — CRITICAL FORMULAS
Fiscal Multiplier = 1 / [1 − MPC × (1 − t)]
Change in AD (spending) = ΔGovernment Spending × Fiscal Multiplier
Change in AD (from tax) = −ΔTax × MPC × Fiscal Multiplier
Balanced Budget: equal ↑ spending and ↑ taxes → NET POSITIVE AD effect (spending multiplier > tax multiplier)
To perfectly offset $X spending: tax increase = X / MPC (not $X)
Multiplier Relationships
  • Higher MPC → Higher multiplier
  • Higher tax rate → Lower multiplier
  • Spending directly enters economy (stronger effect)
  • Tax cut: reduced by savings portion (weaker effect)
  • Low-income tax cuts most effective (high MPC)
Ricardian Equivalence
  • If taxpayers save exactly enough to repay future debt from deficit → NO net effect on AD
  • Argument AGAINST worrying about deficit size
  • In practice: rarely holds perfectly
14.4 Implementation — Lags & Limitations (LOS 14.d)
1. Recognition Lag
State of economy is complex; policymakers take time to identify problem and its extent
2. Action Lag
Governments must debate, vote, and legislate — takes time to enact changes
3. Impact Lag
Time between enactment and actual economic effect; multiplier effects occur gradually
Risk: Lags can make fiscal policy counterproductive — stimulus may arrive after economy has self-recovered
Additional Limitations
  • Misreading statistics: Full employment not precisely measurable; stimulus at full capacity → inflation
  • Crowding-out effect: Govt borrowing → ↑ interest rates → ↓ private investment; reduces fiscal effectiveness
  • Supply shortages: If slowdown from supply constraints (not low demand) → stimulus → inflation, not growth
  • Limits to deficits: If debt/GDP too high → markets may refuse to fund → ↑ interest rates, worsening situation
  • Multiple targets: High unemployment + high inflation simultaneously → fiscal policy cannot address both
Expansionary fiscal policy: ↑ spending, ↓ taxes, ↑ deficit  |  Contractionary: ↓ spending, ↑ taxes, ↓ deficit
Structural (cyclically-adjusted) budget deficit: Deficit that would exist at full employment — better gauge of true fiscal stance; actual deficit includes automatic stabilizer effects
R15 MONETARY POLICY
15.1 Central Bank Roles & Objectives (LOS 15.a)
6 Key Roles of Central Banks
  • Sole currency supplier: Issues fiat money (legal tender, not gold-backed)
  • Banker to govt & banks: Provides banking services
  • Regulator/supervisor: Sets reserve requirements, oversees payments system
  • Lender of last resort: Supplies money to banks in shortfall — prevents bank runs
  • Holds gold & FX reserves: Repository of national reserves
  • Conducts monetary policy: Controls money supply and credit
Objectives
  • Primary: Control inflation / price stability
  • Target: ~2–3% in developed economies (NOT 0% — risk of deflation)
  • US Fed: price stability + max employment + moderate LT rates (no explicit target)
  • Japan: Deflation is persistent problem — no explicit target
  • Additional goals: Exchange rate stability | Full employment | Sustainable growth | Moderate LT interest rates
  • Menu costs: Cost of frequently changing prices (from high inflation)
  • Shoe leather costs: Cost of frequent bank trips to avoid holding depreciating cash
15.2 Tools & Transmission Mechanism (LOS 15.b)
Tool 1: Policy Rate
  • US: Discount rate (rate Fed charges banks)
  • ECB: Refinancing rate
  • UK: Two-week repo rate
  • Federal funds rate (US): Rate banks charge each other on overnight loans; Fed targets via OMO
  • ↓ Policy rate → encourages lending → ↓ overall rates
Tool 2: Reserve Requirements
  • ↑ Reserve req → ↓ loanable funds → ↓ money supply → ↑ rates (contractionary)
  • ↓ Reserve req → ↑ loanable funds → ↑ money supply → ↓ rates (expansionary)
  • Only effective if banks willing to lend & customers willing to borrow
Tool 3: Open Market Operations
  • CB buys securities → ↑ bank reserves → ↑ money supply → ↓ rates (expansionary)
  • CB sells securities → ↓ bank reserves → ↓ money supply → ↑ rates (contractionary)
  • US Fed's PRIMARY tool; used to hit federal funds rate target
Monetary Transmission Mechanism — 4 Channels
Policy Rate Change → (1) Other Short-Term Rates  →  (2) Asset Values  →  (3) Currency Exchange Rates  →  (4) Expectations  →  Price Level / Inflation
Contractionary Policy (↑ Policy Rate) Effects
↑ Policy Rate → ↑ ST lending rates → ↓ consumer credit & business investment → ↓ bond/equity/asset prices (wealth effect: ↑ savings, ↓ consumption) → ↓ growth expectations → ↓ expenditure → attract foreign capital → domestic currency APPRECIATES → ↓ exports → NET: ↓ Aggregate Demand → ↓ Inflation, ↓ Growth
Expansionary Policy Chain
CB buys securities → ↑ bank reserves → ↓ interbank rate → ↓ ST & LT rates → currency DEPRECIATES → ↑ business investment + consumer spending + exports → ↑ Aggregate Demand → ↑ Inflation, ↑ Employment, ↑ Real GDP
15.3 Effective Central Banks & Targeting (LOS 15.c)
Quality 1: Independence
  • Operational independence: CB independently determines policy rate (most CBs)
  • Target independence: CB defines inflation measure, sets target, determines horizon (ECB only — has BOTH)
  • Politicians have incentive to boost economy pre-election
Quality 2: Credibility
  • Follow through on stated intentions
  • Credible targets → self-fulfilling (wages/contracts set at target inflation)
  • Govt-set targets lack credibility (incentive to allow higher inflation)
Quality 3: Transparency
  • Publish inflation reports
  • Disclose economic indicators used in decisions
  • Clear communication → credibility + anticipability of policy changes
Targeting Approaches
MethodHow It WorksKey Points
Inflation TargetingSet explicit inflation target (~2% ±1%); adjust money supply to hit 2-year forward inflationMost common currently. Not 0% (deflation risk). UK, Brazil, Canada, Australia, Mexico, ECB
Interest Rate Targeting↑ money supply when rate rises above band; ↓ when belowHistorical; less common now
Exchange Rate TargetingPeg currency to another (usually USD); buy/sell domestic currency to maintainCommon in developing economies; imports other country's inflation rate; reduces monetary policy independence
Neutral Interest Rate = Real trend rate of economic growth + Inflation target
Policy rate ABOVE neutral → Contractionary  |  Policy rate BELOW neutral → Expansionary
Limitations of Monetary Policy
  • Long-term rates ≠ short-term rates: Inflation expectations can cause LT rates to move opposite to ST rates
  • Bond market vigilantes: If money growth seen as inflationary → investors sell bonds → ↑ LT yields despite easing policy
  • Liquidity trap: Demand for money is highly elastic — people hold cash instead of bonds → ↑ money supply doesn't ↓ ST rates; Japan experienced this
  • Zero lower bound: Nominal policy rates cannot go below 0% — limits ability to fight deflation
  • Banks may not lend: Even with excess reserves, banks can be unwilling to lend (post-2008 credit bubble)
  • Deflation harder to reverse than inflation
  • Quantitative Easing (QE): CB purchases LT bonds / mortgage securities to reduce LT rates and generate excess reserves when conventional tools exhausted; US (QE, QE2), UK used this post-2008
  • Developing economies: Lack liquid govt debt market | Rapid financial innovation changes money demand | May lack CB credibility | May lack independence from politics

R16 INTRODUCTION TO GEOPOLITICS
16.1 Cooperation vs. Competition (LOS 16.a)
Core Terms
  • Geopolitics: Interactions among nations; how geography affects national/international relations
  • State actors: National governments
  • Nonstate actors: Corporations, NGOs, individuals
  • Soft power: Influence without force (culture, media, norms, standards)
  • Geophysical resource endowment: Natural resources influencing national interests and trade needs
  • Institutions: Laws, organizations, customs — stable institutions facilitate cooperation
Cooperation Areas
  • Diplomatic and military matters
  • Economic interactions (trade, capital flows, tariff harmonization)
  • Cultural interactions
  • Standardization of regulations (e.g., IFRS)
  • Countries cooperate when it advances their national interests
  • Degree of cooperation varies along a spectrum and can change over time
16.2 Geopolitics & Globalization (LOS 16.b)
Globalization: Long-term trend toward worldwide integration of economic activity and cultures | Trade as % of world output: ~25% (early 1970s) → ~60% (pre-2008) → ~60% (post-2008)
Nationalism: Nation pursuing its own interests independently/in competition with others (as used in CFA curriculum)
4 Geopolitical Archetypes (2×2 Matrix)
Non-CooperationCooperation
GlobalizationHEGEMONY: Open to globalization; large enough to influence others without cooperating (e.g., dominant economies)MULTILATERALISM: Extensive international trade & cooperation with many countries; may exhibit regionalism (cooperation with nearby countries)
NationalismAUTARKY: National self-reliance; produce most goods domestically; state-controlled economy, industry, mediaBILATERALISM: Cooperation with individual countries; many bilateral relationships but avoids multilateral arrangements
Regionalism: Multilateral cooperation limited to nearby countries  |  Portfolio investment flows: Buy/sell foreign securities  |  FDI (Foreign Direct Investment): Own physical capacity abroad

16.4 Geopolitical Risk (LOS 16.d)
Geopolitical risk: Possibility of events that interrupt peaceful international relations
Event Risk
Timing KNOWN but outcome uncertain (e.g., elections, scheduled referendums)
Exogenous Risk
Unanticipated events (e.g., war outbreak, rebellion, pandemic) — high velocity
Thematic Risk
Known long-term factors (e.g., migration, cyber risks, climate) — low velocity
3 Dimensions to Assess Impact: Likelihood (probability) | Impact (magnitude on investment values) | Velocity (speed of effect on investments)
  • Black swan risk: Low-likelihood, high-impact exogenous events with substantial short-term effects
  • High velocity: Exogenous risks — immediate market impact
  • Medium velocity: Can damage specific companies/industries (cost/production disruptions)
  • Low velocity: ESG-type risks — important for LONG time horizon investors
  • Geopolitical risks have GREATER impact on investment values during recessions vs. expansions
  • Signposts: Data signals that probability of an event is changing (e.g., VIX, volatility indicators)
  • Scenario analysis: Qualitative or quantitative — used to assess portfolio impact
16.5 Tools of Geopolitics & Investment Impact (LOS 16.e, 16.f)
National Security Tools
  • Armed conflict
  • Espionage
  • Bilateral/multilateral security agreements
  • Armed conflict: destroys productive capital, causes migration
  • Can be active (currently used) or threatened
Economic Tools
  • Cooperative: Free trade areas, common markets, economic/monetary unions
  • Noncooperative: Domestic content requirements, voluntary export restraints, nationalization
Financial Tools
  • Cooperative: Allow foreign investment, free currency exchange
  • Noncooperative: Restrict FI, currency controls
  • Sanctions: Restrictions on specific actor's financial interests — used alongside security tools
R17 INTERNATIONAL TRADE
17.1 Benefits & Costs of Trade (LOS 17.a)
Comparative advantage: Lower RELATIVE (opportunity) cost of production → arises from technology differences and resource endowments
Benefits of Free Trade
  • Specialization per comparative advantage → ↑ total output & wealth
  • Economies of scale → ↓ export costs
  • Greater product variety for consumers
  • Increased competition → ↓ costs, ↑ quality
  • Reduces pricing power of domestic monopolies
  • More efficient resource allocation
  • Newer models also cite: monopolistic competition with differentiated products (autos: countries export one type, import another)
Costs of Free Trade
  • Job losses in import-competing domestic industries
  • Increased income inequality
  • Short-run disruption; workers need retraining
  • Exporting country: ↑ export demand may ↑ prices for domestic consumers (in short run)
  • Overall: gains exceed losses in LR (winners could compensate losers)
17.2 Trade Restrictions (LOS 17.b)
Rationale WITH Economic Support
  • Infant industry: Protect new industries until they reach competitive scale (learning curve)
  • National security: Protect defense-essential industries
Rationale WITHOUT Strong Economic Support
  • Protecting domestic jobs (other jobs created in export industries)
  • Protecting domestic industries (usually favors producers at consumers' expense)
  • Retaliation, tariff revenue, anti-dumping, countering foreign subsidies
  • Dumping: Exporting at below production cost
Types of Trade Restrictions

Exception: Large country quota/tariff CAN theoretically improve welfare if it reduces world price (country is large enough buyer to influence market price)
Capital Flow Restrictions
  • Outright prohibition of foreign investment in domestic country
  • Prohibition of / taxes on income earned on foreign investments by citizens
  • Prohibition of foreign investment in certain domestic industries
  • Restrictions on repatriation of earnings by foreign entities
  • Overall: ↓ economic welfare; short-run benefit for small economies during volatile capital flows may not offset LR costs

R18 CAPITAL FLOWS AND THE FX MARKET
18.1 The Foreign Exchange Market (LOS 18.a)
Exchange Rate Notation
  • 1.25 USD/EUR = "$1.25 per euro" = $1.25 is price of 1 euro
  • Price currency = numerator (USD)  |  Base currency = denominator (EUR)
  • Direct quote: From price currency investor's perspective
  • Indirect quote: From base currency investor's perspective
  • ↑ P/B rate → base currency APPRECIATES, price currency DEPRECIATES
  • Spot rate: Immediate delivery (settled T+2)
  • Forward rate: Exchange at future date (30/60/90/180 days)
  • Nominal exchange rate: Rate at point in time
FX Market Participants
  • Sell side: Large multinational banks (primary dealers, forward contract originators)
  • Buy side: All others — corporations, investment accounts, govts, retail
  • Real money accounts: Mutual funds, pension funds, insurance — NO derivatives
  • Leveraged accounts: Hedge funds, prop trading — USE derivatives
  • Hedging: FX transaction that REDUCES existing risk
  • Speculating: FX transaction that INCREASES risk
  • Central banks: sometimes engage to affect short-term exchange rates per govt policy
Real Exchange Rate
Real P/B Exchange Rate = Nominal P/B Rate × (CPI_base / CPI_price)
ChangeEffect on Real P/B RatePurchasing Power of Price Currency
↑ Nominal P/B rate↑ Real P/B rate↓ Decreases in base country
↓ Nominal P/B rate↓ Real P/B rate↑ Increases in base country
↑ Price level in BASE country (relative)↑ Real P/B rate↓ Decreases in base country
↑ Price level in PRICE country (relative)↓ Real P/B rate↑ Increases in base country
% Change in Currency
% Change in base currency = (New Rate / Old Rate) − 1
% Appreciation of price currency = 1/(1 + % depreciation of base) − 1
Note: % appreciation of price currency ≠ −(% depreciation of base currency). They are inverses, not negatives. Always convert to find the other.

18.3 Capital Flow Restrictions (LOS 18.c)
Objectives of Capital Restrictions
  • Reduce domestic asset price volatility: Prevent sudden capital flight during crises
  • Maintain fixed exchange rates: Controls make FX target easier to maintain
  • Keep domestic interest rates low: Prevent capital outflow seeking higher foreign rates
  • Protect strategic industries: Prevent foreign ownership of defense/telecom
Example: China
  • Fixed exchange rate regime
  • Capital flow restrictions allow maintaining target FX rate
  • Also allows monetary policy independent of FX rate concerns
  • Can keep domestic interest rates lower than market would otherwise allow
R19 EXCHANGE RATE CALCULATIONS
19.1 Currency Cross Rates (LOS 19.a)
Cross rate: Exchange rate between two currencies implied by their rates with a common third currency (usually USD or EUR)
Method 1: Multiply (USD cancels)
MXN/AUD = USD/AUD × MXN/USD
= 0.60 × 10.70 = 6.42 MXN/AUD
Use when you can arrange quotes so the middle currency cancels by multiplication
Method 2: Divide (USD in denominator of both)
CHF/NZD = (CHF/USD) ÷ (NZD/USD)
= 1.7799 ÷ 2.2529 = 0.7900
Use when both quotes have same currency in denominator — divide
Rule: Arrange quotes algebraically so target currencies end up correctly. If needed, take the inverse of a quote to get it in the right form.
19.2 Forward Rates & Interest Rate Parity (LOS 19.b)
Covered Interest Rate Parity (No-Arbitrage Condition)
Forwardd/f / Spotd/f = (1 + rdomestic) / (1 + rforeign)
Forwardd/f = Spotd/f × [(1 + rdomestic) / (1 + rforeign)]
Spotd/f = Forwardd/f × [(1 + rforeign) / (1 + rdomestic)]
Sub-annual: Forward = Spot × [(1 + rd × days/360) / (1 + rf × days/360)]
Key Principle
  • Currency with HIGHER interest rate must DEPRECIATE in forward market
  • Forward premium ≈ interest rate differential
  • Prevents riskless arbitrage (borrow cheap currency, invest in high-rate currency, lock forward)
Arbitrage Mechanism
  • If forward ≠ no-arbitrage rate: borrow low-rate currency → convert to high-rate → invest → lock forward → riskless profit
  • Arbitrageurs: buy spot (↑ spot price) + sell forward (↓ forward price) → restore equilibrium
Forward Quotes
Points
  • Last decimal place of spot rate quote
  • e.g., Spot = 1.4158, +25.3 points → Forward = 1.4158 + 0.00253 = 1.41833
  • e.g., Spot = 1.3050, −42.5 points → Forward = 1.3050 − 0.00425 = 1.30075
Percentage
  • Forward = Spot × (1 + %)
  • e.g., Spot = 0.7313, −0.062% → Forward = 0.7313 × (1 − 0.00062) = 0.7308
Forward Premium & Discount
Forward Premium/Discount (base currency) = (Forward / Spot) − 1
  • Positive → forward PREMIUM on base currency (base expected to appreciate)
  • Negative → forward DISCOUNT on base currency (base expected to depreciate)
  • Annualize by multiplying by (12/months) or (360/days)
  • Forward rates are POOR predictors of future spot rates (unbiased but noisy; may get direction right)
  • Best interpretation: No-arbitrage rate at a specific point in time — NOT a forecast of future spot rate
IRP vs. Capital Market Analysis (Apparent Contradiction)
Interest Rate Parity (Forward)
Higher domestic rates → domestic currency DEPRECIATES in forward market (to prevent arbitrage)
Capital Market Analysis (Spot)
Higher domestic rates → attract foreign capital → domestic currency APPRECIATES spot
These are not contradictory — they apply to different mechanisms and time horizons. IRP is a no-arbitrage condition at a point in time; capital flows affect spot rates over time. Interest rate differences are historically poor predictors of future spot rates.
