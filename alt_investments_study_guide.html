Alternative Investment Features, Methods & Structures
▼

What are Alternative Investments?

  • NOT traditional (long-only cash/public stocks/bonds)
  • Types: Hedge funds, Private equity, Real estate, commodities, infrastructure, illiquid securities
  • Actively managed

3 Broad Categories

  • Private Capital: Private equity + Private debt
  • Real Assets: Real estate, infrastructure, natural resources, digital assets
  • Hedge Funds: Pooled, qualified investors, diverse strategies

Key Features vs. Traditional Investments

FeatureAlt InvestmentsTraditional
Knowledge requiredMore specializedLess
Correlation with trad.Low (on avg.)N/A
LiquidityLess liquidMore liquid
Time horizonLongerShorter
Min. investmentLargerSmaller
FeesHigherLower
RegulationLess regulatedMore regulated
Correlations with traditional investments may increase significantly during periods of economic stress — diversification benefit erodes when most needed!

Investment Methods (LOS 76.b)

Fund Investing

  • Pool with other investors
  • Manager selects/manages assets
  • Investor has no control
  • Earns: Management fee + Incentive fee
  • Higher min. capital, longer lockups, less transparency

Co-Investing

  • Fund investing + right to invest directly alongside manager in specific assets
  • Lower fees than pure fund
  • Benefits from manager expertise
  • Helps investor build skills for direct investing
  • Benefits GP: more capital, more diversification

Direct Investing

  • Purchases assets itself
  • No outside manager fees
  • More control
  • Disadvantages: less diversification, higher min. investment, need expertise & due diligence
Investor without in-house due diligence expertise → Fund investing. Due diligence is the fund manager's responsibility.

Ownership Structure — Limited Partnerships (LOS 76.c)

General Partner (GP)

  • = Fund manager
  • Makes ALL investment decisions
  • Takes on partnership liabilities (incl. debt repayment)
  • Receives management + performance fees

Limited Partners (LPs)

  • = Investors
  • Own proportional share based on investment
  • No say in management
  • Liability limited to investment only
  • Must be accredited investors

Key LP Terms

  • Limited Partnership Agreement: governing rules
  • Side letters: special terms for one LP
  • Excusal right: LP can withhold capital call
  • Most-favored-nation clause: special terms offered to others also apply to you
  • Term sheet: investment policy, fees, participation requirements

Capital Mechanics

  • LPs commit capital; may contribute partially, rest drawn over time
  • Dry powder: committed but not yet invested capital
  • Drawdown period: typically 3–5 years
  • MLP (Master Limited Partnership): publicly traded; common in natural resources/real estate

Fee Structure Overview

  • Management fee: 1–2% of assets (paid regardless of performance)
  • Performance/Incentive fee: % of profits (also: carried interest)
  • Hedge funds: mgmt fee on AUM
  • Private equity: mgmt fee on committed capital

Hurdle Rates, Catch-up, Waterfall & Clawback

TermDefinitionEffect
Hurdle rate (preferred return)Min. return before perf. fees paidProtects LP
Soft hurdle rateIf met → perf. fee = % of TOTAL gainMore for GP
Hard hurdle ratePerf. fee = % of gains ABOVE hurdle onlyLess for GP
Catch-up clauseGP gets all gains above hurdle to "catch up" to agreed % share; similar effect to soft hurdleReaches 80/20 split
High-water markNo perf. fee on gains that only offset prior losses; fee paid only when value exceeds highest prior NAVProtects LP from double fees
American waterfall (deal-by-deal)Profits distributed as each investment soldFavors GP
European waterfall (whole-of-fund)LP gets 100% of capital + hurdle first, then GP gets carried interestFavors LP
Clawback provisionLPs can recover excess perf. fees if subsequent deals create losses; net effect = European waterfallProtects LP
Soft Hurdle Example (hurdle=8%, return=12%, perf. fee=20%)
Perf. fee = 20% × 12% = 2.4% of total
Hard Hurdle Example (hurdle=8%, return=12%, perf. fee=20%)
Perf. fee = 20% × (12% − 8%) = 0.8% of total
Catch-Up Example (hurdle=8%, return=14%, perf. fee=20%)
First 8% → LPs | Next 2% → GP (catch-up to 20% of 10%) | Remaining 4% → 80% LP / 20% GP

Alternative Investment Performance & Returns
▼

4 Additional Risk Sources in Alt. Investments

  • Timing of cash flows over life cycle
  • Use of leverage by managers
  • Valuation of illiquid assets (no market prices)
  • Complexity of fees, taxes, accounting

J-Curve Effect — 3 Life Cycle Phases

  • Capital Commitment phase: capital calls begin; returns NEGATIVE (cash outflows)
  • Capital Deployment phase: invest in firms; returns still NEGATIVE (esp. start-ups)
  • Capital Distribution phase: investments pay off; returns turn POSITIVE, accelerate

Return Measurement

IRR (Money-Weighted Rate of Return)

  • Most appropriate measure for alt. investments
  • Appropriate when manager controls timing of cash flows
  • Drawback: assumes reinvestment at IRR; depends on cost of capital assumption

Multiple of Invested Capital (Money Multiple / MOIC)

  • Simpler; ignores timing
  • Can be considered "naïve"
MOIC Formula
MOIC = (Total capital returned + Remaining asset value) ÷ Total capital invested

Leveraged Return Formula

Leveraged Portfolio Return
Leveraged Return = [r(V₀ + V_B) − r_B × V_B] ÷ V₀
V₀ = initial equity | V_B = borrowed funds | r = portfolio return rate | r_B = borrowing interest rate
Leverage amplifies BOTH gains and losses. If r > r_B → leverage helps; if r < r_B → leverage hurts.
Leverage risks: margin calls → forced liquidation at bad prices; lenders may restrict further borrowing.

Fair Value Hierarchy (Valuation of Investments)

LevelDescriptionExamples
Level 1Quoted prices in active marketsExchange-traded securities
Level 2Observable inputs (direct or indirect)Many derivatives (model-priced)
Level 3Unobservable inputs — fair value est.Real estate, private equity
Level 3 valuations stay near cost for long periods → reported returns appear HIGHER, risk appears LOWER, correlations appear LOWER than reality. This is a bias.

Redemption Controls

Lockup period

Time after investment when LPs CANNOT redeem (or incur big fees). Especially common in hedge funds.

Notice period

30–90 days fund has to fulfill redemption request. Allows orderly position unwinding.

Gate / Redemption fee

Gate: temporary partial restriction on redemptions. Redemption fee: offsets transaction costs of redemptions.

Other Fee Concepts

  • Founders class shares: early investors get lower fees or better liquidity
  • Either-or fees: annual fee = max(management fee, incentive fee)
  • Vintage year comparison: comparing funds that started investing in same year (addresses life-cycle bias)
  • "2 and 20": 2% mgmt + 20% performance (traditional); now under competitive pressure
  • "1 and 10": fund-of-funds standard fee

Biases in Alt. Investment Returns

BiasDescriptionEffect on Reported Returns
Survivorship biasFailed funds excluded from index; >25% hedge funds fail in 3 yrsOverstates returns, understates risk
Backfill biasManagers add historical returns when joining index (only winners self-report)Overstates returns
Selection biasIndex providers assign funds inconsistently or have different inclusion requirementsDistorts representation

Return Calculation Formulas

GP Total Fees (simple case)
Total fees = m × V₁ + max[0, p(V₁ − V₀)]
m = mgmt fee rate | p = performance fee rate | V₀ = beginning assets | V₁ = end assets
Investor After-Fee Return
r = (V₁ − V₀ − Total fees) ÷ V₀

Fee Calculation Example Workflow

Step-by-Step (Hedge Fund with HWM + Soft Hurdle)

  1. Calculate management fee = fee rate × AUM (beginning or end, per agreement)
  2. Find return net of mgmt fee
  3. Check if return exceeds hurdle rate
  4. Check high-water mark — perf fee applies only on gains above HWM
  5. If soft hurdle met → perf fee = % × total gain above HWM
  6. If hard hurdle → perf fee = % × (gain above HWM − hurdle amount)
  7. After-fee return = (End value − fees) ÷ Beginning value − 1

Investments in Private Capital: Equity & Debt
▼

Private Capital = Private Equity + Private Debt

  • Funding NOT from public markets
  • Portfolio companies: companies in which PE fund invests
  • PE funds: invest in private companies or take public companies private

Risk-Return Spectrum (lowest → highest)

Infrastructure debt → Senior real estate debt → Senior direct lending → Unitranche debt → Mezzanine debt → Private equity (highest)

Private Equity Types

TypeDescriptionKey Notes
LBO (Leveraged Buyout)Acquire public co. with large % debt; take it privateMost common PE fund type; adds value via operational improvement
MBO (Mgmt Buyout)LBO where existing management participatesVariant of LBO
MBI (Mgmt Buy-in)LBO where PE replaces existing managementVariant of LBO
Venture Capital (VC)Early-stage companies; high risk, high returnGets common equity, convertible debt, or preferred stock
Minority equity investingLess-than-controlling interest in public cos.Via PIPE (Private Investment in Public Equity)

VC Investment Stages

StageSub-stageDetails
Formative StagePre-seed / Angel investingIdea stage; business plan; funding from individuals ("angels"), NOT VC funds
Seed-stage / Seed capitalProduct dev, marketing, market research; FIRST stage VC funds invest
Early-stage / Start-upFund operations before production & sales
Later-stage / Expansion VC—After production & sales begun; growth, expansion; founders often sell control to VC
Mezzanine-stage—Preparing for IPO; timing term, not method term (≠ mezzanine financing)
VC funds' FIRST typical investment: Seed stage (not angel/pre-seed which is individuals)

Private Equity Exit Strategies

MethodDescriptionPros/Cons
Trade saleSell to strategic buyer (direct/auction)+ Premium for synergies, fast, low cost | − Management resistance, limited buyers
IPOMost common; investment bank underwrites+ Highest price, visibility | − High cost, compliance burden; needs large firm, stable financials
Direct listingStock listed without underwriters+ Lower cost | − No new capital raised

Write-off / LiquidationFailed investment — take lossesLast resort

Average holding period: ~5 years

Private Debt Categories

TypeDescriptionRisk Level
Direct lendingLoans directly to private co., no intermediary; typically senior & secured with covenantsLow-med
Leveraged loanFund's loan portfolio is itself leveraged to magnify returnsMed
Venture debtVC-backed start-ups not yet profitable; often convertible/with warrants; maintains founder controlHigh
Mezzanine debtSubordinated to senior secured debt; may have conversion rights/warrants for extra risk compensationMed-High
Distressed debtDebt of financially troubled (bankrupt/defaulting) mature companies; active restructuringHigh
Unitranche debtCombines secured + unsecured into ONE loan at blended rate; ranks between senior and subordinatedMed

Private Debt Key Points

  • Higher returns than traditional bonds (illiquidity + default risk premium)
  • Rate usually set vs. reference rate (SOFR) → floating rate
  • Low correlation with traditional investments → diversification benefit
  • Senior private debt: less risk, steadier yields vs. mezzanine; mezzanine has more upside

Diversification & Vintage Year

Diversification

  • Empirical correlations of private capital with public markets: 0.63–0.83 (some benefit but not zero)
  • Should diversify across vintage years

Vintage Year Timing Strategy

  • Expansion vintage → better for early-stage/VC funds
  • Contraction vintage → better for distressed debt funds
  • PE returns higher than stocks on avg., but standard deviation also higher
PE indexes: self-reporting → survivorship + backfill biases → overstated returns. Infrequent revaluation → volatility and correlation underestimated.

  • Controls borrowing, property mgmt, exit strategy

  • Lender owning mortgage or MBS
  • Collateralized (secured) by RE
  • Higher priority claim than equity in default
  • Must be repaid first

Office buildings | Shopping | Industrial/Warehouse/Distribution | Rental residential

REIT Features

REIT Advantages

  • Exchange traded → liquid; NO redemption/liquidation risk (unlike mutual funds)
  • Exempt from double taxation
  • Professional management; diversified pool
  • Report GAAP metrics (EPS) + non-GAAP (Net Asset Value)

REIT Types

  • Equity REIT: invest directly in RE/partnerships
  • Mortgage REIT: lend money or invest in MBS/CMBS
  • Hybrid REIT: both RE + mortgages

REIT Strategy Risk Spectrum (lowest → highest)

StrategyStructureRisk Profile

Overall Real Estate Risk Spectrum (lowest → highest)

First mortgage / Investment-grade CMBS (most bond-like) → Core → Core-plus → Value-add → Opportunistic (most equity-like)

REIT correlation with equities is HIGHER than direct investment. Correlations increase during steep market downturns. Still improves portfolio risk-return vs. traditional-only.

Infrastructure (LOS 79.c)

Infrastructure Types

  • Transportation: roads, airports, ports, railways
  • Utility: gas distribution, electric grids, waste treatment
  • ICT: telecom towers, cable systems
  • Social: prisons, schools, hospitals

Cash Flow Types

  • Availability payments: for making infra available
  • Usage-based payments: e.g., highway tolls
  • Take-or-pay arrangements: buyer pays min. price for agreed volume

Infrastructure Investment Categories

TypeDescriptionRisk/Return
GreenfieldNew construction; BOT (Build-Operate-Transfer) life cycleHighest risk; greater growth potential; lower near-term yield; developing economies → high LT returns
BrownfieldExpand or privatize existing infra; e.g., sale-leasebackStable cash flows, high current yield, little growth
Secondary-stage brownfieldFully operational facilities (existing toll roads, hospitals)LOWEST risk and return; most predictable cash flows

Infrastructure Investment Characteristics

  • Long-lived assets; large scale → low direct liquidity
  • More liquid indirect: ETFs, mutual funds, PE funds, MLPs
  • Equity infra: stable cash flows, low correlation with public equities
  • Infra debt: safer, less affected by economic cycles
  • Risks: regulatory risk, financial leverage risk, construction risk, operational risk
  • Most suitable for: pension plans, life insurance cos., sovereign wealth funds (long-horizon)
  • Riskiest infra: demand-based (uncertain future demand)

Natural Resources = 3 Categories

  • Raw land
  • Farmland & Timberland
  • Commodities

Investment via: direct, ETFs, REITs, LPs, LLCs, commodity futures/swaps

Farmland vs. Timberland

FarmlandTimberland
Typical investorIndividualsInstitutions
Lot sizeSmallerMuch larger
FlexibilityNone (harvest when ready)Flex option (cut when prices high)
Expertise vehicleNewer REITs for retailTIMOs (Timberland Inv. Mgmt Orgs)

Key Land Characteristics (All 3 types)

  • All illiquid; value driven by location
  • Farmland & timberland: proximity to transport, water access, soil quality matter
  • All generate: lease income + price appreciation
  • Farmland & timberland: ALSO generate income from output
  • Fewer financing alternatives than RE; mainly bank loans/direct private debt
  • Agricultural crops consume carbon → attractive for ESG/climate investors

Commodities (LOS 80.b)

3 Major Commodity Sectors

  • Metals: base/industrial (copper, aluminum) + precious (gold, silver)
  • Agricultural: grains, livestock
  • Energy: oil, gas, coal

Classified by: grade (quality) + delivery location

Commodity Investment Methods

  • Direct ownership (grain, gold)
  • Futures (most common): exchange-traded, NO counterparty risk
  • ETPs/ETFs/ETNs: track commodity prices/indices
  • Managed futures funds / CTAs (commodity trading advisers): actively managed LP or mutual fund structure
  • SMAs (separately managed accounts): large/institutional investors, custom portfolios
  • Specialized sector funds

Commodity Pricing & Futures (CRITICAL)

Futures Pricing Formula
Futures price ≈ Spot price × (1 + Risk-free rate) + Storage costs − Convenience yield
OR: Futures price = Spot price + Net cost of carry
Net cost of carry = Financing cost + Storage costs − Convenience yield
TermConditionEffect on Long-only Return
ContangoFutures price > Spot price (low/no convenience yield; positive net cost of carry)📉 Decreases return (rolling losses)
BackwardationFutures price < Spot price (high convenience yield; negative net cost of carry)📈 Increases return (rolling gains)
Convenience yield: non-monetary value of physically having the commodity (e.g., production certainty). High when inventories are low.
Commodity supply is inelastic in the short run (long lead times to change production). Result: high price volatility when demand shifts or supply shocks occur.

Commodity Returns & Diversification (LOS 80.c)

Risk-Return Stats

  • Commodity returns & volatility: HIGHER than stocks or bonds
  • Timberland/farmland: higher avg. returns; lower volatility than global stocks; similar volatility to global bonds
  • Low historical correlation with global equities & bonds → diversification benefit

Inflation Hedge

  • Commodity prices move WITH inflation (positive correlation)
  • High inflation → commodities outperform stocks & bonds
  • Low inflation → commodities underperform
  • More sensitive to geopolitical and weather-related risks

Supply/Demand Analysis Tools

  • Producers analyze: economic events, govt policy, future supply forecasts
  • Investors analyze: inventory levels, production forecasts, govt policy changes, economic growth expectations

Hedge Fund Definition & Features

  • Private pooled investment vehicles
  • Only for qualified/accredited investors
  • Lightly regulated; very flexible mandates
  • Primary return drivers: market inefficiency + price volatility
  • Managers often invest OWN money → alignment
  • Evaluated on total or risk-adjusted return (NOT benchmark)

Hedge Fund Structures

Commingled Fund (Master-Feeder)

  • Capital from many investors pooled
  • Master-feeder structure: offshore feeder + onshore feeder → master fund
  • Tax efficient; economies of scale; global investors
  • Bypasses regional regulatory requirements

SMA (Separately Managed Account)

  • Single large investor; customized portfolio
  • Manager has no stake → less alignment
  • More operational oversight needed
  • Lower negotiated fees; but only gets most liquid trades
  • Suitable for large/institutional investors

Fund-of-Funds (FoF)

  • Invests in other hedge funds
  • Advantages: diversification across strategies, manager expertise, access to otherwise unavailable funds, reduced lockup periods
  • Disadvantage: additional layer of fees (typically 1% + 10% on top of underlying fund fees)
  • Traditional FoF: "1 and 10" on top of underlying "2 and 20"

Sources of Hedge Fund Returns

Return Components
HF Return = Market Beta + Strategy Beta + Alpha
Market beta: broad index return (get via index funds) | Strategy beta: sector exposure return | Alpha: manager skill (security selection) | Leverage amplifies strategy beta + alpha | Fees drag on performance

Performance Biases

Hedge fund performance is highly time-period dependent. With proper due diligence, HFs provide diversification benefits. HFs more correlated with equities than fixed income.
HF fees under competitive pressure. Newer structures: 1% mgmt + 30% perf. fee vs. benchmark (not total return). Voluntary reporting → reporting biases inflate index returns.

Introduction to Digital Assets
▼

Digital Asset Basics

  • Digital assets: electronically created, stored, transferred
  • Includes: cryptocurrencies, tokens, digital collectibles
  • Secured via DLT (Distributed Ledger Technology) = blockchain technology
  • Cryptocurrencies: own blockchains
  • Crypto tokens: built on existing blockchains

Distributed Ledger (DLT)

  • Shared database among market participants
  • All participants have identical copy
  • Benefits: accuracy, transparency, security; rapid ownership transfer; P2P interactions
  • Disadvantages: data protection concerns, privacy violations, large computational power needed

DLT Network Components

Digital Ledger

Records all transactions; immutable once added; cryptographically encrypted

Consensus Mechanism

Validates transactions, updates ledger; creates immutable & transparent records

Smart Contracts

Self-executing computer programs based on predetermined terms; e.g., automated collateral transfers during default

Blockchain Basics

  • Records info sequentially in blocks, linked (chained) together
  • Each block: group of transactions + hash (secure link to previous block)
  • New transaction added only after validated by authorized participants
  • Records are immutable (unchangeable)

Consensus Protocols

ProtocolMechanismNotes
Proof of Work (PoW)Miners solve cryptographic problems using powerful computers; rewards miners with cryptocurrencyMost widely used; requires 51% network control to manipulate; large energy use; more participants = more secure
Proof of Stake (PoS)Validators pledge collateral (staking) to guarantee block validity; additional validators verifyEmerging; validators rewarded with return on staked collateral; lower energy

Network Types

Permissionless (Open)

  • All transactions visible to all users
  • Any user can execute transactions; perform any function
  • Example: Bitcoin
  • No trust required between parties (consensus-based)
  • Immutable record; decentralized

Permissioned

  • Some network activities restricted
  • Controls: from adding transactions → viewing-only (for regulators)
  • More cost-effective than permissionless
  • Stronger restrictions; more centralized

Investment Vehicles (LOS 82.c)

Direct Investment

  • Transaction recorded directly on blockchain
  • Requires cryptocurrency wallet (digital encryption keys)
  • Examples: buy tokens on exchange, trade NFT, invest in ICO
  • Continuous trading on most exchanges
  • Risks: fraud, scam ICOs, pump-and-dump, market manipulation, theft, passkey loss; "whales" can manipulate prices
  • ~20% of Bitcoin estimated in lost/abandoned wallets

Indirect Investment

  • Coin trusts: OTC, like closed-end fund; eliminates wallet need; high fees
  • Futures contracts: e.g., CME Bitcoin futures; cash settled; leveraged; more volatile & less liquid than traditional futures
  • Crypto ETPs/ETFs: mimic digital asset returns via cash or derivatives
  • Crypto stocks: public exchanges, payment providers, miners, blockchain cos.
  • Crypto hedge funds: discretionary long, L/S, quant, multistrategy + mining

Exchanges

Centralized ExchangeDecentralized Exchange
OwnershipPrivately heldNo centralized authority
PrevalenceMost popular typeLess common
SecuritySingle server risk; security vulnerabilities; trading can halt if compromisedMulti-computer → if one attacked, exchange continues; more attack-resistant
RegulationSome regulated as financial exchangesVery difficult to regulate
Shared issueBoth lack regulatory oversight → fraud, pump-and-dump risk

Nondigital Asset Tokenization

Asset-backed tokens & DeFi

  • Asset-backed tokens: digital ownership of physical/financial assets (oil, gold, equities); collateralized by underlying; classified as securities by regulators
  • Increase liquidity via fractional ownership (e.g., real estate)
  • Immutable ownership record → transparency, lower transaction costs
  • Issued on Ethereum via smart contracts (P2P via dApps)
  • DeFi (Decentralized Finance): marketplace of dApps for sophisticated financial products using open-source apps; medium of exchange, store of value, tokenization, immutable records; most current dApps focus on digital asset speculation

Risk, Return & Diversification (LOS 82.d)

Risk-Return Profile

  • Bitcoin limited to 21 million units → scarcity → "digital gold"
  • High returns & high volatility vs. traditional assets
  • Price rise: $0.05 (2010) → $65,000+ (Nov 2021) → below $20,000 (June 2022)
  • Volatility driven by overall uncertainty

Diversification & Price Drivers

  • Low historical correlations with traditional assets → potential diversification benefit
  • Correlations may INCREASE during extreme market stress
  • Price drivers: market adoption, risk appetite, tech innovation, network impacts, speculation, regulation
  • Bitcoin correlation with S&P 500: ~0.21; with bonds: ~0.14

All Formulas

GP Total Fees (simple)
Fees = m×V₁ + max[0, p(V₁−V₀)]
m=mgmt fee rate | p=perf fee rate | V₀=beginning | V₁=ending
Investor After-Fee Return
r = (V₁ − V₀ − Total fees) ÷ V₀
Leveraged Return
= [r(V₀+V_B) − r_B×V_B] ÷ V₀
V₀=equity | V_B=borrowed | r=portfolio return | r_B=borrowing rate
MOIC (Money Multiple)
= (Capital returned + Remaining value) ÷ Capital invested
Futures Pricing
F ≈ S×(1+r_f) + Storage costs − Convenience yield
F=futures price | S=spot price | r_f=risk-free rate
Net Cost of Carry
= Financing cost + Storage costs − Convenience yield
Positive → Contango (F>S) | Negative → Backwardation (F<S)
Soft Hurdle Perf. Fee
= perf_fee_rate × Total gain (entire return)
Applied if return > hurdle rate
Hard Hurdle Perf. Fee
= perf_fee_rate × (Return − Hurdle rate)
Only gains ABOVE hurdle are subject to fee
Hedge Fund Return Components
Return = Market β + Strategy β + Alpha
Leverage amplifies strategy β and alpha (NOT market beta)
Real Estate Equity Value
Equity value = Property value − Outstanding debt
