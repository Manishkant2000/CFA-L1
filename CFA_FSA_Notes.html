R27 INTRODUCTION TO FINANCIAL STATEMENT ANALYSIS
LOS 27.a: 6-Step FSA Framework
Steps (memorize in order)
  • Step 1: State objective and context
  • Step 2: Gather data (FS + industry + economy)
  • Step 3: Process data (adjust FS, calculate ratios, common-size)
  • Step 4: Analyze and interpret data
  • Step 5: Report conclusions/recommendations (comply with Code & Standards)
  • Step 6: Update analysis periodically
LOS 27.b/c: Roles & Regulatory Framework
Key Entities
  • FASB: US GAAP standard-setter | IASB: IFRS standard-setter
  • SEC (US), FCA (UK): Regulatory authorities (government, legal enforcement)
  • IOSCO: International coordination body (200+ members, 95% of markets); NOT a regulator itself; 3 objectives: protect investors, fair/efficient/transparent markets, reduce systemic risk
  • ESMA: European Securities and Markets Authority
  • Sarbanes-Oxley 2002: External auditor restrictions; CEO/CFO certification; management internal controls assessment
  • SEC Filings: 10-K (annual), 10-Q (quarterly), 8-K (material events), Proxy Statement (shareholder votes—board elections, compensation)
Audit Opinions & Documents
  • Unqualified (clean) opinion: Statements free from material omissions/errors — best
  • Qualified opinion: Exceptions to accounting principles noted
  • Adverse opinion: Statements not presented fairly
  • Disclaimer of opinion: Auditor unable to express opinion (scope limitation)
  • Key/Critical audit matters: Disclosed in audit report; most significant accounting choices
  • Management commentary (MD&A): Liquidity, capital resources, results of operations; may be UNAUDITED
  • Footnotes (notes): Accounting methods, estimates, segment data, contingencies; ARE audited
  • Segment disclosure: Required if segment >10% of revenue/assets/income; must cover ≥75% of external sales. Disclose: revenue, profit/loss, assets/liabilities, interest, D&A, PP&E acquisitions, tax expense
LOS 27.d: IFRS vs US GAAP Key Differences
  • IFRS: Principles-based | US GAAP: Rules-based
  • IFRS: Allows inventory write-up reversals | US GAAP: No reversals
  • IFRS: Development costs may be capitalized | US GAAP: Expense both R&D
  • IFRS: Revaluation model for PP&E | US GAAP: Cost model only
  • IFRS: LIFO prohibited | US GAAP: LIFO allowed
  • Monitor: IASB (www.ifrs.org), FASB (www.fasb.org)
LOS 27.e: Information Sources
  • Issuer sources: Earnings calls, press releases, ad hoc events, management communications (usually UNAUDITED)
  • Public 3rd-party: Trade journals, govt stats, social media (free)
  • Proprietary 3rd-party: Analyst reports, Bloomberg/FactSet, industry consultancies (paid)
  • Proprietary primary research: Commissioned studies, product testing, technical specialists
R28 ANALYZING INCOME STATEMENTS
LOS 28.a: Revenue Recognition — 5-Step Process (IFRS 15 / ASC 606)
5 Steps: (1) Identify contract → (2) Identify performance obligations → (3) Determine transaction price → (4) Allocate price to obligations → (5) Recognize revenue when obligation satisfied
Key Concepts
  • Performance obligation: Promise to deliver a distinct good/service
  • Transaction price: Amount expected from customer (fixed or variable)
  • Over time recognition: If customer benefits continuously, seller enhances customer-controlled asset, or asset has no alternative use + right to payment for work done
  • Long-term contracts: % completion = costs incurred ÷ estimated total costs (input method); or milestones (output method)
  • Costs to secure contract (e.g., sales commissions): CAPITALIZED and expensed over life
  • Unearned revenue: Cash received before revenue recognized = liability
Special Revenue Scenarios
  • Principal vs. Agent: Principal reports GROSS revenue; Agent reports NET (commission only). Agent: bears no inventory/credit risk, acts on behalf of another
  • Bill-and-hold: Customer pays before shipping; revenue recognized if performance complete, goods identified as customer's, ready for transfer, cannot be redirected
  • Franchise/License fees: Initially deferred; amortized over contract life. Royalties: recognized when contractually payable
  • Software licensing: Over life if supplier updates; at outset if "sold as is" + separate support contract
  • Cloud-based: Service → recognize over contract life
Required Disclosures
  • Contracts by category
  • Assets/liabilities from contracts
  • Outstanding performance obligations + allocated prices
  • Management judgments on timing/amount of recognition
Analyst Implications
  • Revenue recognized when EARNED, not when cash received
  • Review estimates, collectability assumptions
  • Check FOB shipping point vs. destination (affects timing)
  • Watch for channel stuffing, barter transactions
LOS 28.b: Expense Recognition
Expense Recognition Methods
  • Matching principle: Expenses recognized same period as related revenue (e.g., COGS, warranty expense)
  • Period costs: Expensed as incurred (admin, advertising, training, reorganization, startup costs)
  • Capitalization: Cost initially on balance sheet → expensed via depreciation/amortization over asset life
  • Aggressive: Delayed expense recognition ↑ current income | Conservative: Earlier recognition ↓ current income
  • Bad debt & warranty expense: Estimated and recognized at time of sale (matching)
  • R&D: IFRS — research expensed; development MAY be capitalized | US GAAP — both expensed (except software)
  • Software (US GAAP): Expense until "technological feasibility" established, then capitalize costs of salable product
  • Capitalized interest: During self-construction of assets; appears in CFI, not CFO. For coverage ratios, add back capitalized interest + remove related depreciation

LOS 28.c: Non-Recurring Items
Unusual/Infrequent Items
  • Reported BEFORE TAX, ABOVE income from continuing operations
  • Examples: gains/losses on asset sales, impairments, restructuring costs, write-offs
  • Analyst: Evaluate whether truly non-recurring; some "unusual" losses recur
Discontinued Operations
  • Reported NET OF TAX, BELOW income from continuing operations
  • Must be physically/operationally distinct (assets, operations, investing & financing)
  • Measurement date: When formal disposal plan developed
  • Phaseout period: Between measurement date and disposal date
  • Prior income statements restated to separate discontinued operations
  • Analyst: Exclude from earnings forecasts
Accounting Changes
  • Change in policy: Retrospective application (restate prior periods) — e.g., change in inventory method
  • Modified retrospective: Adjust beginning balances; no prior period restatement (used for revenue recognition changes)
  • Change in estimate: Prospective application only (no restatement); e.g., change in useful life of asset. No cash flow effect
  • Prior-period error correction: Retrospective (restate prior periods)
  • Changes in scope (M&A) and exchange rates: Not required to disclose separately; analyst must be alert
LOS 28.d: Earnings Per Share (EPS)
Basic & Diluted EPS Formulas
Basic EPS = (Net income − Preferred dividends) / Weighted avg common shares outstanding Diluted EPS = (Net income − Preferred divs + Convertible preferred divs + After-tax interest on conv. debt) / (Wtd avg shares + All dilutive potential shares)
  • Simple capital structure: No dilutive securities → report Basic EPS only
  • Complex capital structure: Has dilutive securities → report both Basic & Diluted EPS
  • Dilutive security: If converted, DECREASES EPS → include in diluted EPS
  • Antidilutive security: If converted, INCREASES EPS → EXCLUDE from diluted EPS
  • Stock dividend/stock split: Applied RETROACTIVELY to beginning of year to ALL shares before the event
  • Shares issued: enter from date of issuance | Reacquired: excluded from date of reacquisition
Convertible Securities & Options
Convertible preferred — check if dilutive: Per-share impact = Preferred dividend / New shares created If < Basic EPS → dilutive Convertible debt — check if dilutive: Per-share impact = After-tax interest / New shares created If < Basic EPS → dilutive Stock options/warrants — Treasury Stock Method: Net new shares = Options outstanding − (Exercise price × Options / Avg market price) = Options × (1 − Exercise price / Avg market price) Dilutive only if exercise price < average market price Numerator adjustments: + Convertible preferred dividends (if dilutive) + After-tax interest on convertible bonds (if dilutive) = Bond interest × (1 − tax rate)
LOS 28.e: Common-Size Income Statements & Ratios
Common-Size Format
  • Vertical common-size income statement: Each line ÷ Revenue
  • Exception: Tax expense → expressed as % of pretax income = effective tax rate
  • Allows time-series and cross-sectional comparison; eliminates size effects
Key Income Statement Ratios
Gross profit margin = Gross profit / Revenue Net profit margin = Net income / Revenue Operating profit margin = EBIT (or operating income) / Revenue Pretax margin = EBT / Revenue
R29 ANALYZING BALANCE SHEETS
LOS 29.a: Intangible Assets
Identifiable vs. Unidentifiable Intangibles
  • Identifiable: Patents, trademarks, copyrights, franchises — can be acquired separately or arise from contractual/legal rights
  • Unidentifiable: Goodwill (cannot be purchased separately; indefinite life)
  • Finite-lived: Amortized over useful life; tested for impairment
  • Indefinite-lived: NOT amortized; tested for impairment at least annually
  • Purchased intangibles: Recorded at cost; amortized if finite life
  • Internally generated: EXPENSED as incurred (with exceptions)
  • IFRS: Research → expense; Development → MAY capitalize if criteria met (technically feasible, intention, resources, market exists)
  • US GAAP: BOTH R&D expensed; software for sale → capitalize after tech feasibility; software for internal use → capitalize when probable to complete
  • Always expense (both IFRS & GAAP): Startup, training, admin overhead, advertising, relocation, termination costs
  • IFRS: Cost model OR revaluation model (if active market exists) | US GAAP: Cost model only
LOS 29.b: Goodwill
  • Goodwill = Purchase price − Fair value of identifiable net assets acquired
  • Only created in a business combination (purchase acquisition)
  • Internally generated goodwill is EXPENSED as incurred
  • NOT amortized; tested for impairment at LEAST annually
  • Impairment = loss on income statement; no cash flow impact
  • Bargain purchase: If purchase price < FV of net assets → gain recognized immediately in income statement
  • Accounting goodwill ≠ Economic goodwill (economic = expected future excess returns)
  • Analysts: Remove goodwill from balance sheet; remove impairment charges from IS for comparability
  • Management may allocate more to goodwill (not amortized) vs. identifiable assets (amortized) → higher future earnings

LOS 29.d: Non-Current Liabilities
Long-Term Financial Liabilities
  • Bonds payable, bank loans, notes payable: reported at amortized cost (unless hedged by derivatives)
  • Amortized cost moves toward par value at maturity as premium/discount amortizes
  • Discount bond: carrying value INCREASES over time | Premium bond: carrying value DECREASES over time
  • Held-for-trading liabilities, derivatives: reported at fair value
Deferred Tax Liabilities
  • Income taxes payable in future periods due to temporary timing differences
  • Created when financial accounting income > taxable income (e.g., straight-line vs. accelerated depreciation)
  • Created when revenue recognized in FS before taxable
  • Will reverse in future periods when taxes are paid
LOS 29.e: Common-Size Balance Sheet & Ratios
Common-Size Format & Ratios
  • Vertical common-size balance sheet: Each item ÷ Total assets
  • Enables size-neutral comparison across firms and over time
Current ratio = Current assets / Current liabilities Quick ratio (Acid-test) = (Cash + Marketable securities + Receivables) / Current liabilities Cash ratio = (Cash + Marketable securities) / Current liabilities Long-term debt-to-equity = LT debt / Total equity Total debt-to-equity = Total debt / Total equity Debt ratio (Debt-to-assets) = Total debt / Total assets Financial leverage ratio = Average total assets / Average total equity
Ratio Limitations
  • Different accounting standards limit cross-company comparisons
  • Ratios not comparable across different industries
  • Interpretation requires judgment
  • Balance sheet data = single point in time only
R30 ANALYZING STATEMENTS OF CASH FLOWS I
LOS 30.a: Cash Flow Statement Linkages
Cash Flow Activities
  • CFO (Operating): Current assets & liabilities; day-to-day business
  • CFI (Investing): Non-current assets; acquisitions/disposals
  • CFF (Financing): Non-current liabilities & equity; borrowings, equity issuances, dividends paid
  • Quality of earnings: CFO close to or > net income = high quality
  • CFO < net income persistently → aggressive accounting
Balance Sheet — Cash Flow Links
Cash collections = Revenue − ΔAccounts receivable + ΔUnearned revenue Cash paid to suppliers = COGS + ΔInventory − ΔAccounts payable Cash paid for taxes = Tax expense − ΔDTL + ΔDTA − ΔTaxes payable Key rules: ↑ Asset = USE of cash (subtract) ↓ Asset = SOURCE of cash (add) ↑ Liability = SOURCE of cash (add) ↓ Liability = USE of cash (subtract)
LOS 30.b: Direct vs. Indirect CFO Methods
Direct Method
  • Shows actual cash receipts and payments by category
  • Start with each IS line → adjust for related B/S changes → ignore non-cash charges
  • BOTH IFRS and US GAAP encourage; US GAAP requires indirect method footnote if direct used
  • More informative but rarely used in practice
Cash from customers = Revenue − ΔAR + ΔUnearned revenue Cash to suppliers = −COGS − ΔInventory + ΔAP Cash operating expenses = −Exp − ΔPrepaid + ΔAccrued liabilities Cash taxes = −Tax expense + ΔDTL − ΔDTA + ΔTaxes payable
Indirect Method
  • Start with Net Income → adjust for: (1) non-cash charges, (2) non-operating items, (3) working capital changes
  • Shows reconciliation of NI to CFO → indicates earnings quality
  • Most commonly used in practice
CFO = Net income + Depreciation & amortization (add back — non-cash) + Amortization of discount / − Amortization of premium on bonds − Gains / + Losses on asset sales (non-operating; remove from CFO) − ↑ Operating assets / + ↓ Operating assets + ↑ Operating liabilities / − ↓ Operating liabilities
LOS 30.b: Computing CFI & CFF
CFI Calculations
Asset disposal proceeds = Carrying value ± Gain/Loss on sale Carrying value of disposed asset = Beginning CV − Depreciation + Additions − Ending CV PP&E roll-forward: Beginning gross PP&E + Additions − Disposals (cost) = Ending gross PP&E Beginning AccumDepr + Depr expense − AccumDepr on disposals = Ending AccumDepr → Solve for cost and AccumDepr of disposed assets → Carrying value = Cost − AccumDepr of disposed asset → Proceeds = Carrying value + Gain (or − Loss)
CFF Calculations
CFF = Net new borrowings (bonds issued − repaid) + Net equity issuance (shares issued − repurchased) − Cash dividends paid Dividends declared = ΔRetained earnings + Net income (rearranged) Cash dividends paid = Dividends declared − ΔDividends payable Note: Level I assumes bonds issued at par (no premium/discount complications) Note: Stock buybacks assumed at original issuance price
LOS 30.c & 30.d: Conversions & IFRS vs. US GAAP
Indirect → Direct Conversion
  • Step 1: Aggregate all revenues/gains and all expenses/losses
  • Step 2: Remove non-cash charges; disaggregate remaining items
  • Step 3: Convert from accruals to cash by adjusting for working capital changes

R31 ANALYZING STATEMENTS OF CASH FLOWS II
LOS 31.a: Cash Flow Analysis & Common-Size
Cash Flow Analysis
  • Positive CFO > capital expenditures over long-term = sustainable
  • Early-stage firms: negative CFO funded by debt/equity (not sustainable long-term)
  • CFO manipulation: reducing noncash WC (liquidate inventory/receivables, ↑ payables) — not sustainable
  • CFO to NI ratio declining = potential earnings quality problem
  • CFI analysis: ↑ capex = growth; ↓ capex or asset sales = cash conservation (may signal future higher capex needs)
  • CFF analysis: Issuing debt to buy back shares/pay dividends = analyst concern
Common-Size Cash Flow Statement
  • Method 1: Each item as % of revenue (best for forecasting)
  • Method 2: Each inflow as % of total inflows; each outflow as % of total outflows
  • Revenue-based: Good for trend analysis and forecasting

FCFF = CFO + [Interest paid × (1−t)] − Fixed capital investment (capex)

R32 ANALYSIS OF INVENTORIES
LOS 32.a: Lower of Cost or NRV / Market
Inventory Valuation Rules
  • IFRS: Lower of cost or NRV (= selling price − selling costs − completion costs); write-ups ALLOWED (limited to original write-down)
  • US GAAP (non-LIFO/retail): Lower of cost or NRV; write-ups NOT allowed
  • US GAAP (LIFO/retail method): Lower of cost or market; market = replacement cost constrained: NRV (upper) ≥ market ≥ NRV − normal profit margin (lower); write-ups NOT allowed
  • Commodity exception (both IFRS/GAAP): Agricultural, forest, mineral, precious metals → can carry at NRV above cost; unrealized G/L on IS
  • Write-down: Recognize loss on IS (as COGS or separate line); reduce inventory on BS
  • LIFO firms: Less likely to need write-down (older lower cost basis)
Effects of Inventory Write-Down
  • ↓ Inventory (current asset) → ↓ Current assets → ↓ Total assets
  • ↓ Current ratio (QR unaffected — no inventory in QR)
  • ↑ Inventory turnover (COGS ↑, inventory ↓) → ↓ Days inventory on hand
  • ↑ Asset turnover, ↑ Debt-to-assets (assets ↓)
  • ↓ Equity → ↑ Debt-to-equity
  • ↓ Gross margin, operating margin, net margin
  • ↓ ROA and ROE in write-down period
  • Post write-down periods: ↑ ROA, ROE (lower asset/equity base; possibly lower COGS)
  • No cash flow effect (not tax deductible until sold)

LOS 32.c: Inventory Disclosures & Ratios
Required Disclosures (IFRS & US GAAP)
  • Cost flow method (FIFO, LIFO, weighted avg)
  • Total carrying value; by classification (raw materials, WIP, finished goods)
  • Carrying value at fair value less selling costs
  • Cost recognized as expense (COGS)
  • Write-downs during period
  • Write-up reversals (IFRS only — US GAAP prohibits reversals)
  • Inventory pledged as collateral
  • LIFO not permitted under IFRS
Analyst Red Flags
  • Finished goods ↑ while raw materials & WIP ↓ → declining demand, future write-downs
  • Raw materials/WIP ↑ → rising future demand
  • Finished goods growing faster than sales → declining demand / obsolescence
  • High turnover + slow sales growth → inadequate inventory / lost sales
  • High turnover + high sales growth → efficiency improvement
  • Low turnover → slow-moving/obsolete inventory
Inventory turnover = COGS / Average inventory Days of inventory on hand = 365 / Inventory turnover
R33 ANALYSIS OF LONG-TERM ASSETS
LOS 33.a: Intangible Long-Lived Assets — Purchased vs. Internally Generated vs. Acquired in Business Combination
Internally Created Intangibles
  • Generally EXPENSED as incurred
  • IFRS: Research → expense; Development → capitalize if criteria met (technically feasible, intention & resources to complete, market exists)
  • US GAAP: Both R&D expensed; Software for sale → expense until tech feasibility, then capitalize; Internal use software → capitalize when probable to complete
  • Capitalizing dev costs: ↑ assets & equity; ↑ CFO (outflow in CFI); smoother earnings
  • Internally generated goodwill: ALWAYS expensed
Purchased Intangibles
  • Recorded at cost (fair value at acquisition)
  • Group purchase: allocate by fair value of each asset
  • Financial statement effects same as capitalizing vs. expensing (see R28)
  • Company with internally generated intangibles has LOWER balance sheet assets than one that buys them (impacts ratios)
Business Combination (Acquisition Method)
  • All identifiable assets/liabilities recorded at fair value at acquisition date
  • Previously expensed identifiable intangibles NOW capitalized (on acquiring firm's BS)
  • Goodwill = Purchase price − FV of identifiable net assets
  • Goodwill = UNIDENTIFIABLE intangible (cannot be separated from business)
  • Capitalizing more identifiable intangibles → less goodwill → less future impairment risk but more future D&A expense

LOS 33.c: Long-Term Asset Disclosures & Analysis
Asset Age Formulas (assuming straight-line, zero salvage)
Average age = Accumulated depreciation / Annual depreciation expense Total useful life = Gross PP&E / Annual depreciation expense Remaining useful life = Net PP&E / Annual depreciation expense (also = Total useful life − Average age) Fixed asset turnover = Revenue / Average net PP&E Capex-to-depreciation = Capital expenditures / Depreciation expense
Required PP&E Disclosures
  • Both IFRS & US GAAP: Depreciation method, expense, useful life/rate, gross CV & accum. depr, title restrictions & pledged assets, impairment details
  • IFRS only: Revaluation date, FV determination, CV using historical cost, revaluation surplus in OCI
  • US GAAP only: Estimate of amortization for next 5 years (intangibles)
  • Average age NOT a required disclosure (calculated by analyst)
R34 TOPICS IN LONG-TERM LIABILITIES AND EQUITY
LOS 34.a: Leases
Finance lease criteria (≥1 must be met): (1) Ownership transfers to lessee | (2) Lessee has purchase option expected to be exercised | (3) Lease = most of asset's useful life | (4) PV of lease payments ≥ asset's fair value | (5) Asset specialized/no other use for lessor
Lessee — Finance Lease (IFRS & US GAAP)
  • Record ROU asset + Lease liability = PV of lease payments
  • IS: Amortization expense (ROU) + Interest expense (liability)
  • CFF: Principal repayment | CFO (GAAP) or CFO/CFF (IFRS): Interest paid
  • ROU amortized straight-line (IFRS) → ROU < lease liability during life
  • Early years: Total IS expense > Operating lease; Later years: <
  • Exceptions: Short-term (<12 months) or low-value (<USD 5,000 — IFRS only) → rent expense only; no BS entries
Lessee — Operating Lease (US GAAP)
  • Also records ROU asset + Lease liability = PV of payments
  • But: IS shows single lease expense = payment amount (not split into interest + amortization)
  • ROU amortized = principal repayment each period → ROU = Lease liability at all times
  • CFO: Entire payment classified as operating outflow
  • Result: Finance > Operating lease assets in early years (IFRS ROU amortized more aggressively)
  • IFRS: No distinction — all non-exempt leases treated like finance leases for income statement (interest + amortization)
Lessor Accounting
  • Finance lease: Remove asset; add lease receivable; record interest income (EIR method); profit/loss at inception if manufacturer/dealer
  • Sales-type lease (manufacturer/dealer): Revenue = PV of lease receipts; COGS = asset CV − PV of residual value; profit/loss at initiation
  • Direct financing lease (financing co.): No profit/loss at inception; gain/loss deferred and recognized as interest over life
  • Operating lease: Keep asset on BS; depreciate it; recognize lease payments as income straight-line
  • Lessor cash flows: ALWAYS classified as CFO regardless of lease type

LOS 34.b: Deferred Compensation
Pension Plans
  • Defined contribution (DC): Firm contributes fixed amount; no future obligation; pension expense = contribution; employee bears investment risk
  • Defined benefit (DB): Firm promises future benefits; employee bears no investment risk; firm records net pension asset/liability
  • Funded status = Fair value of plan assets − Present value of benefit obligation (PVDBO/PBO)
  • Overfunded → net pension ASSET | Underfunded → net pension LIABILITY
  • Treat DB pension liability as DEBT instrument in leverage analysis (carries interest element)

Stock-Based Compensation
  • Advantages: Aligns managers/shareholders; reduces agency costs; no cash outflow initially
  • Disadvantages: Individual may not control stock price; options → asymmetric payoffs → excessive risk-taking; dilutes existing shareholders
  • Both IFRS & US GAAP: Estimate fair value at grant date; expense over vesting period (service period = grant date to first exercise date)
  • Stock grants: FV = market price at grant date; if immediate vesting → expense immediately; if deferred → straight-line over service period; increase common stock + APIC
  • Stock options: FV via Black-Scholes-Merton or binomial model; highly subjective inputs (especially volatility); expense over vesting period; on exercise → cash in (exercise price), new shares issued
  • SARs (Stock Appreciation Rights): Cash payment linked to stock performance; no new shares; like options but cash settled; no dilution but cash outflow when stock performs well
  • Phantom stock: SAR variant for non-public firms; cash based on hypothetical stock performance
  • Restricted stock units (RSUs): Stock grants that vest on length of service or performance goals
  • Performance shares: Grant dependent on performance targets (e.g., ROE); risk of financial statement manipulation
R35 ANALYSIS OF INCOME TAXES
LOS 35.a: Key Tax Terminology
Tax Return Terms
  • Taxable income: Income per tax return (tax rules)
  • Taxes payable: Tax liability = taxable income × statutory rate (= current tax expense)
  • Income tax paid: Actual cash for taxes (may differ due to payments/refunds from other years)
  • Tax loss carryforward: Past losses to reduce future taxable income → creates DTA
  • Tax base: Net amount of asset/liability for tax purposes
Financial Reporting Terms
  • Accounting profit: Pretax income per GAAP (= income before tax = EBT)
  • Income tax expense: Per income statement = taxes payable + ΔDTL − ΔDTA
  • DTA (Deferred Tax Asset): Taxes payable > tax expense; future tax savings expected; created by deductible temporary differences
  • DTL (Deferred Tax Liability): Tax expense > taxes payable; future tax outflow expected; created by taxable temporary differences
  • Carrying value: Net book value of asset/liability per GAAP
  • Valuation allowance: Reduces DTA when future realization uncertain (US GAAP uses contra account; IFRS reduces DTA directly)
  • Permanent difference: Never reverses; NO DTA or DTL created (e.g., tax-exempt muni bond interest, non-deductible expenses)
  • Temporary difference: Will reverse; creates DTA or DTL
LOS 35.a/b: DTA & DTL Creation

Analyst treatment of DTLs: If expected to REVERSE → treat as liability | If NOT expected to reverse (continued capital expenditure growth) → treat as equity
LOS 35.c: Tax Rates
Statutory Tax Rate
Corporate income tax rate of firm's domicile country
Effective Tax Rate
Income tax expense / Pretax accounting income (EBT)
Useful for forecasting future earnings

Useful for forecasting future tax cash flows

R36 FINANCIAL REPORTING QUALITY
LOS 36.a/b: Reporting Quality Spectrum
Quality Definitions
  • Financial reporting quality: Characteristics of financial statements — adherence to GAAP, decision-usefulness, relevance, faithful representation (completeness, neutrality, free from error), materiality
  • Quality of reported results: Level and sustainability of earnings, cash flows, balance sheet values
  • High-quality earnings: Adequate return to investors AND sustainable in future periods
  • A firm can have HIGH reporting quality but LOW earnings quality (GAAP-compliant but unsustainable earnings)
Quality Spectrum (Best → Worst)
  1. GAAP-compliant, decision-useful; sustainable & adequate earnings ← BEST
  2. GAAP-compliant, decision-useful; low earnings quality (unsustainable/inadequate)
  3. GAAP-compliant; low earnings quality; biased choices/estimates
  4. GAAP-compliant; actively managed (smoothed) earnings
  5. Non-GAAP; based on actual economic activities
  6. Non-GAAP; fictitious/fraudulent numbers ← WORST
LOS 36.c: Conservative vs. Aggressive Accounting
Aggressive vs. Conservative (Key Examples)
AreaAggressive (↑ current NI)Conservative (↓ current NI)

Earnings Smoothing & Cookie Jar
  • During HIGH earnings periods: Conservative bias → store earnings ("cookie jar")
  • During LOW earnings periods: Aggressive bias → release stored earnings
  • Goal: Reduce earnings volatility (↑ share value)
  • GAAP itself can impose conservatism: write-downs allowed but write-ups restricted; research expensed under uncertainty
  • Conservative bias also: ↓ litigation risk, ↓ current period tax (if tax follows accounting), ↓ information asymmetry risk
LOS 36.d/e: Motivations & Mechanisms
Motivations for Low-Quality Reporting
  • Meet/exceed earnings guidance (prior management guidance, analyst consensus, prior year EPS)
  • Career advancement / incentive compensation linked to earnings/stock
  • Avoid violating debt covenants
  • Improve perception by customers/suppliers
  • 3 elements: Motivation + Opportunity + Rationalization
  • Opportunity factors: Weak internal controls, inadequate board oversight, wide range of acceptable accounting treatments
Disciplinary Mechanisms & Limitations
  • Regulators: SEC (US), FCA (UK), IOSCO (international), ESMA (EU)
  • Require: Periodic FS + notes, independent audit, management commentary, signed certification
  • Auditors: Clean opinion ≠ guarantee of no fraud; only "reasonable assurance" of no material errors; audit NOT designed primarily to detect fraud; firm selects & pays auditor
  • Private contracts: Loan covenants specify calculation methods; creditors have incentive for quality reporting
LOS 36.f/g/h: Non-GAAP Measures, Accounting Choices & Warning Signs
Non-GAAP Measures
  • Often exclude items to look better; claim items are "non-recurring" or "non-cash"
  • US GAAP required: Show GAAP measure equally prominently; explain why useful; reconcile; include recurring items even if labeled non-recurring
  • IFRS required: Define and explain relevance; reconcile to comparable IFRS measure
Earnings Management Methods
  • Revenue timing: FOB shipping point, channel stuffing, bill-and-hold, barter, accelerating/delaying shipments
  • Estimates: Bad debt reserve, warranty reserve, valuation allowance for DTA
  • Depreciation: Methods, useful life estimates, salvage values, impairment timing
  • Inventory: Cost flow method choice
  • Capitalization: Inappropriate capitalization of expenses → ↑ current NI, ↑ CFO
  • Related-party transactions: Shift profits between entities
  • Stretching payables: Pay later → ↑ CFO (no effect on earnings)
  • IFRS classification flexibility: Interest/dividends received/paid → manage CFO
  • Capitalizing interest → ↑ CFO (moves from operating to investing)
Warning Signs for Analysts
  • Revenue growth inconsistent with peers
  • ↓ Receivables turnover (collecting slower — channel stuffing?)
  • ↓ Total asset turnover (especially if growing via acquisition)
  • Non-operating items included in revenue
  • ↓ Inventory turnover
  • LIFO liquidations
  • Capitalization policies differ from industry peers
  • CFO/NI ratio < 1 persistently or declining
  • Depreciation methods/lives/salvage differ from peers
  • Unusual Q4 earnings pattern (not explained by seasonality)
  • Frequent related-party transactions
  • Frequent "non-recurring" items
  • Abnormally high gross/operating margins vs. peers
  • Minimal disclosure; aggressive use of non-GAAP measures
  • Frequent acquisitions (obscures comparability)
  • Large restructuring/impairment charges (corrections of prior overstatements)
  • Changes in revenue recognition methods; lack of transparency
R37 FINANCIAL ANALYSIS TECHNIQUES
LOS 37.a: Tools & Limitations
Analytical Tools
  • Ratio analysis: Relationships among data; forecast earnings/cash flow; assess management; compare firms over time
  • Common-size analysis: Vertical (% of total) and horizontal (indexed to base year)
  • Graphical analysis: Stacked column/bar graphs; line graphs for trends

Ratio Limitations
  • Ratios meaningless in isolation — need comparison benchmark
  • Cross-company comparisons limited by different accounting treatments (IFRS vs. GAAP)
  • Difficult for multi-industry companies (which peer group?)
  • Single ratio insufficient; must be viewed in context of others
  • Defining "target" or "normal" ratio value requires judgment
  • Must consider business cycle, strategy, and analyst expectations
  • Ratio definitions vary across analysts (be consistent)

LOS 37.d: DuPont Analysis
3-Part DuPont (Original)
ROE = Net profit margin × Total asset turnover × Financial leverage = (NI/Revenue) × (Revenue/Avg assets) × (Avg assets/Avg equity) ROE = ROA × Financial leverage multiplier
5-Part DuPont (Extended)
ROE = Tax burden × Interest burden × EBIT margin × Asset turnover × Financial leverage = (NI/EBT) × (EBT/EBIT) × (EBIT/Revenue) × (Revenue/Avg assets) × (Avg assets/Avg equity) Tax burden = NI/EBT (higher = lower taxes) Interest burden = EBT/EBIT (lower = higher interest burden; leverage doesn't always improve ROE)
LOS 37.e: Industry-Specific Ratios & LOS 37.f: Forecasting
Industry-Specific Ratios
  • Service/consulting: Net income/employee, sales/employee
  • Retail/restaurant: Same-store sales growth, sales per square foot
  • Hotel: Average daily rate, occupancy rate
  • Streaming/subscription: Average revenue per user (ARPU)
  • Banks: Capital adequacy ratio, liquid asset requirement, net interest margin (interest income / interest-earning assets), value at risk (VaR)
  • Business risk: Coefficient of variation = Standard deviation / Mean (size-adjusted risk measure)
Pro Forma Modeling & Variability Analysis
  • Common-size ratios → forecast expenses as % of sales
  • Pro forma income statement → balance sheet → cash flow statement
  • Sensitivity analysis: "What if" questions; single variable changes
  • Scenario analysis: Specific sets of outcomes for multiple variables
  • Simulation: Computer-generated distribution using probability distributions for key variables
R38 INTRODUCTION TO FINANCIAL STATEMENT MODELING
LOS 38.a: Sales-Based Pro Forma Model — 8 Steps
Step 1: Estimate revenue growth & future revenue (market growth, market share, trend, GDP-relative)
Step 2: Estimate COGS (% of sales or detailed analysis of strategy/competition)
Step 3: Estimate SG&A (fixed, revenue-linked, or other method)
Step 4: Estimate financing costs (interest rates, debt levels, capex changes)
Step 5: Estimate income tax expense and cash taxes (effective rates, segment rates, deferred tax changes)
Step 6: Model balance sheet (working capital accounts flow from IS)
Step 7: Estimate capex and net PP&E (from depreciation + capex for maintenance and growth)
Step 8: Construct pro forma cash flow statement from IS and BS
LOS 38.b: Behavioral Biases in Analyst Forecasts
BiasDescriptionMitigation
OverconfidenceToo much faith in own work; confidence intervals too narrow; "going against the grain" analysts most susceptibleShare/seek critique; evaluate past errors; use scenario analysis; widen confidence intervals
Illusion of ControlOverestimate control; seek "expert" opinions to justify; over-complex models (more variables); overfitted models perform poorly out-of-sampleFocus on variables with known explanatory power; seek only relevant outside opinions; simpler models

LOS 38.c: Porter's Five Forces
5 Forces & Impact on Pricing/Costs
  • Threat of substitutes: High threat & low switching costs → LESS pricing power
  • Industry rivalry intensity: High rivalry (many competitors, high fixed costs/exit barriers, slow growth, undifferentiated products) → LESS pricing power
  • Bargaining power of suppliers: High supplier power → HIGHER input costs → lower earnings prospects
  • Bargaining power of customers: High buyer power (few customers, large purchases, low switching costs) → LESS pricing power
  • Threat of new entrants: High barriers to entry → MORE pricing power; able to sustain economic profits
LOS 38.d: Input Cost Inflation/Deflation
  • Input cost risk mitigated by: derivatives hedging, fixed-price forward contracts, vertical integration
  • Temporary cost increases: Cut other costs (advertising) to preserve margins
  • Demand elasticity: Most demand is elastic; % ↓ in unit sales > % ↑ in price → total revenue ↓
  • First mover to raise prices → greater market share loss; laggard → declining margins
  • If full cost pass-through & units unchanged: operating profit $ unchanged but operating/gross margins ↓
  • Availability of substitutes = primary driver of elasticity
LOS 38.e: Forecast Horizon & Terminal Value
Choosing Forecast Horizon
  • Buy-side: Often = expected holding period (e.g., 25% annual turnover → 4-year avg holding)
  • Cyclical companies: Horizon must include midpoint of business cycle to capture normalized earnings
  • M&A, restructurings: Temporary effects; horizon long enough to see benefits realized
  • Manager-dictated horizon also common
  • Long-term forecast: Use historical average revenue growth rate over previous cycle
Terminal Value Methods
  • Price multiple approach: Use historical P/E; assumes future growth/required return same as past
  • DCF approach: Terminal value = PV of perpetuity of future earnings/CFs; use normalized (midcycle) earnings; small changes in perpetual growth rate → large TV changes
  • Inflection points: When future ≠ past (economic cycle change, regulation, technology disruption)
  • Normalized earnings: Expected midcycle earnings excluding temporary effects of current events/cyclicality
