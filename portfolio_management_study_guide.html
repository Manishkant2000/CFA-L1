Key Concepts

  • Risk ↑ ⟹ Return ↑ (risk-return tradeoff)
  • Real return ≈ Nominal return − Inflation
  • Returns are negatively skewed (downside tail)
  • Returns have excess kurtosis (fat tails, leptokurtic)

  • Liquidity concern: emerging markets, low-quality corporate bonds, infrequently traded securities
Non-normal distribution → mean/variance analysis is a simplification
LOS 83.b — Risk Aversion

Risk-Averse

  • Prefers less risk to more
  • Given equal E(R), chooses lower σ
  • Will hold risky assets if compensated
  • Standard assumption in finance

Risk-Neutral

  • Indifferent to risk level
  • Cares only about E(R)
  • Indifferent between gamble and certainty if E(R) equal

Risk-Seeking

  • Prefers more risk to less
  • Given equal E(R), chooses higher σ
  • Prefers gamble over certain equivalent
LOS 83.c — Optimal Portfolio, Indifference Curves & Capital Allocation Line

Indifference Curves

  • Utility function: represents risk/return preferences
  • Indifference curve: combinations of (σ, E(R)) giving same utility
  • Slopes upward for risk-averse investors
  • More risk-averse → steeper curves
  • Less risk-averse → flatter curves → optimal portfolio has more risk
  • Higher indifference curve (I₁ > I₂ > I₃) = higher utility
  • Risk aversion coefficient: higher = steeper curve

Two-Fund Separation Theorem

  • All investors hold combination of risk-free asset + optimal risky portfolio
  • Combining RF + risky portfolio → risk & return are linear
  • σ_portfolio = W_A × σ_A (when one asset is risk-free)

Capital Allocation Line (CAL)

  • Line from R_f through optimal risky portfolio
  • All points on CAL = combinations of R_f + risky portfolio
  • Optimal portfolio = tangency point of CAL with highest indifference curve
  • Less risk-averse → tangency point further right (more risky asset)
CAL Portfolio Expected Return
E(R_p) = W_A·E(R_A) + W_B·R_f
CAL Portfolio Std Dev (RF + Risky)
σ_p = W_A · σ_A
(since σ_RF=0, ρ(RF,risky)=0)
LOS 83.d — Mean, Variance, Covariance, Correlation (Historical Data)
Population Variance
σ² = Σ(Rₜ − μ)² / T
Sample Variance
s² = Σ(Rₜ − R̄)² / (T−1)
s = √s² (sample standard deviation)
Sample Covariance
Cov₁,₂ = Σ[(R_{t,1}−R̄₁)(R_{t,2}−R̄₂)] / (n−1)
Correlation Coefficient
ρ₁,₂ = Cov₁,₂ / (σ₁ · σ₂)
Range: −1 ≤ ρ ≤ +1
Covariance from Correlation
Cov₁,₂ = ρ₁,₂ · σ₁ · σ₂

Correlation Interpretation

  • ρ = +1: perfectly positively correlated
  • ρ = −1: perfectly negatively correlated
  • ρ = 0: uncorrelated — knowing one tells nothing about other
  • Covariance = 0 → correlation = 0 → no linear relationship
LOS 83.e — Portfolio Standard Deviation (2 Risky Assets)
Portfolio Variance (2 assets)
Var_p = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂Cov₁,₂
Portfolio Variance (with correlation)
Var_p = w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂
Portfolio Std Dev
σ_p = √(w₁²σ₁² + w₂²σ₂² + 2w₁w₂ρ₁₂σ₁σ₂)
Special Case: ρ = +1
σ_p = w₁σ₁ + w₂σ₂ (weighted average)

Effect of Correlation on Portfolio Risk

  • ρ = +1 → maximum portfolio risk (no diversification benefit)
  • ρ < +1 → portfolio risk < weighted average of individual risks
  • ρ = 0 → third term = 0; significant diversification benefit
  • ρ = −1 → maximum diversification; can eliminate risk entirely at specific weights
  • Lower ρ → greater diversification benefit
ρ = −1: zero-variance portfolio only at specific weights, not all weights
Portfolio risk < weighted avg risk whenever ρ < +1
LOS 83.f & 83.g — Efficient Frontier & Minimum-Variance Portfolios

Minimum-Variance Frontier

  • Minimum-variance portfolio: lowest σ for given E(R)
  • Minimum-variance frontier: set of all minimum-variance portfolios
  • Global minimum-variance portfolio (GMVP): the single portfolio with the lowest possible risk on the frontier (leftmost point)

Efficient Frontier

  • Efficient frontier: portfolios with highest E(R) for each level of σ
  • Efficient frontier = top portion of minimum-variance frontier
  • Risk-averse investors choose only portfolios on the efficient frontier
  • Portfolios below efficient frontier are dominated (inefficient)
  • Optimal portfolio = tangency of investor's indifference curve with efficient frontier
R84 Portfolio Risk & Return: Part II — CAL, CML, CAPM, SML, Beta, Performance Measures
LOS 84.a & 84.b — CAL, CML, Capital Market Line

Capital Allocation Line (CAL)

  • Line of possible risk/return combinations: RF + any risky portfolio
  • Best CAL = most preferred set of portfolios for investor
  • Optimal risky portfolio = CAL tangent to efficient frontier

Homogeneous Expectations Assumption

  • All investors have same estimates of E(R), σ, and ρ for all assets
  • → All investors face same efficient frontier
  • → All investors have same optimal risky portfolio
  • → That portfolio must be the market portfolio

Active vs Passive

  • Passive: invest in index (proxy for market portfolio) + RF asset
  • Active: overweight undervalued, underweight overvalued securities
Capital Market Line (CML) Equation
E(R_p) = R_f + [(E(R_M) − R_f) / σ_M] × σ_p
CML Slope = Sharpe Ratio of Market
Slope = [E(R_M) − R_f] / σ_M
Market risk premium = E(R_M) − R_f

CML Key Points

  • X-axis: total risk (σ_p)
  • Only efficient portfolios plot on CML
  • Y-intercept = R_f (zero risk)
  • Tangency point = market portfolio M
  • Left of M: lending portfolios (long RF + long M)
  • Right of M: borrowing portfolios (borrow at RF, invest >100% in M)
LOS 84.c — Systematic vs Unsystematic Risk

Risk Decomposition

Total Risk
Total Risk = Systematic Risk + Unsystematic Risk
  • Systematic risk = market risk = nondiversifiable risk
  • Caused by: GDP, interest rates, inflation, market-wide factors
  • Unsystematic risk = unique risk = diversifiable risk = firm-specific risk
  • Caused by: firm events, industry factors
  • ~12–30 stocks needed for 90% of diversification benefit
  • Beyond ~30 stocks: only systematic risk remains

Key Principle: What Gets Compensated

  • Investors are compensated only for systematic risk
  • Unsystematic risk can be eliminated free via diversification
  • → No compensation for bearing unsystematic risk in equilibrium
  • Expected equilibrium return depends only on systematic risk
  • High total risk stock (e.g., biotech) may have low systematic risk → low equilibrium return
Riskiest stock (by σ) ≠ highest expected return. Systematic risk is what matters.
LOS 84.d — Return Generating Models

Types of Factors

  • Macroeconomic factors: GDP growth, inflation, consumer confidence
  • Fundamental factors: earnings, earnings growth, firm size, R&D
  • Statistical factors: no theoretical basis; risk of data mining

Notable Multi-Factor Models

  • Fama-French (3-factor): market excess return + firm size + book-to-market ratio
  • Carhart (4-factor): Fama-French + price momentum (prior period returns)
General Multi-Factor Model
E(Rᵢ) − Rf = βᵢ₁·E(F₁) + βᵢ₂·E(F₂) + … + βᵢₖ·E(Fₖ)
Market Model (Single-Index)
Rᵢ = αᵢ + βᵢ·Rₘ + eᵢ
αᵢ = intercept · βᵢ = slope (beta)
eᵢ = abnormal return = Rᵢ − (αᵢ + βᵢRₘ)
Single-Factor (Excess Returns Form)
E(Rᵢ) − Rf = βᵢ × [E(Rₘ) − Rf]
Security characteristic line = regression of asset excess returns on market excess returns. Slope = Beta.
LOS 84.e — Beta
Beta (Primary Formula)
βᵢ = Cov(Rᵢ, Rₘ) / σ²ₘ
Beta (Using Correlation)
βᵢ = ρᵢₘ × (σᵢ / σₘ)
Beta (Using Covariance Definition)
Cov(Rᵢ,Rₘ) = ρᵢₘ·σᵢ·σₘ
∴ βᵢ = ρᵢₘ·σᵢ·σₘ / σ²ₘ = ρᵢₘ·(σᵢ/σₘ)

Beta Interpretation

  • β = 1: moves with market; same systematic risk as market
  • β > 1: more volatile than market (e.g., luxury goods)
  • β < 1: less volatile than market (e.g., utilities)
  • β = 0: uncorrelated with market returns
  • Theoretical average β of all stocks = 1 (market beta = 1)
  • Estimated by regressing asset returns on market index returns
  • Slope of security characteristic line = beta
LOS 84.f & 84.g — CAPM & Security Market Line (SML)
CAPM Equation
E(Rᵢ) = Rf + βᵢ[E(Rₘ) − Rf]
Market risk premium = E(Rₘ) − Rf
Risk premium for asset = βᵢ × market risk premium

CAPM Assumptions

  • Risk aversion — investors require higher E(R) for more risk
  • Utility maximizing investors
  • Frictionless markets — no taxes, no transaction costs
  • One-period horizon — all investors plan same time horizon
  • Homogeneous expectations — all have same estimates
  • Divisible assets — infinitely divisible
  • Competitive markets — price takers

SML Key Points

  • X-axis: beta (systematic risk)
  • ALL securities and portfolios plot on SML in equilibrium
  • (Including inefficient portfolios — unlike CML)
  • Y-intercept = R_f
  • Passes through market portfolio M (β=1, E(R)=E(Rₘ))

LOS 84.h — CAPM Applications: Identifying Mispriced Securities

Security Valuation vs SML

  • Above SML: Forecast return > Required return → Undervalued → BUY
  • Below SML: Forecast return < Required return → Overvalued → SHORT SELL
  • On SML: Forecast return = Required return → Properly valued
If estimated return plots OVER SML → security is UNDER-valued
If estimated return plots UNDER SML → security is OVER-valued

Required Return Calculation (CAPM)

Required R = Rf + β × (Rₘ − Rf)
Forecast / Expected Return
Forecast R = (P₁ − P₀ + Div) / P₀
  • If Forecast R > Required R → underpriced (buy)
  • If Forecast R < Required R → overpriced (short)
LOS 84.i — Performance Measures: Sharpe, Treynor, M², Jensen's Alpha
Sharpe Ratio
Sharpe = (R_p − R_f) / σ_p
= Excess return per unit of TOTAL risk
= Slope of CAL for that portfolio
Treynor Measure
Treynor = (R_p − R_f) / β_p
= Excess return per unit of SYSTEMATIC risk
= Slope of line from RF through portfolio on SML graph
M² (M-Squared / Modigliani-Modigliani)
M² = R_f + (σ_M / σ_p) × (R_p − R_f)
= SR_p × σ_M + R_f
M² alpha = M² − R_M
Jensen's Alpha
α_p = R_p − [R_f + β_p(R_M − R_f)]
= Actual return − CAPM required return
Positive α → portfolio above SML

When to Use Which Measure

  • Single manager / concentrated portfolio: Use Sharpe & M² (total risk)
  • Multiple managers / well-diversified fund: Use Treynor & Jensen's α (systematic risk)

Relationships

  • Sharpe & M² produce same rankings (M² is in % terms)
  • Treynor & Jensen's α produce same rankings
  • Portfolio above CML → Sharpe > market Sharpe → M² > Rₘ → M² alpha > 0
  • Portfolio above SML → Treynor > market Treynor → Jensen's α > 0
R85 Portfolio Management: An Overview — Portfolio Process, Investor Types, Pension Plans, Pooled Investments
LOS 85.a — Portfolio Approach to Investing

Portfolio Perspective

  • Evaluate investments by contribution to portfolio risk/return (not in isolation)
  • Modern portfolio theory: extra risk from single security NOT rewarded
  • Diversification: reduces risk without reducing expected return
  • Markowitz (1950s): framework for diversification; unless ρ = +1, diversification reduces risk
  • MPT (1960s): Treynor, Sharpe, Mossin, Lintner; equilibrium returns linear function of market risk

Diversification Ratio

Diversification Ratio = σ(equal-weighted portfolio) / σ(avg single security)
  • Lower ratio → greater risk reduction benefit
  • Ratio = 1 → no diversification benefit
  • Equal-weighted ≠ minimum-variance portfolio
  • Crisis periods: correlations ↑ → diversification benefits ↓
  • Computer optimization finds weights for lowest σ
LOS 85.b — Three Steps of Portfolio Management Process

Step 1: Planning

  • Analyze risk tolerance, return objectives
  • Time horizon, tax exposure
  • Liquidity needs, income needs
  • Unique circumstances/preferences
  • Output: IPS (Investment Policy Statement)
  • IPS includes objective benchmark
  • Update IPS every few years or when circumstances change significantly

Step 2: Execution

  • Analyze risk/return of asset classes
  • Determine asset allocation
  • Top-down analysis: macro conditions (GDP, inflation, rates) → identify attractive asset classes
  • Bottom-up analysis: security analysis → identify undervalued securities
  • Diversify across: cash, fixed income, equities, hedge funds, PE, real estate, commodities

Step 3: Feedback

  • Monitor changes in investor circumstances
  • Monitor risk/return characteristics of asset classes
  • Rebalance portfolio to target allocations
  • Measure portfolio performance vs benchmark
  • Adjust allocations as needed

Institutional Investor Types

LOS 85.d — Pension Plans

  • Defined Contribution (DC): firm contributes fixed sum each period; employee bears investment risk; no guaranteed benefit
  • Defined Benefit (DB): firm promises specific retirement payment; employer bears investment risk; poor performance → higher employer contributions
  • DB benefit formula: Final salary × 2% × Years of service
LOS 85.e — Asset Management Industry

Firm Types

  • Buy-side firms: asset managers (manage investments)
  • Sell-side firms: broker-dealers, investment banks
  • Full-service asset managers: variety of styles & asset classes
  • Specialist asset managers: focus on specific style or class
  • Multi-boutique firm: holding company of specialist managers
  • Traditional managers: equities + fixed income
  • Alternative managers: PE, hedge funds, real estate, commodities

Industry Trends

  • Active management: outperform benchmark via skill; higher fees
  • Passive management: replicate benchmark index; ≈ 1/5 of AUM; growing market share
  • Smart beta: passive with specific risk factor exposure
  • Robo-advisors: algorithm-based advice; appeal to younger investors
  • Passive fees lower; data explosion driving investment in IT
LOS 85.f — Pooled Investment Vehicles

Mutual Funds

  • NAV = (Total net assets) / (shares issued)
  • Open-end fund: buy/sell at NAV; no brokerage cost; daily redemption
  • Closed-end fund: fixed shares trade on exchange; price can differ from NAV; often actively managed
  • No-load funds: no purchase/redemption fees
  • Load funds: up-front or redemption fees

Fund Types

  • Money market funds: short-term debt; NAV typically $1; low risk
  • Bond mutual funds: fixed income; differentiated by maturity, credit, issuer
  • Index funds: passively managed; match index (e.g., S&P 500)
  • Actively managed funds: higher fees; higher turnover; greater tax liability

R86 Basics of Portfolio Planning & Construction — IPS, Objectives, Constraints, Asset Allocation, ESG
LOS 86.a & 86.b — IPS Purpose & Components

Why IPS?

  • IPS = starting point of portfolio management process
  • Forces investment discipline; ensures goals are realistic
  • Requires investor to articulate circumstances, objectives, constraints
  • Mnemonic: R-R-T-T-L-L-U (Risk, Return, Time horizon, Tax, Liquidity, Legal, Unique)

IPS Major Components

  • Description of client
  • Purpose of IPS
  • Duties & responsibilities (manager, custodian, client)
  • Procedures to update IPS
  • Investment objectives
  • Investment constraints
  • Investment guidelines (asset types, leverage)
  • Performance evaluation / benchmark
  • Appendices (strategic allocation, rebalancing policy)
LOS 86.c — Risk & Return Objectives

Risk Objectives

  • Absolute risk objective: e.g., "no loss >10% in any 12-month period"
  • Can be stated as probability: "no >5% probability of return <−5%"
  • Relative risk objective: relative to benchmark, e.g., "within 2% of MSCI return"
  • High returns + low risk are often mutually exclusive

Return Objectives

  • Absolute return: "overall return of at least 6% per annum" (nominal or real)
  • Relative return: "exceed S&P 500 by 2% per year"
  • Peer benchmarks are not investable → problematic
  • Risk and return objectives must be compatible
  • Return objective = NOT a constraint (it's an objective)
LOS 86.d — Ability vs Willingness to Take Risk

Two Components of Risk Tolerance

  • Ability (Capacity): financial circumstances (wealth, liabilities, income stability, time horizon, insurance). Objective.
  • Willingness: attitudes and beliefs about investments. Subjective; assessed via questionnaire.
Overall risk tolerance = lower of ability and willingness

Resolution of Conflicts

  • Ability & willingness compatible → no problem
  • Willingness > Ability → use ability (low); educate investor
  • Ability > Willingness → conform to willingness; educate investor (but don't change personality)
  • Adviser should NOT construct portfolio with risk level client is clearly uncomfortable with
LOS 86.e — Investment Constraints (R-R-T-T-L-L-U)

Liquidity

  • Ability to convert to cash quickly without price concession
  • Tuition, medical, living expenses → need liquid assets
  • P&C insurers need high liquidity (unpredictable claims)
  • Hedge funds, PE → illiquid; not suitable for near-term needs
  • High liquidity needs → high allocation to bonds/cash

Time Horizon

  • Longer horizon → more risk & less liquidity acceptable
  • Short horizon → government securities, bank CDs (low risk, high liquidity)
  • 20-year horizon → equities acceptable
  • Near-term large purchase → conservative investment only

Tax Situation

  • Higher tax rate → prefer tax-free bonds or capital gains
  • Capital gains often taxed at lower rate than income
  • Tax-exempt/deferred accounts: put taxable-income securities there
  • Personal accounts: seek capital gains & tax-exempt income
  • Focus on after-tax returns

Legal & Regulatory

  • Trust, corporate, qualified accounts: restrictions on security types
  • Restrictions on percentage allocations
  • Corporate officers: legal restrictions on trading own firm's securities
  • Fiduciary rules may apply

Unique Circumstances

  • Responsible investing: ethical preferences (no tobacco, no firearms)
  • Human rights restrictions
  • Religious preferences (no interest-bearing)
  • Diversification needs: don't invest in competitor of founder's firm
  • Concentrated position constraints

Key IPS Reminders

  • Return objective = NOT a constraint
  • Permitted asset types & leverage = Investment guidelines (not objectives)
  • Update IPS: regular intervals + when client objectives/constraints change
  • Do NOT update based on recent portfolio performance
LOS 86.f — Asset Class Specification

Rules for Asset Classes

  • Correlations within asset class: relatively HIGH (assets similar)
  • Correlations between asset classes: relatively LOW (diversification benefit)
  • Traditional: equities, bonds, cash, real estate
  • Alternative investments: hedge funds, PE, commodities, artwork, IP rights
  • Equities subdivided: domestic/foreign, large/small, emerging/developed
  • Bonds subdivided: maturity, domestic/foreign, govt/corporate, IG/HY
  • Asset classes should match IPS permissible investments

LOS 86.g — Portfolio Construction Principles

  • Use efficient frontier of asset classes to identify optimal allocation
  • IPS risk/return objectives + efficient frontier → strategic asset allocation
  • Strategic asset allocation: baseline percentage allocations
  • Tactical asset allocation: deviations from strategic to exploit short-term opportunities
  • Security selection: deviations from index weights within asset class
  • Risk budgeting: set overall risk limit; allocate portions to strategic allocation risk, tactical allocation risk, security selection risk
  • Core-satellite approach: majority in passive index (core) + small active portion (satellite)
LOS 86.h — ESG Integration

ESG Approaches

  • Negative screening: exclude companies/industries based on ESG
  • Positive screening: invest in companies with good ESG practices
  • Thematic investing: select sectors to promote ESG goals
  • Impact investing: return + promote positive ESG practices
  • Engagement/active ownership: use share ownership to improve ESG
  • ESG integration: consider ESG factors throughout asset allocation & security selection

ESG Performance Considerations

  • ESG constraints → affect portfolio performance (uncertain direction)
  • Negative screening → use ESG-appropriate benchmark, not broad index
  • Good corporate governance → may increase returns
  • Limiting universe → may decrease returns
  • Active ownership: clarify whether investors vote or direct manager to vote
  • Positive/thematic/impact: customized portfolios & benchmarks required
R87 Behavioral Biases of Individuals — Cognitive Errors, Emotional Biases, Market Implications
LOS 87.a — Cognitive Errors vs Emotional Biases

Cognitive Errors cognitive

  • Due to faulty reasoning, irrationality, information processing errors, memory errors
  • Can arise from: statistical misunderstanding, illogical reasoning
  • Can be reduced by: increased awareness, better training, more information
  • 2 subtypes: Belief Perseverance + Information Processing

Emotional Biases emotional

  • Stem from feelings, impulses, intuition — not conscious thought
  • Difficult to overcome; may need to be accommodated
  • Spontaneous reactions; harder to correct
  • If bias has both elements: focus on cognitive aspects for success
  • Overconfidence: more emotional than cognitive
LOS 87.b — Cognitive Errors: Belief Perseverance belief perseverance

1. Conservatism Bias

  • Rationally forms initial view but fails to update as new information arrives
  • Overweights prior probabilities; slow to react to new data
  • May ignore complex-to-process information
  • Result: hold investments too long; avoid mental effort of updating

2. Confirmation Bias

  • Seek information that supports prior beliefs
  • Avoid/diminish conflicting information
  • Distort new information to remain consistent with prior beliefs
  • Results: ignore negative info; overconfident; incorrect data screens
  • Remedy: actively seek contrary views

3. Representativeness Bias

  • Classify investment by superficial characteristics and assume it shares all properties of that category
  • Base-rate neglect: ignore population probabilities; focus on surface characteristics
  • Sample-size neglect: classify based on small, unrepresentative sample
  • Result: attach too much importance to few characteristics

4. Illusion of Control Bias

  • Believe they can control outcomes when they cannot
  • Associated with: illusion of knowledge, self-attribution, overconfidence
  • Result: overweight securities they feel they control → poor diversification

5. Hindsight Bias

  • Selective memory: remember correct predictions; forget incorrect ones
  • "I-knew-it-all-along" phenomenon
  • View past events as more predictable than they were
  • View outcomes that occurred as inevitable
  • Result: overconfidence in ability to predict; discard valid techniques that didn't work by chance

Cognitive Dissonance

  • Holding conflicting beliefs or receiving belief-challenging information
  • Causes stress → discount conflicting info (easier than updating)
  • Root of many belief perseverance biases
LOS 87.b — Cognitive Errors: Information Processing processing errors

6. Anchoring & Adjustment Bias

  • Base expectations on an initial number (anchor) and insufficiently adjust for new information
  • Examples: security value relative to current price; EPS relative to prior estimate
  • Result: underestimate implications of new information
  • Remedy: consider new data without regard to anchor

7. Mental Accounting Bias

  • View money from different accounts/sources differently
  • Conflicts with: decisions should be made based on total portfolio context
  • E.g., "found money" bonus → risky speculation; inheritance → safe bonds
  • Common form: treat income differently from capital appreciation
  • Result: suboptimal portfolio; offsetting positions; wrong income mix

8. Framing Bias

  • Decisions affected by how question/data is presented
  • Same problem framed as gain vs loss → different choices
  • Loss aversion: fear losses more than value gains
  • Gain frame → risk-averse choice; Loss frame → risk-seeking choice
  • Result: overly conservative portfolios (overestimate short-term volatility)
  • Investment managers must avoid framing bias in risk tolerance questionnaires

9. Availability Bias

  • Undue emphasis on easily recalled or available information
  • Recent events easier to recall → overweight recent, underweight historical
  • Assume easily remembered = more probable
  • Results: choose manager/investment based on advertising; limit to familiar firms; overreact to recent market conditions; too much weight on media-covered events
LOS 87.b — Emotional Biases emotional

1. Loss-Aversion Bias (Kahneman & Tversky)

  • Feel more pain from loss than pleasure from equal gain
  • Decisions made relative to a reference rate
  • Below reference = loss; above = gain
  • When facing gains: prefer certainty (risk-averse)
  • When facing losses: prefer risk (risk-seeking) to avoid loss
  • Asymmetrical response to gains and losses
  • Results: sell winners too soon (small gains); hold losers too long; take excessive risk hoping to recover from losses
  • Distinction: Risk aversion = prefer lower σ given equal E(R). Loss aversion = more pain from losses than gains.

2. Overconfidence Bias

  • Overestimate own intuitive ability or reasoning
  • Illusion of knowledge: think they predict better than they do
  • Self-attribution bias: credit self for success (self-enhancing); blame others for failure (self-protecting)
  • Prediction overconfidence: underestimate uncertainty (too narrow confidence intervals)
  • Certainty overconfidence: overstate probability of being right
  • Fueled by: good results + self-attribution + hindsight bias
  • More emotional than cognitive (difficult to correct)
  • Results: underestimate risk; overestimate return; poor diversification; overtrading

3. Self-Control Bias

  • Lack self-discipline; favor short-term over long-term goals
  • Hyperbolic discounting: small payoffs now vs larger payoffs later
  • Results: insufficient retirement savings → excessive risk to compensate; overemphasis on income-producing assets
  • Mitigation: establish investment plan (asset allocation) + budget; review regularly

4. Status Quo Bias

  • Comfort with existing situation → resistant to change
  • Default choices (opt-out) → status quo bias increases participation
  • Similar to regret-aversion
  • Results: hold portfolios with inappropriate risk; fail to consider better alternatives

5. Endowment Bias

  • Value owned assets more than if not owned
  • Assets feel "special" simply because they are owned
  • Common with inherited assets
  • Detection question: "Would you buy this today with new money?"
  • WTA (sell price) > WTP (buy price) for same asset → endowment effect
  • Results: hold inappropriate assets; don't sell when no longer suitable

6. Regret-Aversion Bias

  • Do nothing out of excessive fear of being wrong
  • Overweight errors of commission (did something wrong)
  • Underweight errors of omission (didn't do something that would have worked)
  • Herding behavior: follow consensus/popular opinion (not my fault if everyone was wrong)
  • Results: excess conservatism; avoid riskier assets to escape regret if they decline
LOS 87.c — Behavioral Biases & Market Anomalies

Market Anomalies & Explanations

  • Anomalies: results that don't fit prevailing risk/return models
  • Many explained by: small sample size, time period bias, model misspecification
  • Value/growth anomaly: value stocks (low P/B, low P/E, high div yield) outperform growth stocks
  • Fama-French (1992): adding size & book-to-market factors eliminated value outperformance → not an anomaly
  • Halo effect: version of representativeness — company's growth/price rise → assumed to be good stock → overvaluation of growth stocks
  • Home bias: overweight domestic or regional firms; less-than-rational diversification; due to information belief or emotional preference

Bubbles & Crashes — Behavioral Explanations

  • Behavioral finance does NOT provide complete explanation for bubbles
  • Overconfidence → overtrading, underestimate risk, poor diversification
  • Self-attribution + hindsight bias → fuels overconfidence in bull markets
  • Confirmation bias → ignore negative signals; misread initial declines as buying opportunity
  • Anchoring → believe recent highs are rational even after decline begins
  • Fear of regret → keep skeptical investors in market too long
R88 Introduction to Risk Management — Framework, Governance, Risk Types, Measurement, Modification
LOS 88.a — Definition of Risk Management

Risk Management Process

  • Identify organization's risk tolerance
  • Identify and measure risks faced
  • Modify and monitor these risks
  • NOT about minimizing/eliminating all risk
  • May increase exposure to risks firm can manage well
  • Aligns risk exposures with risk tolerances
  • Returns above RF earned by taking risk
  • Returns not controllable; risk levels are
  • Goal: optimal bundle of risks for organization
LOS 88.b — Risk Management Framework

Seven Elements of Framework

  1. Establishing processes and policies for risk governance
  2. Determining the organization's risk tolerance
  3. Identifying and measuring existing risks
  4. Managing and mitigating risks to achieve optimal bundle
  5. Monitoring risk exposures over time
  6. Communicating across the organization
  7. Performing strategic risk analysis

LOS 88.c & 88.d — Risk Governance & Tolerance

  • Risk governance: senior management's determination of risk tolerance, optimal risk exposure strategy, and oversight framework
  • Should be at enterprise level (not business unit or individual)
  • Risk management committee: bring up risk measurement issues, integration, mitigation of undesirable risks
  • Factors determining risk tolerance: expertise in business lines, ability to respond to negative events, regulatory environment, financial strength
LOS 88.e — Risk Budgeting

Risk Budgeting

  • Allocating firm resources to assets/investments by risk characteristics
  • Goal: allocate acceptable risk to mix with greatest expected returns
  • Single metric: portfolio beta, VaR, portfolio duration, returns variance
  • By investment categories: domestic equities, domestic debt, international equities, international debt
  • By risk factors: interest rate risk, equity market risk, FX rate risk
  • Risk budget set via risk governance; specific allocations = risk budgeting
LOS 88.f — Financial & Non-Financial Sources of Risk

Financial Risks from financial markets

  • Credit risk: counterparty may not fulfill contractual obligations
  • Liquidity risk: forced sale at price below fair value
  • Market risk: uncertainty about asset prices (stocks, commodities, currencies) and interest rates

Non-Financial Risks from operations / external

  • Operational risk: human error, faulty processes, inadequate security, business interruptions; includes cyber risk
  • Solvency risk: run out of cash; unable to continue operations
  • Regulatory risk: regulatory environment changes, imposing costs
  • Governmental/political risk: tax increases, political actions
  • Legal risk: exposure to future legal action
  • Model risk: asset valuations based on incorrect analytical models
  • Tail risk: extreme events more likely than analysis indicates; fat tails
  • Accounting risk: accounting policies/estimates judged incorrect

Individual Risk Types

  • Mortality risk: dying before providing for family → addressed with life insurance
  • Longevity risk: living longer than assets last → addressed with lifetime annuity
  • Health care expense risk → health insurance

Risk Interactions

  • Risks are NOT independent; they interact frequently
  • Example: market risk → credit risk → legal risk → liquidity risk → solvency risk (cascade)
  • Especially important during periods of financial market stress
  • Must consider interactions, not each risk in isolation
LOS 88.g — Measuring & Modifying Risk Exposures

Risk Measurement Tools

  • Standard deviation (σ): volatility of prices and rates; not ideal for non-normal distributions (negative skew, fat tails)
  • Beta: market risk of equities; considers diversification; use for securities in well-diversified portfolio
  • Duration: price sensitivity of debt securities to interest rate changes

Derivatives Risk ("The Greeks")

  • Delta (Δ): sensitivity of derivative value to price of underlying
  • Gamma (Γ): sensitivity of delta to changes in price of underlying
  • Vega (ν): sensitivity of derivative value to volatility of underlying price
  • Rho (ρ): sensitivity of derivative value to risk-free rate
Value at Risk (VaR)
VaR = minimum loss over a period that will occur with a specific probability
Example: 1-month VaR of $1M at 5% → loss of at least $1M expected 5% of months
VaR does NOT give maximum loss
Used for bank minimum capital requirements
Conditional VaR (CVaR)
CVaR = E[Loss | Loss ≥ VaR]
= Probability-weighted average of all losses ≥ VaR
Analogous to "loss given default" in credit

Supplementary Risk Assessment Methods

  • Stress testing: effect of a specific (usually extreme) change in ONE key variable (interest rate, FX rate)
  • Scenario analysis: what-if analysis with multiple simultaneous input changes (e.g., rate change + oil price change)
  • Rare event risk: subjective estimate (not data-driven)
  • Market-based estimates: use cost of hedging (insurance, derivatives) as proxy for market's estimate of risk
Risk Modification Methods

Avoid / Prevent

  • Not engage in activity with uncertain outcome
  • E.g., avoid political risk → don't invest in that country
  • Prevent: improve data security to prevent breach
  • Comes from top management risk tolerance decisions

Accept / Self-Insure / Diversify

  • Self-insurance: organization decides to bear the risk
  • May establish reserve account to cover losses
  • Diversification: more efficient way to bear specific risk
  • Note: self-insurance = may be inaction, not strategic

Transfer / Shift

  • Risk transfer: another party takes on risk → insurance is risk transfer
  • Surety bond: insurer pays if third party fails to perform under contract
  • Fidelity bond: covers losses from employee theft/misconduct
  • Risk shifting: change distribution of possible outcomes → primarily via derivative contracts
  • E.g., buy put options → floor on stock value
  • Sell call options → give up upside; reduce downside by premium received
  • FX forward/futures/swaps → eliminate currency risk
Criterion for choosing method: always compare costs and benefits of risk modification. Use multiple methods if appropriate. Goal: risk profile matching organization's risk tolerance.
