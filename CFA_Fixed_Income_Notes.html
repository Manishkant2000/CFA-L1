R47 FIXED-INCOME INSTRUMENT FEATURES
LOS 47.a: Features of a Fixed-Income Security
Core Features (Memorize All)
  • Issuer: Sovereign govts, corporations, local govts, supranational entities (IMF), quasi-govt, special purpose entities (SPEs → ABS)
  • Maturity date: Date of final cash flow | Tenor: Remaining time to maturity
  • Money market securities: Original maturity ≤1 year
  • Capital market securities: Original maturity >1 year
  • Perpetual bonds: No stated maturity date
  • Par / Face / Principal value: Amount repaid (usually at maturity)
  • Coupon rate: Annual % of par paid as interest; can be annual/semi/quarterly/monthly
  • Floating-rate notes (FRNs/floaters): Coupon = MRR + fixed margin (in basis points)
  • MRR (Market Reference Rate): e.g., SOFR
  • Zero-coupon / Pure discount bonds: No coupon; sold at discount to par
  • Seniority: Senior debt ranks above junior (subordinated) debt
  • Contingency provisions: Embedded options (call, put, convertible)
Yield & Yield Curve
  • Yield: Expected return from investing in bond; inversely related to price
  • Inverse price-yield relationship: ↑ price → ↓ yield; ↓ price → ↑ yield (for fixed-coupon bonds)
  • Yield curve: Plot of yields vs. maturity for same issuer/class
  • Normal (upward-sloping): Higher yields for longer maturities — most common
  • Inverted (downward-sloping): Less common; may signal recession expectations
  • Spread: Extra yield over benchmark (e.g., govt bond); measures credit risk of riskier issuers
  • Basis points (bps): 1/100th of 1% (0.01%)
LOS 47.b: Bond Indenture & Covenants
Bond Indenture
  • Bond indenture (trust deed): Legal contract between issuer and bondholders defining obligations, restrictions, sources of repayment
  • Secured bond: Backed by collateral (lien/pledge on specific assets)
  • Unsecured bond: General claim on issuer's cash flows only
  • ABS: Repaid from cash flows of assets held by SPE
  • Sovereign bonds: Repaid from taxation powers
  • Local govt bonds: From local taxes or infrastructure revenue
Covenants
  • Affirmative covenants: Actions issuer MUST take (reports, use of proceeds, right to redeem at premium if merger)
  • Cross-default clause: Default on ANY debt → default on THIS bond
  • Pari passu clause: Same priority claims as other senior debt
  • Negative covenants: Actions issuer is RESTRICTED from (asset sales, additional pledges, senior debt issuance, extra borrowing/buybacks/dividends)
  • Negative pledge clause: Cannot issue debt more senior than existing
  • Incurrence test: Actions only if financial ratios meet threshold (e.g., debt/EBITDA below X)
R48 FIXED-INCOME CASH FLOWS AND TYPES
LOS 48.a: Cash Flow Structures & Contingency Provisions
Principal Repayment Structures
  • Bullet structure: Coupon payments + full principal at maturity
  • Fully amortizing: Equal periodic payments of interest + principal; principal fully repaid by final payment. Calculator: N, I/Y, PV, FV=0, CPT PMT
  • Partially amortizing: Some principal repaid periodically + balloon payment at maturity (remaining principal)
  • Sinking fund provision: Trustee redeems portions of principal at intervals; reduces credit risk but creates reinvestment risk for bondholders
  • Waterfall structure: ABS/MBS; junior tranches don't receive principal until senior paid in full
Coupon Structures
  • Fixed coupon: Set % of par
  • FRN (Floater): Coupon = MRR + quoted margin; coupon set at start of period, paid in arrears
  • Step-up coupon: Increases on predetermined schedule
  • Credit-linked note: Coupon rises if issuer credit rating falls (and vice versa); same for leveraged loans with debt/EBITDA threshold
  • Payment-in-kind (PIK): Coupon paid by increasing principal (more bonds); high-leverage issuers
  • Green bond: Coupon rises if environmental goals not met
  • Index-linked / Inflation-linked (linkers): Cash flows tied to inflation index
  • Zero-coupon bond: Single payment at maturity; sold at discount; minimizes reinvestment risk
  • Deferred coupon bond: No coupons for a period, then regular coupons begin
Inflation-Indexed Bond Types
  • Interest-indexed: Coupon rate adjusted for inflation; principal unchanged (principal NOT protected)
  • Capital-indexed (TIPS): Coupon rate fixed; principal adjusted for inflation → coupon payment = real rate × adjusted principal. In deflation: TIPS pay max(inflation-adjusted or original par)
  • Capital-indexed coupon rate = real rate of interest
Embedded Options
  • Straight / option-free bond: No embedded options
  • Callable bond: Issuer can redeem before maturity at call price; creates call risk for bondholder. Call price limits bond upside → callable bond < straight bond price
  • Call protection period: No call before this date
  • Putable bond: Bondholder can sell back at put price; valuable to investor → putable bond > straight bond price
  • Convertible bond: Bondholder can convert to shares; lower yield; conversion price, conversion ratio, conversion value
  • Conversion ratio = Par / Conversion price
  • Conversion value = Conversion ratio × Share price
  • Warrants: Right to buy shares at fixed price; detachable and tradable
  • CoCos (Contingent Convertibles): Auto-convert to equity if bank capital falls below required level
LOS 48.b: Legal, Regulatory & Tax Considerations
Bond Classification by Market
  • Domestic bonds: Issuer's home country and currency
  • Foreign bonds: Foreign issuer, trades in different domestic market
  • Eurobonds: Issued outside any single country's jurisdiction; any currency; less regulated; initially bearer bonds, now mostly registered
  • Eurodollar bonds: Eurobonds denominated in USD
  • Euroyen bonds: Eurobonds denominated in JPY
  • Global bonds: Trade in Eurobond market AND ≥1 domestic market
  • International bonds: Collective term for foreign, global, Eurobonds
  • Sukuk: Sharia-compliant bonds; payments = rent on underlying assets
Taxation
  • Coupon interest: Taxed as ordinary income
  • Capital gains: From selling bond above purchase price; often taxed at lower rate; long-term gains taxed at lowest rate
  • OID bonds (Original Issue Discount): Sold at significant discount to par; discount treated as taxable interest income each year, even without cash payment
  • Municipal bonds (US): Interest usually exempt from national income tax (and state tax in issuer's state)
  • Premium bonds: Some jurisdictions allow portion of premium to offset taxable coupon
FRN Example Calculation
FRN coupon per period = (MRR + QM) / periodicity × par

Example: MRR = 2.3% annually, QM = 0.75%, quarterly pay:
Quarterly coupon = (2.3% + 0.75%) / 4 = 0.7625% × par
Amortizing Loan (Calculator)
Fully amortizing: N, I/Y, PV=loan, FV=0 → CPT PMT
Partially amortizing: N, I/Y, PV=loan, FV=balloon → CPT PMT
R49 FIXED-INCOME ISSUANCE AND TRADING
Market Classification
  • By issuer: Sovereign govt, corporate, SPE (ABS)
  • By credit quality: Investment Grade Baa3/BBB− and above | High Yield Ba1/BB+ and below ("junk")
  • By maturity: Short-term/money market ≤1yr | Intermediate 1–10yr | Long-term >10yr
  • Fallen angels: Previously IG bonds downgraded to HY
  • Aggregate indexes: Broad selection across all sectors (e.g., Bloomberg Barclays Aggregate)
Investor Positioning
  • Pension funds / insurance: Long-term IG (to match long-term liabilities); prohibited from HY by regulation
  • Corporations: Short-term IG (commercial paper, repos, ABCP)
  • Central banks: Intermediate-term Treasury notes (monetary policy)
  • Bond funds / ETFs: Intermediate IG (excluding Treasuries)
  • Asset managers (higher return): High-yield intermediate; distressed debt funds
  • Banks / financial intermediaries: Treasuries across all maturities
Primary & Secondary Markets
  • Primary market: New bonds issued; issuer receives capital
  • Public offering: Registered with regulators; sold to public
  • Private placement: Sold to select investors only
  • Underwritten offering: Price guaranteed by investment bank
  • Best-efforts offering: No price guarantee; bank earns commission
  • Shelf registration: Register total value upfront; issue over time as needed
  • Debut issuer: First-time bond issuer; requires roadshows
  • Secondary market: Trading of existing bonds; primarily OTC dealer market
  • Bid/ask spread: Narrower for liquid bonds; wider for less liquid / older / smaller issues
  • On-the-run bonds: Most recently issued; most actively traded
  • Distressed debt: Bonds of issuers in or near bankruptcy
  • Fixed-income indexes vs equity: More constituents, higher turnover, larger govt weights
R50 FIXED-INCOME MARKETS FOR CORPORATE ISSUERS
LOS 50.a: Short-Term Funding Alternatives
Non-Financial Corporations — Lines of Credit
  • Uncommitted line: Bank can refuse; no commitment fee; least reliable
  • Committed (regular) line: Bank commits for specific period; ~50 bps commitment fee on full or unused amount; renewable; renewal risk
  • Revolving (operating) line: Most reliable; longer term; restrictive covenants; similar fees to committed
  • Secured loans: Backed by collateral (fixed assets, receivables, inventory); weaker credit issuers
  • Factoring: Transfer/sale of receivables to lender ("factor") at a discount; factor assumes collection risk
  • Commercial paper (CP): Short-term unsecured debt for large IG firms; lower cost than bank loan; typically <3 months; bridge financing
  • Rollover risk: Risk cannot sell new CP to replace maturing CP; managed with backup liquidity lines
  • Eurocommercial paper (ECP): International CP market
Financial Institutions — Short-Term Funding
  • Demand deposits (checking): No interest; immediate availability
  • Operational deposits: Cash management/custody/clearing services
  • Savings deposits / CDs: Stated term and interest; nonnegotiable (penalty for early withdrawal) or negotiable (sellable)
  • Interbank funds: Bank-to-bank loans, 1 day to 1 year, secured or unsecured; rate based on MRR
  • SOFR: Secured Overnight Funding Rate (US MRR)
  • Central bank funds market: Banks with excess reserves lend to banks that need funds at central bank funds rate; lender of last resort = "discount window"
  • ABCP (Asset-Backed Commercial Paper): SPE buys bank loans → SPE sells ABCP to investors; bank provides backup credit line
LOS 50.b: Repurchase Agreements (Repos)
Repo Structure & Key Terms
  • Repo: Sell security today + commit to repurchase at higher price later; security seller = BORROWER of cash; security buyer = LENDER of cash
  • Repo rate: Annualized interest implied by price difference
  • Overnight repo: 1-day term | Term repo: Longer period
  • Initial margin: Collateral value required as % of loan (e.g., 103%)
  • Haircut: Discount from collateral market value to get loan amount = 1 − (1/initial margin)
  • Variation margin: Extra collateral if collateral value falls below required threshold
  • General collateral repo: Any qualifying securities as collateral
  • Special trade: Specific security specified (e.g., short selling)
  • Reverse repo: From perspective of party wanting to borrow the security (buyer in the repo)
  • Bilateral repo: Directly between 2 parties
  • Tri-party repo: Third-party custodian manages; reduces operational risk (not credit risk)
Repo Formulas & Uses
Purchase price (loan amount) = Market value of collateral / Initial margin

Repurchase price = Purchase price × [1 + Repo rate × (days/360)]

Haircut = 1 − (1 / Initial margin)

Variation margin = Loan value × Initial margin − Current collateral value
Repo Uses
  • Banks: Finance securities trading positions
  • Institutional investors: Earn repo rate on excess short-term funds
  • Central banks: Monetary policy (buy = expand money supply; sell = contract)
  • Short sellers (hedge funds): Borrow specific securities to short sell
Repo Rate Higher When:
  • Alternative money market rates are higher | Longer term | Lower quality collateral | Undercollateralized | Specific security hard to source → rate may be negative
Repo Risks
  • Default risk | Collateral risk | Margining risk | Legal risk | Netting and settlement risk

R51 FIXED-INCOME MARKETS FOR GOVERNMENT ISSUERS
Sovereign Debt
  • Backed by taxing power; highest credit quality in domestic market
  • Developed market: Stable diversified economies; transparent fiscal policy; debt in reserve currency (USD, CNY, EUR, etc.)
  • Emerging market: Faster growing, less stable; concentrated economies; tied to commodity/industry
  • Domestic debt: In home currency; held by domestic investors; may not be freely convertible
  • External debt: Owed to foreign creditors; may be in reserve currency → foreign investor avoids direct currency risk but faces indirect risk (EM govt must earn reserve currency to repay)
  • Ricardian equivalence: Under idealized assumptions, govt should be indifferent among debt maturities. In practice: must manage short-term vs. long-term split to avoid excess rollover risk
  • Benefits of liquid govt yield curve: benchmark for spreads, interest rate risk hedging, collateral for repos, central bank monetary policy
Non-Sovereign Government Debt
  • Agency / quasi-government bonds: Issued by govt-created entities (e.g., Ginnie Mae US); often govt-backed → similar credit ratings to sovereign
  • General obligation (GO) bonds: Backed by full taxing power of issuing entity
  • Revenue bonds: Backed by fees/revenues from specific project (toll road, airport); higher credit risk than GO bonds
  • Supranational bonds: World Bank, IMF, Asian Development Bank; high credit quality; supports by member govts
  • Unlike sovereigns, regional govts cannot use monetary policy to repay debt
  • Debt-service coverage ratio: Revenue after operating costs / Interest + principal payments (for revenue bonds)
Government Debt Auctions
  • Competitive bids: Used to set price; not guaranteed allocation
  • Noncompetitive bids: Guaranteed allocation at auction price
  • Single-price auction: All pay at cut-off yield (lowest price accepted); reduces yield volatility; broader distribution
  • Multiple-price auction: Each successful bidder pays their own bid price
  • Cut-off yield: Yield of last accepted competitive bid
  • Primary dealers: Required to make competitive bids, submit on behalf of others, serve as central bank counterparty
  • On-the-run bonds: Most recently issued; most actively traded; yield used as benchmark
R52 FIXED-INCOME BOND VALUATION: PRICES AND YIELDS
LOS 52.a: Bond Pricing & YTM
Bond Pricing Formulas
Annual coupon bond:
PV = PMT/(1+YTM) + PMT/(1+YTM)² + ... + (PMT+FV)/(1+YTM)^N

Calculator: N=years, I/Y=YTM, PMT=annual coupon, FV=100, CPT→PV

Semiannual coupon bond:
N=2×years, I/Y=YTM/2, PMT=coupon/2, FV=100, CPT→PV

Stated YTM (semiannual) = periodic return × 2
Price-YTM Relationships
  • Coupon = YTM → Price = Par
  • Coupon > YTM → Price > Par (premium)
  • Coupon < YTM → Price < Par (discount)
  • Price-yield relationship is convex: price increase (from ↓yield) > price decrease (from ↑yield) of same magnitude
  • Pull to par: Bond price converges to par as maturity approaches (regardless of yield)
  • Constant-yield price trajectory: Path of bond price over time if YTM unchanged
Accrued Interest, Flat & Full Price
Accrued Interest = Coupon × (Days since last coupon / Days in coupon period)

Full price (dirty/invoice price) = PV on last coupon date × (1 + periodic yield)^(t/T)
where t = days since last coupon, T = days in coupon period

Flat price (clean/quoted price) = Full price − Accrued interest
  • Actual/actual convention: Govt bonds; actual days in numerator and denominator
  • 30/360 convention: Corporate bonds; assumes 30-day months, 360-day year
  • Bonds are quoted as flat price; transaction settles at full price
To Earn YTM, Investor Must:
  • Hold bond to maturity AND issuer makes all payments AND reinvest coupons at same YTM
LOS 52.b & 52.c: Price Sensitivity & Matrix Pricing
Price Sensitivity Rules
  • ↑ YTM → ↓ price (inverse relationship)
  • Lower coupon rate → higher price sensitivity to yield changes
  • Longer maturity → higher price sensitivity
  • Price increase (from ↓yield) > price decrease (from ↑yield) — CONVEXITY
Matrix Pricing
  • Method to estimate YTM for infrequently traded bonds using traded bonds of same credit quality
  • Use linear interpolation if maturities differ: YTM_target = YTM_short + [(target−short)/(long−short)] × (YTM_long−YTM_short)
  • For new issues: estimate required yield spread above benchmark by observing spreads on existing similar bonds
R53 YIELD AND YIELD SPREAD MEASURES FOR FIXED-RATE BONDS
LOS 53.a: Yield Measures
Effective Annual Yield (EAY)
EAY = (1 + YTM/n)^n − 1
where n = periodicity (compounding periods/year)
YTM for Semiannual Bond
Stated YTM = 2 × (semiannual periodic rate)
N=2×yrs, I/Y=?, PMT=coupon/2, FV=100, PV=−price → I/Y×2
Current Yield (Income / Running Yield)
Current Yield = Annual coupon / Flat price
(ignores capital gain/loss and reinvestment income)
Simple Yield
Simple Yield = [Annual coupon ± (discount or premium)/N] / Flat price
(straight-line amortization of discount/premium)
Yield to Call (YTC)
Calculator: N=periods to call, FV=call price, PMT=coupon, PV=−price → I/Y×2
Yield to Worst (YTW)
YTW = min(YTM, all YTCs)
Option-Adjusted Yield
Yield callable bond would offer if it were option-free
= YTM of callable bond − extra yield for option
Street convention: Yield using stated coupon dates | True yield: Using actual payment dates (accounts for weekends/holidays); slightly lower
Periodicity relationship: For same EAY, higher periodicity → lower stated YTM
LOS 53.b: Yield Spread Measures
Spread Types
  • Benchmark spread / Yield spread: Bond YTM − Benchmark YTM
  • G-spread (Government spread): Spread over govt bond yield (same or interpolated maturity)
  • I-spread (Interpolated spread): Spread over swap rate (MRR-based); common for EUR bonds
  • Z-spread (Zero-volatility spread): Constant spread added to each spot rate on benchmark curve such that PV of cash flows = market price; accounts for curve shape; more precise than G-spread
  • OAS (Option-Adjusted Spread): Z-spread with option removed; represents spread for credit, liquidity, and taxation risks only; allows comparison across bonds with/without options
Spread Relationships
Z-spread = OAS + Option value (in bps)
OAS = Z-spread − Option value

For callable bond: Z-spread > OAS (call option increases yield required)
Option value = Z-spread − OAS
  • ↑ yield spread with unchanged benchmark → microeconomic (credit/liquidity risk) factors
  • ↑ yield spread with same spread increase → macroeconomic factors (benchmark moved)
  • Macro factors: Affect benchmark (real risk-free rate + inflation premium)
  • Micro factors: Affect credit and liquidity premiums (spread component)
  • "Option-adjusted" = option REMOVED; compare option-free equivalent
R54 YIELD MEASURES FOR FLOATING-RATE & MONEY MARKET INSTRUMENTS
LOS 54.a: Floating-Rate Note (FRN) Yields
  • Quoted margin (QM): Fixed margin actually paid in coupon
  • Discount margin (DM) / Required margin: Margin required by market to price FRN at par
  • At issuance: QM = DM → FRN trades at par
  • If issuer credit deteriorates → DM > QM → FRN trades at discount
  • If issuer credit improves → DM < QM → FRN trades at premium
  • Interest paid in arrears: Coupon set at start, paid at end of period
FRN Valuation:
N = periods remaining, I/Y = (MRR + DM)/periodicity
PMT = (MRR + QM)/periodicity × par
FV = par, CPT → PV
LOS 54.b: Money Market Yields
  • Discount yield: Annualized discount from face value (T-bills, US CP); based on 360-day year
  • Add-on yield: Interest earned on amount invested (CDs, repos, MRRs); can be 360 or 365-day
  • Bond Equivalent Yield (BEY): Add-on yield on a 365-day basis (allows comparison with bonds)
Discount yield: Quoted = actual discount × 360/days

Add-on yield (365): Quoted = HPY × 365/days

T-bill price = Face × (1 − quoted discount × days/360)
HPY(T-bill) = (Face − Price)/Price

BEY of discount instrument:
1. Find price from discount yield
2. HPY = (Face − Price)/Price
3. BEY = HPY × 365/days

Semiannual bond equivalent: 2 × [(1+HPY)^(183/days) − 1]
R55 THE TERM STRUCTURE: SPOT, PAR, AND FORWARD CURVES
LOS 55.a: Spot Rates & Bond Pricing
Spot Rates
  • Spot rate: Market discount rate for a single future payment (= zero-coupon rate = zero rate)
  • Spot curve / Zero curve / Strip curve: Spot rates vs. maturity
  • Normal: upward sloping | Inverted: downward sloping
  • No-arbitrage price: Bond priced using spot rates; if different, arbitrage opportunity exists
Bond price using spot rates:
PV = C₁/(1+S₁) + C₂/(1+S₂)² + ... + (Cₙ+FV)/(1+Sₙ)^N

YTM = single discount rate ≈ weighted average of spot rates
Par Rates
  • Par yield: Coupon rate for a hypothetical bond to trade at par, given spot curve; interpreted as weighted average of spot rates
  • Par curve: Par yields vs. maturity; constructed from spot curves to avoid illiquidity/taxation distortions
Par bond: Solve for PMT (coupon) such that:
PMT/(1+S₁) + PMT/(1+S₂)² + (PMT+100)/(1+S₃)³ = 100
LOS 55.b: Forward Rates
Forward Rate Notation & Formulas
  • Forward rate notation: "ayb" = rate for b-year loan starting a years from now (e.g., 2y1y = 1-yr rate 2 yrs from now)
Key No-Arbitrage Relations:
(1+S₂)² = (1+S₁)(1+1y1y)
(1+S₃)³ = (1+S₁)(1+1y1y)(1+2y1y)

Solve for forward rate:
1y1y = [(1+S₂)²/(1+S₁)] − 1
2y1y = [(1+S₃)³/(1+S₂)²] − 1

Multi-period forward:
2y2y: (1+S₄)⁴ = (1+S₂)²×(1+2y2y)²
→ 2y2y = [(1+S₄)⁴/(1+S₂)²]^(1/2) − 1

Spot from forwards:
S₃ = [(1+S₁)(1+1y1y)(1+2y1y)]^(1/3) − 1

Approximation: Sₙ ≈ simple average of forward rates

Bond pricing using forwards:
PV = C₁/(1+S₁) + C₂/[(1+S₁)(1+1y1y)] + C₃/[(1+S₁)(1+1y1y)(1+2y1y)]
LOS 55.c: Comparing Yield Curves
CurveWhat It ShowsKey Property
Spot curveSpot rates vs. maturity; derived from zero-coupon bondsFoundation for pricing
Par curveCoupon rates for par bonds; constructed from spotsHypothetical; avoids illiquidity distortions
Forward curveForward rates for future periods (e.g., 1-yr rates each year)Drives spot and par curves
Upward-sloping forward curve: forward rates > spot rates > par yields
Downward-sloping forward curve: forward rates < spot rates < par yields
Flat yield curve: forward rates = spot rates = par yields for all maturities
Forward rates DRIVE spot rates, which DRIVE par yields
R56 INTEREST RATE RISK AND RETURN
LOS 56.a: Sources of Return
3 Sources of Return
  • Coupon and principal payments
  • Interest earned on reinvested coupons
  • Capital gain or loss if sold before maturity
Key Results
  • Hold to maturity, YTM unchanged → return = YTM at purchase
  • Sell before maturity, YTM unchanged → return = YTM at purchase
  • Hold to maturity, YTM ↑ after purchase → return > original YTM (more reinvestment income)
  • Hold to maturity, YTM ↓ after purchase → return < original YTM (less reinvestment income)
  • Short horizon, YTM ↑ → return < YTM (price drops more than reinvestment helps)
  • Short horizon, YTM ↓ → return > YTM (price rises more than reinvestment hurts)
Price Risk vs. Reinvestment Risk
  • Price risk: Uncertainty about bond's sale price due to yield changes
  • Reinvestment risk: Uncertainty about income from reinvesting coupons
  • ↑ YTM → ↑ reinvestment income, ↓ bond price
  • ↓ YTM → ↓ reinvestment income, ↑ bond price
  • Short horizon: Price risk > reinvestment risk
  • Long horizon: Reinvestment risk > price risk
  • Investment horizon: Point where price risk and reinvestment risk exactly offset = Macaulay duration
Horizon yield = [(End value / Purchase price)^(1/n)] − 1
End value = coupons + reinvestment income + sale price (or par)
Carrying Value
  • Value of bond at same YTM as purchase; reflects amortization of discount/premium; capital gain/loss measured vs. carrying value
LOS 56.b & 56.c: Macaulay Duration
Macaulay Duration Definition
  • Weighted average number of years until cash flows received, weighted by PV of each cash flow as % of bond's full price
  • Investment horizon where price risk = reinvestment risk (bond earns original YTM regardless of yield change)
  • For semiannual bond: divide result by 2 to annualize
MacDur = Σ[t × (PV of CF_t / Full price)]

where t = time in years until each cash flow
Duration Gap
Duration gap = Macaulay duration − Investment horizon
  • Positive duration gap (MacDur > horizon): exposed to price risk from ↑ rates
  • Negative duration gap (MacDur < horizon): exposed to reinvestment risk from ↓ rates
  • Zero duration gap: price risk and reinvestment risk exactly offset → earn original YTM
R57 YIELD-BASED BOND DURATION MEASURES AND PROPERTIES
LOS 57.a: Modified Duration, Money Duration, PVBP
Modified Duration (ModDur)
ModDur = MacDur / (1 + YTM/periodicity)
Annual: ModDur = MacDur / (1 + YTM)
Semiannual: ModDurSEMI = MacDurSEMI / (1 + YTM/2)
Price Change Estimate
%ΔP ≈ −ModDur × ΔYTM
(ΔYTM in decimal form, e.g., 0.01 for 1%)
Approximate ModDur
ApproxModDur = (V− − V+) / (2 × V₀ × ΔYTM)
V− = price if YTM decreases by ΔYTM
V+ = price if YTM increases by ΔYTM
ΔYTM in decimal
Money Duration (Dollar Duration)
Money Dur = Annual ModDur × Full price of position
ΔValue = Money Dur × ΔYTM (decimal)
Price Value of a Basis Point (PVBP)
PVBP = (V− − V+) / 2 × par × 0.01
Also: PVBP = Money Duration × 0.0001
(change in bond value per 1 bp YTM change)
ModDur is a LINEAR approximation; actual price-yield relationship is CONVEX → duration alone overestimates price decrease and underestimates price increase for large yield moves

R58 YIELD-BASED BOND CONVEXITY AND PORTFOLIO PROPERTIES
LOS 58.a: Convexity
Convexity Calculation
Convexity of single cash flow at period t:
Conv_t = t(t+1) / (1+r)²
where r = periodic yield

Bond convexity = weighted average of individual CF convexities
(same weights as Macaulay duration)

Annualize for non-annual bonds: divide by periodicity²

Approximate Convexity:
ApproxConvex = (V+ + V− − 2V₀) / (V₀ × ΔYTM²)
ΔYTM in decimal
  • Convexity increases with: longer maturity, lower coupon, lower YTM
  • For same duration, bond with more dispersed cash flows has more convexity
  • All option-free bonds have positive convexity
LOS 58.b: Price Change with Duration + Convexity
%ΔPrice ≈ −ModDur × ΔYTM + ½ × Convexity × (ΔYTM)²

(Duration effect) + (Convexity adjustment)
Convexity adjustment always POSITIVE (for positive convexity)
Money Convexity = Annual Convexity × Full price of position

ΔValue ≈ −(MoneyDur × ΔYTM) + ½ × MoneyCon × (ΔYTM)²
LOS 58.c: Portfolio Duration & Convexity
  • Method 1 (theoretically correct): Single duration/convexity from aggregate portfolio cash flows
  • Method 2 (used in practice): Weighted average of individual bond durations/convexities
  • Limitation: Method 2 assumes parallel shift in yield curve (all maturities change by same amount) — rarely happens in practice
  • Non-parallel shifts (steepening, twisting) require key rate durations
R59 CURVE-BASED AND EMPIRICAL FIXED-INCOME RISK MEASURES
LOS 59.a & 59.b: Effective Duration & Convexity
  • For bonds with embedded options: cash flows uncertain → cannot use YTM-based measures
  • Effective duration: Based on shifts in BENCHMARK CURVE (not bond's own YTM)
EffDur = (V− − V+) / (2 × V₀ × ΔCurve)

EffCon = (V+ + V− − 2V₀) / (V₀ × ΔCurve²)

%ΔPrice ≈ −EffDur × ΔCurve + ½ × EffCon × (ΔCurve)²
  • Callable bond: At low yields, call limits price appreciation → negative convexity; EffDur < straight bond equivalent at low yields
  • Putable bond: Always positive convexity; EffDur < straight bond at high yields (put limits price decline)
  • ModDur vs. EffDur: slightly different even for option-free bonds (nonparallel spot rate shifts when par curve shifts)
  • EffDur isolates benchmark yield sensitivity; holds spread constant
LOS 59.c: Key Rate Duration
  • Key rate duration (partial duration): Price sensitivity to change in yield at ONE specific maturity while other yields unchanged
  • Useful for measuring shaping risk = impact of nonparallel yield curve shifts
  • Sum of all key rate durations = effective duration of the bond/portfolio
Key rate duration_t = ModDur × weight_t

Portfolio impact = −Σ(key rate dur_t × Δyield_t)
LOS 59.d: Empirical vs. Analytical Duration
  • Analytical duration: Mathematically derived (Macaulay, modified, effective)
  • Empirical duration: Estimated from historical relationship between benchmark yield changes and bond price changes
  • Analytical assumes spreads and benchmark yields are uncorrelated
  • Flight to quality: Govt yields ↓, credit spreads ↑ simultaneously → corporate bond prices don't rise as much as analytical duration suggests → empirical duration < analytical duration for corporate bonds
  • Empirical duration more appropriate for credit-risky portfolios; analytical appropriate for govt portfolios

R60 CREDIT RISK
LOS 60.a: Credit Risk Components
The Cs of Credit Analysis
Bottom-Up (Issuer-Specific)
  • Capacity: Ability to make debt payments
  • Capital: Other resources reducing reliance on debt
  • Collateral: Pledged assets securing lender
  • Covenants: Legal terms in bond indenture
  • Character: Management integrity and commitment
Top-Down (Economic)
  • Conditions: General economic environment
  • Country: Geopolitical/legal/political system
  • Currency: FX impact on ability to service foreign debt
Key Distinctions
  • Illiquid: Cannot raise cash to service debt (may still be solvent)
  • Insolvent: Assets < value of debts
  • Cross-default clause: Default on any obligation = default on all
  • Pari passu clause: All bonds of same type rank equally
Measuring Credit Risk — Formulas
Expected Loss = Probability of Default (POD) × Loss Given Default (LGD)

LGD% = Expected Exposure × (1 − Recovery Rate)

Credit spread ≈ POD × LGD%

Loss severity = 1 − Recovery rate

Price change from spread change:
%ΔP ≈ −ModDur × ΔSpread + ½ × Convexity × (ΔSpread)²
Credit Quality Indicators
  • High credit quality: High EBIT margin, high EBIT/interest (interest coverage), low debt/EBITDA, high CFO/net debt
  • IG debt: Primary concern = probability of default increasing; not imminent default
  • HY debt: Primary concern = probability of default AND loss given default (often has secured collateral)
LOS 60.b: Credit Ratings

Rating Limitations: (1) Lag market pricing — spreads react faster | (2) Cannot capture all risks (litigation, natural disasters, fraud) | (3) Not always accurate (e.g., subprime crisis 2008) | (4) Split ratings possible from different agencies
LOS 60.c: Factors Affecting Yield Spreads
Macroeconomic Factors
  • Expansion → ↓ spreads (lower default probability) | Recession → ↑ spreads
  • HY spreads more volatile than IG spreads over cycle
  • Credit curves usually upward sloping (↑ spread with longer maturity)
  • Recession: credit curves flatten/invert (near-term default risk ↑)
  • Expansion: credit curves steepen/fall
  • Flight to quality: investors sell HY, buy govt → HY spreads widen dramatically; bid-offer spreads for HY widen more
Issuer-Specific & Market Factors
  • Financial performance vs. industry average spread
  • Market liquidity risk: Assessed via bid-offer spread; wider = less liquid = higher liquidity risk
  • More debt outstanding + higher rating → narrower bid-offer → lower liquidity risk
  • Other factors: ↑ broker-dealer regulations, funding stresses, heavy new issuance
Yield Spread Decomposition
  • Total yield spread = credit spread + liquidity spread
  • Liquidity spread ≈ (yield at bid) − (yield at offer)
  • Credit spread = total spread − liquidity spread
High-Yield Investment Incentives
  • Diversification: Low/negative correlation with IG bonds
  • Capital appreciation: Larger spread changes → bigger price gains during recovery
  • Equity-like returns: Higher yield, lower volatility than equity (empirically)
R61 CREDIT ANALYSIS FOR GOVERNMENT ISSUERS
Sovereign Creditworthiness — 5 Qualitative Factors
  • 1. Institutions and policy: Political stability, legal property rights, data transparency, willingness to repay (sovereign immunity = no legal recourse if refuses)
  • 2. Fiscal flexibility: Ability to ↑ taxes or ↓ spending to ensure debt service
  • 3. Monetary effectiveness: Independent central bank; ability to manage money supply/rates credibly
  • 4. Economic flexibility: GDP growth trends, income per capita, economic diversification
  • 5. External status: Currency reserve status; ability to issue debt to foreign investors; geopolitical stability
Sovereign Creditworthiness — 3 Quantitative Factors
  • 1. Fiscal strength: Low debt/GDP, low debt/revenue, low interest/GDP (debt affordability)
  • 2. Economic growth & stability: High real GDP growth, large economy size, high per-capita GDP, low GDP growth volatility
  • 3. External stability: High FX reserves/GDP, high FX reserves/external debt, low LT external debt/GDP, low near-term external debt/GDP
Non-Sovereign Government Debt Types
  • Agencies: Govt-created; implied govt support → similar ratings to sovereign
  • Govt sector banks: Specific mission (e.g., green bonds); similar to agencies
  • Supranational: World Bank, IMF, etc.; backed by member govts
  • GO bonds: Backed by full taxing power; lower credit risk
  • Revenue bonds: Backed by project cash flows only; higher credit risk
  • Key: Sovereigns CAN print money; regional govts CANNOT
  • Revenue bonds analyzed like corporate bonds: debt-service coverage ratio (DSC) = (net revenue) / (interest + principal)
R62 CREDIT ANALYSIS FOR CORPORATE ISSUERS
LOS 62.a: Qualitative & Quantitative Factors
Qualitative Factors
  • Business model: Stable, predictable cash flows = high quality
  • Industry competition: Less competition = higher quality
  • Business risk: Low unexpected deviation from expected revenues
  • Corporate governance: Fair treatment of debtholders; debt covenants; accounting policies
  • Warning signs: aggressive revenue recognition, off-balance-sheet financing, frequent auditor/CFO changes, capitalizing vs. expensing
Quantitative Factors — Key Definitions
  • EBITDA: Operating income + D&A (doesn't adjust for capex or working capital changes)
  • CFO (Cash Flow from Operations): Net income + non-cash charges − Δworking capital
  • FFO (Funds from Operations): Net income from cont. ops + D&A + deferred taxes + non-cash; excludes Δworking capital
  • FCF (Free Cash Flow): CFO − capex + net interest expense
  • RCF (Retained Cash Flow): Operating cash flow − dividends

R63 FIXED-INCOME SECURITIZATION
Securitization Process
  • Step 1: Originator (bank/corporation) creates pool of debt-based assets (collateral)
  • Step 2: Pool sold to SPE (Special Purpose Entity) / SPV / Trust
  • Step 3: SPE issues ABS (Asset-Backed Securities) to investors; cash flows from underlying assets fund payments
  • Seller/depositor: Original originator who sells assets to SPE
  • Issuer/trust: The SPE that buys assets and issues ABS
  • Servicer: Collects payments, handles delinquencies, repossessions; often same as seller
  • Trustee: Oversees safekeeping of collateral and cash flows (disinterested)
  • Bankruptcy remote: SPE's assets protected from originator's financial problems; ABS investors have no claim on originator's other assets
Benefits & Risks
Benefits to Originator (Issuer)
  • ↑ Business activity (re-lend proceeds)
  • ↑ Profitability (origination and arrangement fees)
  • ↓ Required capital reserves (credit risk off balance sheet)
  • ↑ Liquidity (illiquid loans → liquid ABS proceeds)
Benefits to Investors
  • Tailored risk/return profiles
  • Access to collateral without origination expertise
  • Greater liquidity than underlying collateral
Benefits to Economy
  • ↓ Liquidity risk (ABS more liquid than collateral)
  • ↑ Market efficiency (equilibrium pricing)
  • ↓ Financing costs for originators; ↓ leverage
Risks to ABS Investors
  • Uncertain timing/size of cash flows (e.g., prepayments)
  • Credit risk of underlying collateral (systemic risk in 2007–2009)
R64 ABS INSTRUMENT AND MARKET FEATURES
LOS 64.a: Covered Bonds vs. ABS
Covered Bonds
  • Senior debt of financial institutions; cover pool remains ON balance sheet (no SPE)
  • Issued primarily by European, Asian, Australian banks
  • Dual recourse: Claims on cover pool AND issuer's unencumbered assets
  • Overcollateralization: Cover pool value > face value of covered bonds; LTV limits on underlying mortgages
  • Third-party monitoring ensures adherence
  • Dynamic cover pool: issuer must replace prepaid/defaulted assets
  • No credit tranching typically
  • Lower yield than comparable ABS (due to dual recourse + credit enhancements)
  • No capital reserve reduction (assets stay on balance sheet)
  • Hard-bullet: Default if payment missed → acceleration | Soft-bullet: Can postpone maturity up to 1 year | Conditional pass-through: Converts to pass-through on maturity date
LOS 64.b: Internal Credit Enhancements of ABS
  • Overcollateralization: Collateral value > ABS face value; absorbs losses up to over-collateral amount
  • Excess spread: Income earned on collateral > coupon paid to ABS investors; builds reserves to absorb losses
  • Credit tranching / Subordination: Multiple bond classes with different priority claims
  • Junior (equity) tranche absorbs losses first → protects senior tranches
  • More subordinated bonds → more protection for senior tranche → higher senior rating
  • Waterfall structure: Losses flow from junior to senior (overflow analogy)
  • All tranches receive coupon (interest); losses reduce face value of junior tranches first
LOS 64.c: Non-Mortgage ABS Types
Credit Card ABS
  • Backed by non-amortizing credit card receivables
  • Lockout/revolving period: Investors receive only interest/fees; principal payments used to buy new receivables (no prepayment risk during this period)
  • After lockout: amortization period; principal passed through
  • Early amortization provision: Faster principal repayment if credit quality deteriorates
Solar ABS
  • Backed by loans for residential solar panel installation
  • Secured on solar system or junior mortgage on property
  • Attractive to ESG investors
  • Pre-funding period: Trust can make investments for fixed period after ABS issuance → larger, more diversified pool
  • Relatively new; not yet tested through full credit cycle
LOS 64.d: Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs)
CDO Structure
  • CDO: Structured security; collateral = pool of debt obligations; has a collateral manager who actively buys/sells (unlike static ABS pools)
  • CBO (Collateralized Bond Obligations): Collateral = corporate and emerging market bonds
  • CLO (Collateralized Loan Obligations): Collateral = leveraged bank loans (most common post-2008)
  • CDOs have subordinated tranches similar to ABS
  • Equity tranche must offer attractive return after accounting for required yields on senior tranches
CLO Types & Controls
  • Cash flow CLOs: Payments from underlying collateral cash flows
  • Market value CLOs: Payments from trading/market value of collateral
  • Synthetic CLOs: Collateral exposure via credit derivatives (trust doesn't own collateral)
CLO Collateral Tests
  • Coverage of payment obligations by collateral cash flows
  • Overcollateralization levels per tranche
  • Diversification requirements
  • Limits on CCC-rated debt in pool
  • Breach → redirect cash flows to purchase collateral or repay senior tranches
R65 MORTGAGE-BACKED SECURITY (MBS) INSTRUMENT AND MARKET FEATURES
LOS 65.a: Prepayment Risk & Time Tranching
Prepayment Risk Definitions
  • Prepayment: Principal repaid faster than scheduled on amortizing loans
  • Prepayment risk: Uncertainty about timing of principal cash flows
  • Contraction risk: Prepayments faster than expected (↑ when rates ↓) → cash flows sooner; reinvestment at lower rates; MBS price doesn't rise as much as option-free bonds (negative convexity like callable bonds)
  • Extension risk: Prepayments slower than expected (↑ when rates ↑) → cash flows later; discounted at higher rates
  • Both are bad for MBS investors
  • Time tranching: Multiple CMO tranches with different maturities redistributing prepayment risk; first tranche = most protection from extension risk; last tranche = most protection from contraction risk
LOS 65.b: Residential Mortgage Features
  • Lender has first lien on property; can foreclose if default
  • LTV (Loan-to-Value): Loan / Property value; lower LTV = less default risk
  • DTI (Debt-to-Income): Monthly debt payments / Monthly pretax gross income; lower = less default risk
  • Prime loans: Good credit, low LTV, low DTI
  • Subprime loans: Lower credit quality, higher DTI, higher LTV, or junior lien
  • Prepayment penalties: Fee for early principal repayment; common in Europe, rare in US
  • Recourse loans: Lender can claim borrower's other assets (Europe: mostly recourse)
  • Nonrecourse loans: Only property as collateral → strategic default more likely when underwater (US: mostly nonrecourse varies by state)
  • Agency RMBS: Govt or GSE guaranteed; min underwriting standards required
  • Non-agency RMBS: Private entities; no govt guarantee; credit enhancements used; catalyst for 2007–2009 crisis
LOS 65.c: RMBS Types
Mortgage Pass-Through Securities
  • Pro-rata claim on pool of mortgages; investors receive monthly cash flows net of fees
  • WAC (Weighted Average Coupon): Weighted average of mortgage interest rates (by outstanding principal balance)
  • WAM (Weighted Average Maturity): Weighted average of final maturities
  • Pass-through rate (net coupon): Coupon paid to MBS investors = WAC − servicing/guarantee fees
WAM = Σ(maturity_i × balance_i) / Σbalances
WAC = Σ(coupon_i × balance_i) / Σbalances
CMO (Collateralized Mortgage Obligations) Tranches
  • Sequential pay CMO: Principal (scheduled + prepayments) flows to Tranche 1 first until paid off, then Tranche 2, etc. First tranche: most contraction risk protection; last tranche: most extension risk protection
  • Z-tranche (accrual/accretion bond): No interest payments during accrual period; interest accrues as extra principal; lowest ranking; payments begin after accrual period ends
  • PO (Principal-Only) securities: Only principal; ↓ rates + ↑ prepayments = higher return (repaid sooner) → effectively zero-coupon
  • IO (Interest-Only) securities: Only interest; ↓ rates + ↑ prepayments = lower return (fewer coupon payments)
  • Floating-rate tranches: Coupon linked to MRR; may include cap and floor; inverse floaters also possible
  • Residual tranches: Junior to all; equity-like
  • PAC (Planned Amortization Class) tranches: Predictable principal payments as long as prepayment speeds within predetermined range (PAC band)
  • Support tranches: Absorb excess prepayments (contraction risk) or defer principal (extension risk) to protect PAC tranches; most prepayment risk
LOS 65.d: Commercial MBS (CMBS)
CMBS Features
  • Backed by income-producing commercial real estate (apartments, industrial, offices, retail, hotels, healthcare)
  • Fewer mortgages than RMBS → less diversification
  • Analysis focuses on property credit risk, not borrower credit risk
  • US: fixed coupons | Europe: floating coupons (and growing)
  • WAMP (Weighted Average Mortgage Proceeds): Regular income from collateral; plays role of WAC for RMBS
  • Commercial mortgages usually nonrecourse (only property as collateral)
  • Balloon risk: Commercial mortgages often not fully amortized → balloon payment at maturity; if borrower cannot refinance → default → extension risk for CMBS investors
CMBS Analysis Ratios & Call Protection
DSCR (Debt Service Coverage Ratio) = Net Operating Income / Debt service cost
Higher DSCR = lower credit risk

LTV = Loan amount / Current property value
Lower LTV = lower credit risk

Net Operating Income = Rental income − Operating expenses − Noncash replacement reserve
Loan-Level Call Protection Methods
  • Prepayment lockout: Prohibited from prepaying for specific period (2–5 years)
  • Prepayment penalty points: % fee on prepaid principal (1 point = 1%)
  • Defeasance: Borrower purchases govt securities portfolio sufficient to make remaining loan payments; removes lender's lien → allows property sale without prepayment
  • CMBS-level call protection: sequential pay tranching (same as CMO)
