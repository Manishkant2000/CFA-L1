Key Terms — LOS 20.a
  • Sole proprietorship: Business IS the owner — unlimited liability, profits taxed as personal income.
  • General partnership: Written/verbal/incidental agreement; ALL partners have unlimited liability.
  • Limited partnership: GPs manage + unlimited liability; LPs contribute capital + limited liability (to investment). LPs typically cannot appoint/remove GPs.
  • LLP (Limited Liability Partnership): All partners are limited partners; in the US, restricted to professional services (law, accounting); restrictions on # of partners and equity amount.
  • Corporation (limited company): Legally separate from owners; shareholders have limited liability; can only lose investment value.
Double Taxation Formula
Effective tax rate on profits paid as dividends: = Corp tax rate + [Personal div rate × (1 − Corp rate)] Example: Corp tax 10%, div tax 15%: ETR = 0.10 + 0.15 × (1−0.10) = 23.5% Alternative: Start with $100 pretax Corp tax = $100 × 10% = $10 After-tax income = $90 Div tax = $90 × 15% = $13.50 Total tax = $23.50 → ETR = 23.5% Double taxation is REDUCED when companies pay a SMALLER fraction of profits as dividends.
LOS 20.b: Describe key features of corporate issuers.
  • Corporation formed by filing articles of incorporation with a regulatory body → becomes a separate legal entity.
  • As a legal entity: can hire, contract, borrow, own property — same rights/responsibilities as an individual.
  • Issues shares to raise large capital; shareholders have voting rights to elect the board of directors.
  • Board may pay dividends (distribution of earnings to shareholders).
  • Shares are easily transferable if traded on an exchange.
  • Double taxation: Corporate profits taxed at corporate level; dividends taxed again at personal level.
LOS 20.c: Compare publicly and privately owned corporate issuers.
Public Companies
  • Listed companies: Shares listed on stock exchange — price/volume transparency.
  • Free float: Actively traded shares (not held by insiders/strategic investors) — expressed as % of total outstanding shares.
  • Subject to compliance: quarterly/annual financial reports, material change disclosures.
Ways to Go Public (Private → Public)
  • IPO (Initial Public Offering): New shares issued; must meet exchange requirements; investment bank underwrites.
  • Direct Listing: Exchange lists existing shares; no new capital raised, no underwriter, faster than IPO.
  • SPAC (Special Purpose Acquisition Company): "Blank check" company; raises capital via IPO → held in trust → must acquire a private company within specified time.
Private Companies
  • Shares do NOT trade on exchange → value not readily observable.
  • Transfer between investors is difficult.
  • Fewer regulatory requirements; less disclosure than public companies.
  • Can take a longer-term view of business.
  • Raise equity via private placements — restricted to accredited investors (institutional, corporate, or high net worth individuals).
  • Going private: Acquirer buys all outstanding shares → company delisted → lower regulatory burden, cost savings.
Corporations have HIGHEST separation of owners and operators. SPAC = "blank check" company — acquired target doesn't need to be identified at time of IPO.

Leverage Impact on ROE Formula
ROE = Net Income / Equity 100% Equity: ROE = EBIT / Equity 50/50 D/E: ROE = (EBIT − Interest) / Equity Key insight: If return on assets > cost of debt → leverage INCREASES ROE for equity holders. If revenues fall below break-even → leveraged firm ROE drops faster (financial risk). Firm Value = Value of Debt + Value of Equity (as firm value rises above debt value, equity rises but debt value stays constant)
Debt has NO upside — best case is receiving promised principal + interest. Equity has unlimited upside. This difference creates conflicts of interest between debtholders and equity holders.
  • Covenants: Contractual provisions in debt agreements limiting borrower actions (e.g., max leverage, min interest coverage ratio). Protect debtholders from actions that increase risk (e.g., issuing more debt, increasing dividends).
LOS 21.b: Describe a company's stakeholder groups and compare their interests.
Stakeholder Theory vs. Shareholder Theory
  • Shareholder theory: Primary focus = maximize market value of common equity. Concerned with manager-owner conflict only.
  • Stakeholder theory: Broader — balance interests of ALL groups with a stake in the firm.
Primary Stakeholders
  • Shareholders: Residual interest; voting rights; interest in growth and profitability.
  • Lenders: Public (bondholders) + private (banks). Private debtholders may have nonpublic info; bondholders rely on public info only.
  • Board of Directors: Protects shareholders; hires/fires/compensates senior managers; sets strategic direction; monitors financial performance.
  • Inside directors: Senior executives, founders — may have conflicts with shareholders.
  • Independent directors: No material relationship with company — better protect shareholders.
  • One-tier board: Inside + independent on single board (US/UK).
  • Two-tier board: Independent directors on supervisory board; inside directors on management board (continental Europe).
  • Staggered board: Only fraction elected each year → decreases shareholders' ability to overhaul board; provides continuity.
  • Senior managers: Salary + performance bonus + perks. Incentive to maximize compensation and employment continuity.
  • Employees: Labor/skills; interest in pay, advancement, working conditions. May hold equity via ESOPs (Employee Stock Ownership Plans).
  • Suppliers: Short-term creditors; interest in ongoing relationship and firm stability.
  • Customers: High-quality goods/services at reasonable price; after-sale support; increasingly care about ESG.
  • Government/Regulators: Tax revenue, employment, regulatory compliance.
LOS 21.c: Describe environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors considered by investors.
Why ESG Matters (3 Reasons)
  1. Governments increasingly prioritize climate/social policies via regulation.
  2. ESG factors can materially impact cash flows (fines, customer goodwill loss, poor governance enables self-dealing).
  3. Younger investors increasingly manage wealth with ESG in mind.

Negative externalities: When company doesn't bear full cost of its actions (e.g., environmental damage). Regulations force recognition of these costs.

Environmental Factors
  • Climate change, air/water pollution, deforestation, energy efficiency, waste management, water scarcity.
  • Physical risk: Adverse effects on assets from severe weather.
  • Transition risk: Regulations/consumer choices requiring shift from high-carbon to low-carbon.
  • Stranded assets: Assets becoming unviable due to regulatory or market changes.
Social Factors
  • Customer privacy, information security, customer satisfaction, employee engagement, diversity & inclusion, labor relations, community relations.
  • Reducing social risk → higher employee productivity, lower turnover, increased customer loyalty, less litigation risk.
Governance Factors
  • Board composition, internal audit committee, executive compensation, bribery/corruption, political contributions, lobbying.
  • Good governance = managers act ethically, lawfully, in shareholders' interests.
ESG Risk Evaluation
  • Equity investors bear the brunt of adverse ESG outcomes.
  • Debt investors exposed only if ESG problems cause default.
  • Longer-maturity debt investors have more ESG exposure than short-term investors (delayed risks).
Reading 22
Corporate Governance: Conflicts, Mechanisms, Risks & Benefits
LOS 22.a: Describe the principal-agent relationship and conflicts that may arise.
Principal-Agent Framework
  • Principal-agent relationship: One party (principal) hires another (agent) to act in their interest. Conflict arises when agent's interests ≠ principal's interests.
  • Agency costs: Costs of principal-agent conflict. Direct: hiring monitors. Indirect: opportunity cost of lost business.
  • In corporations: shareholders = principals; managers & directors = agents.
  • Information asymmetry: Managers have more/better information than shareholders → harder to monitor. More acute in: large firms operating in many markets, complex products, low institutional ownership/free float.
Common Manager-Shareholder Conflicts
  • Insufficient effort: Poor investment evaluation → higher costs.
  • Risk manipulation: Option-compensated managers may take excessive risk (options have no downside); cash-compensated managers may take too little risk.
  • Empire building: Compensation tied to company size → unnecessary acquisitions.
  • Entrenchment: Taking inadequate risk, mimicking competitors, engaging in projects requiring their specific knowledge. Directors may rubber-stamp management.
  • Self-dealing: Exploiting firm resources for personal benefit.
Other Conflicts
  • Controlling vs. minority shareholders: Controlling shareholders may force diversification to reduce their own concentrated risk → minority shareholders (who are diversified) suffer.
  • Dual-class structure: Different share classes with different voting rights → founders keep control with <50% economic claims. CFA Institute advocates against dual-class voting.
  • Creditors vs. shareholders: Shareholders may prefer more risk (more upside); creditors prefer less risk (fixed return). Actions that increase default risk (issue more debt, increase dividends) harm creditors.
Dual-class structures allow one group to further their interests at the expense of others — CFA Institute position: AGAINST these structures.
Exam: Information asymmetry → most relevant between MANAGERS and SHAREHOLDERS (not suppliers/customers or employees/government).
LOS 22.b: Describe corporate governance and mechanisms to manage stakeholder relationships.
Shareholder Mechanisms
  • Annual General Meeting (AGM): Audited financials, performance discussion, shareholder Q&A. Must be communicated per corporate law.
  • Proxy voting: Absent shareholders assign voting rights to another person (director, advisor). Ordinary resolutions (auditor approval, director election) = simple majority.
  • Extraordinary General Meeting (EGM): Called for: bylaw amendments, mergers, special elections, liquidation.
  • Activist shareholders: Hold significant shares and pressure for value-increasing changes. Tactics: lawsuits, board representation, shareholder resolutions, public campaigns.
  • Proxy contest: Seeking other shareholders' proxies to vote for alternative proposals.
  • Tender offer: Offer to buy enough shares for control.
  • Hostile takeover: Not supported by management → threat incentivizes better governance; or triggers defenses (staggered board, poison pill = low-price share offerings to current shareholders).
Creditor Mechanisms
  • Bond indenture: Legal document specifying bondholder rights and company obligations.
  • Covenants: Required actions or restrictions. Collateral: Specific assets pledged against default.
  • Trustee: Financial institution monitoring covenant compliance.
  • Creditor/ad hoc committees: Formed to protect interests during financial distress.
Board Committees
  • Audit Committee: Financial reporting oversight, internal controls, external auditor recommendation.
  • Nominating/Governance Committee: Board elections, governance code, ethics, regulatory compliance.
  • Compensation/Remuneration Committee: Pay for directors and senior managers; must be independent directors only (in most countries).
  • Risk Committee: (Financial services) — risk policy and enterprise risk management oversight.
  • Investment Committee: (Insurance) — prudent investment and capital management policies.
Other Mechanisms
  • Employees: Labor laws, employment contracts, unions, ESOPs.
  • Customers/Suppliers: Contracts; increasingly social media.
  • Government: Regulations, regulatory agencies; some countries have governance codes (adopt or explain).
Board of Directors Duties
  • Select senior management; set compensation; evaluate performance.
  • Set strategic direction of the company.
  • Approve capital structure changes, significant acquisitions, large investments.
  • Review performance; implement corrective steps.
  • Plan management continuity and CEO succession.
  • Establish, monitor, and oversee internal controls and risk management.
  • Ensure quality of financial reporting and internal audit.
LOS 22.c: Describe potential risks of poor and benefits of effective corporate governance.
Risks of Poor Governance
  • Weak control systems → accounting fraud / poor recordkeeping.
  • Managers serve own interests → suboptimal risk-taking → reduced company value.
  • Improper incentive compensation → self-dealing, related-party transactions.
  • Legal and reputational risks from poor compliance.
  • Stakeholder lawsuits from violated rights.
  • Debt default and bankruptcy from poor creditor management.
Benefits of Effective Governance
  • Improved operational efficiency → aligned management and board incentives.
  • Avoidance of legal and regulatory risks.
  • Better operating results from formal conflict of interest policies.
  • Reduced risk of debt default or bankruptcy → lower cost of debt financing.
  • Better financial performance and greater company value.

Working Capital and Liquidity
LOS 23.a: Explain the cash conversion cycle and compare issuers' cash conversion cycles.
Cash Conversion Cycle (CCC)
CCC = DOH + DSO − DPO DOH = Days of Inventory on Hand = (Inventory / COGS) × 365 DSO = Days Sales Outstanding = (Accounts Receivable / Revenue) × 365 DPO = Days Payable Outstanding = (Accounts Payable / COGS) × 365 Total Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities Net Working Capital = Current Assets (excl. cash & mktable securities) − Current Liabilities (excl. short-term & current debt)

A lower CCC = better (cash generated more quickly; less capital tied up in working capital).

A higher CCC = slower cash conversion → potential cash flow problems → limits growth investment.

Ways to Decrease CCC (with Disadvantages)
  • ↓ Inventories → production bottlenecks, stockouts.
  • ↓ Receivables / tighten credit → lost sales.
  • ↑ Payables (delay payment) → but compare cost to bank borrowing rate.
Supplier Trade Credit — EAR Formula
Terms: a/b net c (e.g., 2/10 net 30) a = discount % if paid within b days c = total days until full payment due EAR of NOT taking discount (forgoing credit): EAR = (1 + a/(1−a))^(365/(c−b)) − 1 Example: 2/10 net 30: EAR = (1 + 0.02/0.98)^(365/20) − 1 = 44.6% → Very expensive! Borrow from bank if rate < 44.6% Cost interpretation: Forgoing discount = borrowing $[1−a] for (c−b) days at cost of $a.
CCC varies by industry. Airlines: low CCC (prepaid sales, no inventory). Pharmaceuticals: high CCC (large high-margin inventory). Always compare CCC within the same industry.
LOS 23.b: Explain liquidity and compare issuers' liquidity levels.
Liquidity Concepts
  • Asset liquidity: Nearness to cash. Ranking (most→least liquid): Cash → Marketable securities → Accounts receivable → Inventory.
  • Liability liquidity: Nearness to settlement.
  • Primary liquidity sources: Cash & marketable securities, bank borrowings, cash from operations.
  • Secondary liquidity sources: Suspending dividends, delaying capex, selling assets, issuing equity, restructuring/extending debt, bankruptcy protection filing.
    → Signals negative market signals; more costly than primary sources.
Drags and Pulls on Liquidity
  • Drag on liquidity: Cash INFLOWS lag → ↑DOH (excess/obsolete inventory) or ↑DSO (slow/uncollectible receivables).
  • Pull on liquidity: Cash OUTFLOWS accelerate → ↓DPO (suppliers reduce credit / demand faster payment).
Liquidity Ratios
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities (>1 = sufficient current assets to cover CL) Quick Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities + A/R) / Current Liabilities (Excludes inventory — less liquid) Cash Ratio = (Cash + Marketable Securities) / Current Liabilities (Most stringent; excludes inventory AND A/R)
Current ratio > Quick ratio > Cash ratio (in terms of inclusiveness, reverse in stringency). All three declining over time → liquidity is WORSENING. Cash ratio is most conservative/stringent.
Cost of Liquidity
Cost of liquidity = Discount to fair market value received when selling assets quickly (vs. normal time). Expressed as: (Fair MV − Proceeds) / Fair MV

LOS 24.b: Describe capital allocation process; calculate NPV, IRR, ROIC; contrast their use.
Capital Allocation Process — 4 Steps
  1. Idea generation — from senior management, divisions, employees, external sources. Most important step.
  2. Analyzing project proposals — cash flow forecasts for each project to determine expected profitability.
  3. Create firm-wide capital budget — prioritize by timing, available resources, and strategic plan. Individually attractive projects may not make strategic sense.
  4. Monitor and post-audit — compare actual to projected results; identify systematic forecasting errors; improve operations.
NPV Formula
NPV = CF₀ + CF₁/(1+k)¹ + CF₂/(1+k)² + ... + CFn/(1+k)ⁿ = Σ CFₜ/(1+k)ᵗ for t=0 to n Where: CF₀ = initial investment (negative) CFₜ = after-tax cash flow at time t k = required rate of return (cost of capital, risk-adjusted) Decision rule: Accept if NPV > 0 (increases shareholder wealth) Reject if NPV < 0 (decreases shareholder wealth) NPV = 0 → no effect on shareholder wealth Positive NPV → proportionate increase in stock price (theory)
IRR Formula & Decision
IRR: Discount rate where NPV = 0 0 = CF₀ + CF₁/(1+IRR)¹ + ... + CFn/(1+IRR)ⁿ OR: PV inflows = PV outflows Decision rule: Accept if IRR > required rate of return (hurdle rate) Reject if IRR < required rate of return Hurdle rate = minimum acceptable IRR (often = WACC adjusted for project risk) Calculator: Same CFs as NPV; press [IRR][CPT] Conventional cash flow: sign changes ONCE (+→- or -→+) Unconventional: multiple sign changes → possibly MULTIPLE IRRs

LOS 24.c: Describe principles of capital allocation and common pitfalls.
Capital Allocation Principles
  • After-tax cash flows only — not accounting income. Include tax savings from non-cash deductions (depreciation, amortization).
  • Incremental cash flows only — only cash flows that CHANGE if project undertaken.
  • Ignore sunk costs — already incurred; irrelevant to accept/reject decision.
  • Include spillover effects: Cannibalization = negative effect on other product lines (e.g., diet version reduces original sales). Positive externality = project boosts other product sales.
  • Timing matters — earlier cash flows worth more (TVM).
  • Opportunity cost of internal funds = cost of equity (could have been paid as dividends).
  • Match nominal/real: If using real cash flows → must use real discount rate.
Capital Allocation Pitfalls

Cognitive Errors (Calculation Errors):

  • Poor forecasting (overhead cost errors, ignoring competitor response).
  • Not accounting for cost of internal funds.
  • Incorrectly accounting for inflation.

Behavioral Biases (Errors of Judgment):

  • Pet projects: Senior management-backed projects get overly optimistic projections and less scrutiny.
  • Inertia: Capital budgets anchored to prior year; static/rising budgets + declining returns = warning sign.
  • EPS/ROE focus: Managers avoid long-term NPV+ projects if they reduce short-term EPS/ROE.
  • Failure to generate alternatives: Stopping at first "good" idea instead of finding "better" ideas.
Sunk costs = EXCLUDE from analysis. Cannibalization and positive externalities = INCLUDE (they are incremental). Recipe development = include (only incurred if project proceeds). Prior market survey = exclude (sunk).
LOS 24.d: Describe types of real options relevant to capital investments.
  • Real options: Future actions a firm CAN take (right, not obligation) given they invest in a project today. Value ≥ 0 (never negative — won't exercise if negative). Add to NPV: Project NPV with options > NPV without options.

Reading 25
Capital Structure
LOS 25.a: Calculate and interpret WACC.
WACC = [w_d × r_d × (1 − t)] + [w_e × r_e] Where: w_d = weight of debt (market value based) r_d = pretax cost of debt t = corporate tax rate w_e = weight of equity = 1 − w_d r_e = cost of equity Example: 50% debt, r_d=8%, 50% equity, r_e=11%, t=30% WACC = [0.5 × 0.08 × (1−0.30)] + [0.5 × 0.11] = 0.028 + 0.055 = 0.083 = 8.3% Note: Cost of debt adjusted to AFTER-TAX because interest expense is tax-deductible (tax shield reduces effective cost). Weights: Use TARGET weights (market values preferred for current opportunity cost; book values used by management).
  • Cost of debt < cost of equity because debt has priority of claims over equity → less risk.
  • Use market value weights for current opportunity cost; use book value weights often preferred by managers (less volatile; matches credit rating agency approach).
  • In absence of stated target weights, analyst can use: current capital structure, trend in capital structure, or industry average capital structure.
WACC formula: The (1−t) term only applies to DEBT, not equity. Interest is tax-deductible; dividends are not.
LOS 25.b: Explain factors affecting capital structure and WACC.
Internal Factors
  • Revenue growth & stability: Higher/more stable revenue → supports more debt.
  • Cash flow growth & predictability: Stable, growing cash flows → supports more debt.
  • Business risk: Higher operational/demand risk → LESS debt capacity.
  • Asset quality & liquidity: Tangible, liquid, fungible assets = better collateral → more debt available.
  • Cost & availability of debt: Lower rates → prefer more debt.
  • Existing debt level: Higher existing leverage → lower capacity for additional debt.
  • Corporate tax rate: Higher tax rate → greater tax benefit from debt (larger tax shield).
External Factors
  • Market and business cycle conditions.
  • Regulation and industry norms.
  • Macroeconomic (top-down) factors: Inflation, real GDP growth rate, monetary policy, exchange rates → affect benchmark rates and credit spreads.
  • In downturns: investors demand higher yield spreads to compensate for default risk (especially cyclical industries).
Life Cycle Stage and Debt Capacity
StageDebt LevelReason
Start-upAlmost none — equity onlyNegative/uncertain cash flows; low assets; high business risk; possible convertible debt or leasing
GrowthConservative debtRising revenues but still higher risk; secured debt (fixed assets / A/R as collateral)

More debt-supportive: noncyclical industries, low operating leverage (low fixed costs), subscription-based revenue models, tangible liquid assets, own productive assets (vs. franchise).

LOS 25.c: Explain the Modigliani-Miller propositions regarding capital structure.
MM Assumptions (for MM I and II without taxes)
  • Perfect capital markets (no transactions costs, no taxes, no bankruptcy costs)
  • Homogeneous expectations
  • Riskless borrowing and lending at risk-free rate
  • No agency costs
  • Investment decisions independent of financing decisions
MM Proposition I — No Taxes: Capital Structure Irrelevance
V_firm is UNAFFECTED by capital structure. "The pie analogy": Amount of pie (firm value) doesn't depend on how it's sliced (debt vs equity). Firm Value = PV(operating earnings/EBIT) Both all-equity and levered firm have claim to same EBIT → same total value.
MM Proposition II — No Taxes: Cost of Equity Rises
r_e = r₀ + (D/E)(r₀ − r_d) Where: r_e = cost of equity r₀ = cost of equity with NO debt (all-equity firm) r_d = cost of debt D/E = debt-to-equity ratio As D/E ↑ → r_e ↑ linearly But: WACC remains CONSTANT (savings from cheaper debt exactly offset by rising cost of equity).
MM With Taxes: 100% Debt Maximizes Value
V_levered = V_unlevered + (t × D) = value of unlevered firm + PV of tax shield Tax shield = t × D (tax rate × amount of debt) With taxes, MM II: r_e = r₀ + (D/E)(r₀ − r_d)(1 − T_c) As D/E ↑ → WACC DECLINES (tax shield benefit) → Value maximized at 100% DEBT (theoretical extreme) "Government gets a slice of the pie (taxes). Debt reduces government's slice → more for investors."
MM Propositions — Summary Table
FrameworkFirm ValueWACC
MM I (no taxes)Unchanged by capital structureUnchanged (constant)
MM II (no taxes)UnchangedConstant (r_e rises, offsets cheaper debt)
MM with taxesMAXIMIZED at 100% debtMINIMIZED at 100% debt
With taxes + financial distressMaximized at optimal D/E (less than 100%)Minimized at optimal D/E
In practice, firms are NOT 100% debt because bankruptcy costs increase at higher leverage levels.
LOS 25.d: Describe optimal and target capital structures.
Static Tradeoff Theory
V_levered = V_unlevered + (t × D) − PV(costs of financial distress) Optimal capital structure: D/E where Marginal benefit of tax shield = Marginal cost of financial distress → WACC minimized, Firm value maximized Costs of Financial Distress: 1. Direct costs: legal fees, admin fees (bankruptcy) 2. Indirect costs: foregone investments, loss of trust from customers/creditors/suppliers/employees 3. Agency costs of debt: manager-debtholder conflicts during distress Probability of financial distress ↑ with: → Higher financial leverage → Poor management quality → Weak corporate governance
Other Capital Structure Theories Pecking Order Theory
  • Based on information asymmetry.
  • Managers prefer financing that sends least negative signal to investors.
  • Hierarchy: Retained earnings (most preferred) → Debt → External equity (least preferred).
  • Issuing equity = negative signal (managers think stock is overvalued).
  • Issuing debt = positive signal (confident in ability to make payments).
  • Capital structure = byproduct of individual financing decisions.
Free Cash Flow Hypothesis
  • Debt FORCES managers to be disciplined (less free cash flow to waste).
  • Greater leverage → REDUCES agency costs of equity.
Net Agency Costs of Equity
  • Monitoring costs: Reporting to shareholders, board expenses. Reduced by strong governance.
  • Bonding costs: Assuring shareholders managers act in their interest (insurance, noncompete agreements).
  • Residual losses: Monitoring/bonding can't provide perfect guarantee.
Static tradeoff theory: optimal capital structure balances TAX SHIELD benefit vs. FINANCIAL DISTRESS cost. Pecking order: internal > debt > equity. Free cash flow: more debt = less agency cost.
Reading 26
Business Models
LOS 26.a: Describe key features of business models.
Business Model Framework: WHO, WHAT, HOW, WHERE, HOW MUCH
  • WHO (Customers): Identify target customers via segmenting; define customer acquisition cost and satisfaction monitoring. B2B (business-to-business) vs. B2C (business-to-consumer).
  • WHAT (Product/Service): How it meets customer needs; what differentiates it from competitors (low price, premium quality, innovative features).
  • HOW (Assets & Suppliers): Key assets (patent, software, skilled employees); key suppliers in value chain.
  • WHERE (Channel Strategy): Online, physical, direct mail, trade shows, sales reps; direct sales vs. intermediaries (wholesalers, retailers, agents, franchisees). Omnichannel strategy = both digital and physical channels.
  • HOW MUCH (Pricing Strategy): Why customers will pay that price given competition.
Value Proposition vs. Value Chain
  • Value proposition: How customers will value the firm's product/service vs. competitors.
  • Value chain: How the firm EXECUTES its value proposition — encompasses quality, functionality, service, sales process, pricing vs. competition.
  • Porter's Value Chain Activities (5): (1) Inbound logistics, (2) Operations, (3) Outbound logistics, (4) Marketing, (5) Sales and service.
Pricing Strategies
  • Commodity producers: Price takers (undifferentiated products — oil/gas, mortgages).
  • Pricing power: Few competitors or highly differentiated products (patented drugs).
  • Price discrimination: Different prices for different customers/groups.
  • Tiered pricing: Volume-based discounts.
  • Dynamic pricing: Time-of-day/week (peak vs. off-peak).
  • Value-based pricing: Price based on value delivered to customer.
  • Auction pricing: Market determines price (eBay).
  • Bundling: Combine complementary products (furnished apartment).
  • Razors-and-blades: Low-price equipment; high-margin consumables (printers + ink; e-reader + e-books).
  • Add-on pricing: High-margin options added AFTER purchase decision (car options).
  • Penetration pricing: Sell at low margin or loss to grow market share (Netflix).
  • Freemium: Basic functionality free; charge for premium features (video games).
  • Hidden revenue: "Free" content but revenue from ads; search engines sell user data.
  • Subscription model: Recurring payment for access (Microsoft Office).
  • Licensing/franchising: License production rights (biotech → pharma). Franchisee pays % of sales to franchisor for brand/marketing/territory rights.
LOS 26.b: Describe various types of business models.
Conventional Business Models
  • Industry-specific: natural resource producers, manufacturers, distributors, retailers, banks, brokers, service providers, software.
Other Business Models
  • Private label / contract manufacturers: Produce products for others to sell under their own brand (Costco's Kirkland brand).
  • Licensing agreements: Allow another company to use brand for a fee (Marvel-branded products).
  • Value-added resellers: Offer installation, service, support, customization for complex equipment.
  • SaaS (Software as a Service): Subscription access to software over internet.
Modern/Platform Business Models
  • Network effects: Value of network increases as user base grows (WhatsApp, eBay, Facebook). Supports initial penetration pricing. Capitalize on both SIZE and SCOPE.
  • Two-sided / multi-sided networks: Serve multiple user groups (Airbnb: hosts + guests). Sufficient supply AND demand needed for the network to work.
  • Crowdsourcing models: Benefit from user contributions — content (Wikipedia), traffic/events (Waze), product improvements (open-source software).
  • Innovations: New entrants with new technology challenge incumbents (discount brokers, ultra-low-cost airlines).
For exam: B2B vs B2C; distinguish pricing strategies clearly (freemium = basic free + paid upgrade; NOT subscription). Network effects = two-sided or multi-sided → both sides needed to succeed. Bundling requires complementary products.
